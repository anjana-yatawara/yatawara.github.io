# 12. Multicollinearity, variable selection, and validation

:::{div}

[Español](./es.md)

Measuring body fat well is a nuisance. The accurate way is to weigh a person underwater
and work out their density, which needs a tank, a technician, and a cooperative subject.
The cheap way is a tape measure. So a natural question follows: can a handful of tape
measurements (waist, chest, thigh, wrist, and the rest) predict the percent body fat that
the underwater weighing gives? A 1985 study recorded both for 252 men, and that dataset,
`fat.csv`, is the one this chapter works with from start to finish. (This is the larger
252-man study with thirteen measurements, not the three-measurement, twenty-man body-fat
table you met for extra sums of squares in @ch08-avplots; the two share a "thigh" column
but are different data.)

There is an obvious plan: regress body fat on all thirteen measurements and read off the
coefficients. When you do, the model explains about 75 percent of the variation, which
sounds like a success. Then you look at the coefficients and something is wrong. The
coefficient on weight is negative, as if heavier men had less fat once you hold the other
measures fixed. Several predictors that everyone knows matter come out statistically
insignificant. Drop one measurement and the others lurch. The problem is that the tape
measurements are all measuring roughly the same thing, body size, so they are tangled
together. @fig-ch12-corr-heatmap shows just how tangled.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch12_corr_heatmap.png
:name: fig-ch12-corr-heatmap
:alt: A 13 by 13 correlation heatmap of the body-fat predictors. Most off-diagonal cells among the girth measurements (weight, chest, abdomen, hip, thigh, knee, biceps) are deep red, with correlations from about 0.7 to 0.94, while age and height are pale, nearly uncorrelated with the rest.
The girth measurements are strongly correlated with one another (weight and hip correlate 0.94, chest and abdomen 0.92). Predictors this redundant carry almost the same information, which is what makes their individual coefficients hard to pin down.
```

This chapter is about living with too many predictors, most of them redundant. Three
questions organize it. First, how do we diagnose and describe the damage that correlated
predictors do, using the variance inflation factor? Second, when several models are on the
table, how do we choose, using criteria like Mallows $C_p$ and cross-validation that reward
prediction rather than mere fit? Third, what modern tools (the lasso and ridge regression)
sidestep the whole selection problem by shrinking coefficients instead of deleting them?
Along the way you will meet the most important warning in applied statistics: a model chosen
to fit your data will always look better on that data than it deserves.

:::{admonition} This lesson at a glance
:class: important
- **What we are doing:** Living with many, largely redundant predictors on the body-fat data: diagnosing multicollinearity with the variance inflation factor, choosing a model with $C_p$, AIC, BIC, and cross-validation, and shrinking with ridge and lasso.
- **Why we are doing it:** Regress body fat on all thirteen tape measurements and the coefficients go haywire (weight comes out negative) because they all measure body size; worse, any model chosen to fit its own data looks better on that data than it deserves.
- **Main objective:** Diagnose collinearity with the VIF, compare selection criteria, run $k$-fold cross-validation by hand, and describe ridge and lasso shrinkage.
- **What changed from the last chapters:** Chapters 8 through 11 gave you one multiple regression and the tools to interpret and diagnose it; from here you face many competing models and judge them by out-of-sample prediction rather than in-sample fit, iterating the FIT and CHECK stages of @ch02-workflow together.
:::

## 12.1 Multicollinearity and the variance inflation factor

### Intuition

Chapters 7 and 8 built a multiple regression and read each coefficient as a partial slope,
the effect of one predictor with the others held fixed (@ch08-avplots). That reading quietly
assumed the predictors carried separate information. The vignette showed the damage when they
do not; now we name the cause, **multicollinearity** (Definition 12.1).

:::{admonition} Definition 12.1: Multicollinearity
:class: note definition
**Multicollinearity** is correlation among the predictors of a regression. It inflates the
variance of the individual coefficients without biasing them, and does not harm predictions
made inside the range of the data.
:::

A little of it is harmless and normal. A lot is trouble, because the model cannot tell the
correlated predictors apart. Imagine two predictors that move almost in lockstep, like weight and hip
circumference. The data show what happens to body fat when both rise together, but they show
almost nothing about what happens when one rises and the other stays fixed, because that
combination barely occurs. So the model has no firm basis for splitting the shared effect
between them. It can give weight a big positive coefficient and hip a big negative one, or
the reverse, and fit the data about equally well either way. @fig-ch12-collinearity-wobble
draws the picture.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch12_collinearity_wobble.png
:name: fig-ch12-collinearity-wobble
:alt: Two scatterplots of predictor X2 against predictor X1. On the left the points fill a round cloud, showing the predictors are independent. On the right the points lie almost exactly along a rising diagonal line, showing the predictors are collinear with correlation 0.98, with a note that only the direction X1 plus X2 is well measured and the split between them is nearly free.
When predictors are independent (left) the data pin down each coefficient. When they are collinear (right) only their sum is well measured; the split into separate coefficients is nearly free to move, which is why collinear coefficients have huge standard errors.
```

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
Collinearity is a variance problem, not a bias problem. It leaves the coefficients correct on
average and predictions inside the data range intact; what it damages is the precision of the
individual coefficients, whose standard errors balloon, whose signs flip from sample to sample,
and whose $t$ tests go quiet. Trust a collinear model's predictions, distrust its coefficients.
:::

The **variance inflation factor** (Definition 12.2) measures exactly how much the variance is
inflated.

### The full model, and why its coefficients misbehave

Fit the full model first, so the symptoms are concrete. The response is `brozek`, percent
body fat by Brozek's formula, and the thirteen predictors are the tape and scale
measurements. Recall from @ch04-r-and-slope that for a single predictor, $R^2$ is just the
squared correlation; with many predictors $R^2$ keeps climbing as you add columns, whether or
not they help, so a high $R^2$ alone proves nothing.

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 12.2: Variance inflation factor
:class: note definition
For predictor $X_k$, the **variance inflation factor** is
$$
\mathrm{VIF}_k = \frac{1}{1 - R_k^2},
$$
where $R_k^2$ is the coefficient of determination from the auxiliary regression of $X_k$ on all
the other predictors. It is the factor by which collinearity multiplies the variance of $b_k$.
:::

:::{admonition} Definition 12.3: Auxiliary regression
:class: note definition
The **auxiliary regression** for predictor $X_k$ is the least-squares regression of $X_k$ on
all the other predictors. Its coefficient of determination $R_k^2$ measures how completely the
others reproduce $X_k$, and feeds the VIF through $\mathrm{VIF}_k = 1/(1 - R_k^2)$.
:::

In words: regress predictor $k$ on the remaining predictors, see
how well they predict it, and $\mathrm{VIF}_k$ is one over the leftover fraction. If $X_k$ is
unrelated to the others, $R_k^2 = 0$ and $\mathrm{VIF}_k = 1$. If the others explain 90
percent of it, $R_k^2 = 0.9$ and $\mathrm{VIF}_k = 10$. A common rule of thumb flags
$\mathrm{VIF}_k > 10$ (equivalently $R_k^2 > 0.9$) as serious, though the cutoff is a
convention, not a law.

The shape of that formula is worth a look, because it explains why a little collinearity is
harmless and a lot is a disaster. @fig-ch12-vif-curve plots the VIF against $R_k^2$. The curve
is nearly flat while a predictor is mostly its own, then bends sharply upward as the others
close in on reproducing it. Weight, which the other twelve measurements explain 97 percent of,
sits far up the steep part.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch12_vif_curve.png
:name: fig-ch12-vif-curve
:alt: A curve of the variance inflation factor against the auxiliary R-squared, rising slowly from 1 at R-squared 0 and then shooting upward as R-squared nears 1. A dashed horizontal line marks VIF equals 10 and a dotted vertical line marks R-squared equals 0.9, and the two meet on the curve. Five body-fat predictors are plotted as points: age at R-squared 0.56 and VIF 2.2 low on the flat part; chest, abdomen, and hip clustered near the warning line; and weight at R-squared 0.97 far up the steep part at VIF 33.5.
Because the VIF is one over the leftover fraction $1 - R_k^2$, it barely moves while a predictor keeps most of its own information, then explodes once the other predictors nearly reproduce it. That steepness is why weight, 97 percent explained by the rest, lands all the way up at VIF 33.5.
```

The name is literal. The VIF is the factor by which the variance of $b_k$ is multiplied,
compared with the variance it would have if $X_k$ were uncorrelated with the other
predictors:

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{b_k\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}\,(1 - R_k^2)} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}} \cdot \mathrm{VIF}_k,
\qquad S_{kk} = \sum_{i=1}^n (X_{ik} - \bar{X}_k)^2 .
$$

In words: the variance of a coefficient is the usual $\sigma^2 / S_{kk}$ (small when the
predictor is spread wide) times the inflation factor. Collinearity enters only through that
last factor.

### Derivation (the VIF from the auxiliary regression)

:::{admonition} Theorem 12.4: Variance of a least-squares coefficient
:class: important theorem
Under the general linear model, the coefficient $b_k$ of predictor $X_k$ has variance
$$
\operatorname{Var}\{b_k\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}\,(1 - R_k^2)} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}} \cdot \mathrm{VIF}_k,
\qquad S_{kk} = \sum_{i=1}^n (X_{ik} - \bar{X}_k)^2,
$$
where $R_k^2$ is the auxiliary $R^2$ of $X_k$ on the other predictors. Collinearity enters only
through the factor $\mathrm{VIF}_k = 1/(1 - R_k^2)$.
:::

**Proof.** Fix a predictor $X_k$. By the added-variable
construction of @ch08-avplots, the least-squares coefficient $b_k$ equals the slope from a
simple regression of $Y$ on the part of $X_k$ that the other predictors do not explain. Make
that part concrete: run the auxiliary regression of $X_k$ on all the other predictors, and
call its residuals $\tilde{X}_{ik}$. These residuals are $X_k$ with the other predictors'
information removed.

The added-variable result says $b_k = \sum_i \tilde{X}_{ik} Y_i / \sum_i \tilde{X}_{ik}^2$, a
simple-regression slope with the $\tilde{X}_{ik}$ as the predictor. From @ch02-gauss-markov-slr,
a simple-regression slope $\sum k_i Y_i$ with weights $k_i = \tilde{X}_{ik} / \sum_j
\tilde{X}_{jk}^2$ has variance $\sigma^2 \sum_i k_i^2 = \sigma^2 / \sum_i \tilde{X}_{ik}^2$.
So

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{b_k\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_{i} \tilde{X}_{ik}^2} .
$$

Now identify the denominator. The auxiliary regression has total sum of squares
$\sum_i (X_{ik} - \bar{X}_k)^2 = S_{kk}$ and error sum of squares $\sum_i \tilde{X}_{ik}^2$.
By the definition of $R^2$ applied to that auxiliary regression,

$$
R_k^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_i \tilde{X}_{ik}^2}{S_{kk}}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\sum_i \tilde{X}_{ik}^2 = S_{kk}\,(1 - R_k^2) .
$$

Substitute:

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{b_k\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}\,(1 - R_k^2)} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - R_k^2} .
$$

The first factor $\sigma^2 / S_{kk}$ is what the variance would be if $X_k$ were free of the
others ($R_k^2 = 0$). The second factor $1/(1 - R_k^2) = \mathrm{VIF}_k$ is the price of
collinearity. When the other predictors nearly reproduce $X_k$, $R_k^2 \to 1$ and the
variance explodes. $\blacksquare$

```python
def vif_by_hand(data, predictors):
    out = {}
    for v in predictors:
        others = [p for p in predictors if p != v]
        r2 = smf.ols(v + " ~ " + " + ".join(others), data=data).fit().rsquared
        out[v] = 1 / (1 - r2)
```

### Remedies

There are four honest responses to high VIFs. First, do nothing, if you only care about
prediction inside the range of the data: collinearity does not bias $\hat{Y}$ or widen its
interval much, so a predictive model can carry redundant predictors safely. Second, drop
predictors: if weight, hip, and abdomen say nearly the same thing, keep the one you can
measure best and discard the rest. Third, combine them into a single index (an average, or a
principal component) that captures the shared size signal. Fourth, shrink the coefficients
with ridge regression (Section 12.5), which tolerates collinearity by design. What you must
not do is read a collinear coefficient as a real causal effect and act on its sign.

:::{admonition} Durable skill: Distrust a coefficient before you interpret it
:class: tip
A coefficient is a claim about what happens when one predictor changes and the others hold
still. If the data almost never held the others still (because the predictors move together),
that claim rests on almost nothing, no matter how confidently the software prints it. Before
you tell anyone "each extra kilogram lowers body fat," check whether the data could even see
that comparison. The habit of asking "does my data contain the contrast this coefficient
describes?" will save you from confident nonsense in every regression you ever run, and in
every regression an AI assistant hands you.
:::

::::{admonition} Try it 12.1
:class: important
A colleague reports a VIF of $1.0$ for one predictor and $25$ for another, and concludes the
second predictor is "more important." Correct the reasoning, and say what a VIF of $25$
implies about the auxiliary $R_k^2$.

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
The VIF says nothing about importance; it measures redundancy. A VIF of $1.0$ means the
predictor is uncorrelated with the others, so its coefficient is estimated cleanly. A VIF of
$25$ means the other predictors explain $R_k^2 = 1 - 1/25 = 0.96$ of that predictor, so its
coefficient is poorly estimated (variance inflated 25-fold), the opposite of a reason to
trust it. A high-VIF predictor may be very important or completely useless; the VIF cannot
tell you which. It only warns that this particular coefficient is unstable.
:::
::::

## 12.2 Choosing among models: selection criteria

### Intuition

The full model has redundant predictors; a smaller model might predict just as well and be
easier to trust. But which smaller model? With thirteen predictors there are $2^{13} = 8192$
possible subsets. We need a score that ranks them. The obvious score, $R^2$, is useless for
this, because $R^2$ never decreases when you add a predictor: the biggest model always wins,
even if the added predictors are noise. Recall from @ch04-r-and-slope that $R^2$ is squared
correlation, a measure of fit, not of prediction. A good selection criterion must instead
reward fit while charging a penalty for each extra parameter, so that a predictor earns its
place only if it pays for itself.

Four criteria do this, each in its own currency. **Adjusted $R^2$** discounts $R^2$ by the
degrees of freedom spent. **Mallows $C_p$** estimates the total prediction error and compares
it to the number of parameters. **AIC** and **BIC** trade goodness of fit against a size
penalty from information theory. **PRESS** predicts each case from a model fit without it.
They usually agree on the broad message (drop the deadweight) while disagreeing on the exact
size, which is itself a lesson: there is no single correct model.

### Formula

Let a candidate model have $p$ parameters (predictors plus intercept), error sum of squares
$\mathrm{SSE}_p = \sum_i (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2$, and $\mathrm{SSTO} = \sum_i (Y_i - \bar{Y})^2$.
Let $\hat{\sigma}^2$ be the mean square error of the largest (full) model, our best estimate
of the noise variance $\sigma^2$. The four criteria are

$$
R^2_{a,p} = 1 - \frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p/(n - p)}{\mathrm{SSTO}/(n-1)}, \qquad
C_p = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p}{\hat{\sigma}^2} - (n - 2p),
$$

$$
\mathrm{AIC}_p = n \ln\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p}{n}\right) + 2p, \qquad
\mathrm{BIC}_p = n \ln\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p}{n}\right) + p \ln n .
$$

- $R^2_{a,p}$ (adjusted $R^2$): like $R^2$ but with $\mathrm{SSE}$ and $\mathrm{SSTO}$ divided
  by their degrees of freedom. Adding a useless predictor lowers it. Bigger is better.
- $C_p$ (Mallows): small is better; a model with no bias has $C_p \approx p$, so you look for
  models with low $C_p$ that also sit near the line $C_p = p$.
- $\mathrm{AIC}_p$, $\mathrm{BIC}_p$: fit term plus penalty; smaller is better. BIC's penalty
  $p \ln n$ is heavier than AIC's $2p$ whenever $n > 7$, so BIC prefers smaller models.

That last claim is easy to see in a picture. @fig-ch12-aic-bic-penalty draws the two size
penalties as straight lines. With $n = 252$ men, BIC charges about $\ln 252 \approx 5.5$ for
each extra parameter while AIC charges only $2$, so the BIC line climbs almost three times as
steeply. A predictor has to improve the fit more to pay off its heavier BIC toll, which is
exactly why BIC lands on smaller models.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch12_aic_bic_penalty.png
:name: fig-ch12-aic-bic-penalty
:alt: Two straight lines of size penalty against the number of parameters p from 1 to 14. The AIC line, penalty two times p, rises gently to 28 at p equals 14. The BIC line, penalty p times the natural log of 252, rises much more steeply to about 77, roughly 5.5 per parameter.

```

The fifth criterion, **PRESS**, gets its own subsection because it measures something
different: honest out-of-sample error.

$$
\mathrm{PRESS}_p = \sum_{i=1}^n \bigl(Y_i - \hat{Y}_{(i)}\bigr)^2,
$$

where $\hat{Y}_{(i)}$ is the prediction for case $i$ from the model refit with case $i$
removed. In words: hide each observation, predict it from the rest, and add up the squared
misses. Smaller is better, and unlike $\mathrm{SSE}$, PRESS cannot be driven down just by
adding predictors, because a junk predictor helps the fit but hurts the left-out prediction.

:::{admonition} A note on AIC and BIC constants
:class: note
Different software adds different constants to AIC and BIC. R's `AIC()` counts the variance
as an extra estimated parameter and includes the $2\pi$ term from the normal likelihood;
statsmodels uses a slightly different constant. The absolute numbers therefore differ between
R and Python, but the *differences* between models within one program, which are all that
matter for selection, agree. Compare AIC values only within a single language.
:::

### Derivation (Mallows $C_p$)

:::{admonition} Theorem 12.5: Mallows $C_p$
:class: important theorem
Let a candidate model with $p$ parameters have error sum of squares $\mathrm{SSE}_p$, and let
$\hat{\sigma}^2$ estimate $\sigma^2$ from the full model. Then
$$
C_p = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p}{\hat{\sigma}^2} - (n - 2p)
$$
estimates the standardized total mean squared error $\Gamma_p = \sigma^{-2}\sum_i
E\{(\hat{Y}_i - \mu_i)^2\}$ of the fitted values. A model with no bias has $C_p \approx p$; a
model that omits an important predictor has $C_p$ well above $p$.
:::

**Proof.** We want a criterion
that is small when a model's fitted values $\hat{Y}_i$ are close to the true means
$\mu_i = E\{Y_i\}$, across all $n$ cases. Define the target

$$
\Gamma_p = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n E\bigl\{(\hat{Y}_i - \mu_i)^2\bigr\},
$$

the total mean squared error of the fitted values, standardized by $\sigma^2$. Split each
term into variance and squared bias with the identity $E\{W^2\} = \operatorname{Var}\{W\} +
(E\{W\})^2$ applied to $W = \hat{Y}_i - \mu_i$:

$$
\sum_i E\{(\hat{Y}_i - \mu_i)^2\} = \underbrace{\sum_i \operatorname{Var}\{\hat{Y}_i\}}_{\text{variance}} + \underbrace{\sum_i (E\{\hat{Y}_i\} - \mu_i)^2}_{\text{squared bias, call it } B}.
$$

For the variance term, the fitted values are $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$ with the
candidate model's hat matrix $\mathbf{H}$, and $\sum_i \operatorname{Var}\{\hat{Y}_i\} =
\sigma^2\,\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p\sigma^2$, using $\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p$ from
@ch07-hat-matrix. So

$$
\sum_i E\{(\hat{Y}_i - \mu_i)^2\} = p\sigma^2 + B .
$$

Now connect $B$ to something we can compute, the expected error sum of squares. Write
$\mathbf{Y} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ and
$\mathrm{SSE}_p = \|(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}\|^2$. Then

$$
E\{\mathrm{SSE}_p\} = \|(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H})\boldsymbol{\mu}\|^2 + \sigma^2\,\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H}) = B + (n - p)\sigma^2,
$$

because $(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H})\boldsymbol{\mu}$ has $i$-th entry $\mu_i - E\{\hat{Y}_i\}$ so
its squared length is $B$, and $\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H}$ is idempotent with trace $n - p$ (again
@ch07-hat-matrix). Solve for the bias: $B = E\{\mathrm{SSE}_p\} - (n - p)\sigma^2$. Substitute
into $\Gamma_p$:

$$
\Gamma_p = \frac{p\sigma^2 + B}{\sigma^2} = \frac{p\sigma^2 + E\{\mathrm{SSE}_p\} - (n-p)\sigma^2}{\sigma^2} = \frac{E\{\mathrm{SSE}_p\}}{\sigma^2} - (n - 2p) .
$$

Replace the unknown $E\{\mathrm{SSE}_p\}$ by the observed $\mathrm{SSE}_p$ and the unknown
$\sigma^2$ by the full model's $\hat{\sigma}^2$, and you have Mallows' statistic:

$$
C_p = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p}{\hat{\sigma}^2} - (n - 2p) .
$$

Two consequences fall out. If the candidate model has no bias ($B = 0$, meaning it contains
all the predictors that matter), then $E\{\mathrm{SSE}_p\} = (n - p)\sigma^2$ and
$\Gamma_p = (n - p) - (n - 2p) = p$. So unbiased models have $C_p \approx p$: you look for
models where $C_p$ is both small and close to $p$. A model that omits an important predictor
has $B > 0$, which pushes $C_p$ well above $p$. $\blacksquare$

### Deriving and checking PRESS

PRESS looks expensive: refitting the model $n$ times, once per deleted case, is $252$ fits per
model here. A beautiful identity makes it free. You can get every deleted prediction from a
single fit of the full data, using the ordinary residual and the leverage value.

:::{admonition} Theorem 12.6: PRESS deleted-residual shortcut
:class: important theorem
For a least-squares fit with ordinary residual $e_i = Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$ and leverage value
$h_{ii}$, the leave-one-out deleted residual is
$$
Y_i - \hat{Y}_{(i)} = \frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}},
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
\mathrm{PRESS} = \sum_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}}\right)^2 .
$$
Every leave-one-out prediction error then follows from a single fit of the full data.
:::

**Proof.** Write the design matrix $\mathbf{X}$
with $i$-th row $\mathbf{x}_i'$, the full-data estimate $\mathbf{b} =
(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$, the fitted value $\hat{Y}_i =
\mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{b}$, the residual $e_i = Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$, and the leverage value
$h_{ii} = \mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i$ from @ch07-hat-matrix (the
diagonal of the hat matrix). Let $\mathbf{b}_{(i)}$ be the estimate from the data with case $i$ removed, and
$\hat{Y}_{(i)} = \mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{b}_{(i)}$ the deleted prediction. We show

$$
Y_i - \hat{Y}_{(i)} = \frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}} .
$$

Deleting case $i$ changes the cross-product matrix and vector to
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X} - \mathbf{x}_i\mathbf{x}_i'$ and $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{x}_i Y_i$.
The Sherman-Morrison inverse-update identity (derived and checked in @ch09-cooks-distance)
gives

$$
(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X} - \mathbf{x}_i\mathbf{x}_i')^{-1}
= (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} + \frac{(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i\mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}}{1 - h_{ii}} .
$$

Write $\mathbf{u} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i$, so that $\mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{u} = h_{ii}$
and $\mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{b} = \hat{Y}_i$.
Multiply the updated inverse by $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{x}_i Y_i$:

$$
\mathbf{b}_{(i)} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{u}Y_i + \frac{\mathbf{u}\,(\hat{Y}_i - h_{ii} Y_i)}{1 - h_{ii}}
= \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{u}\,\frac{-Y_i(1 - h_{ii}) + \hat{Y}_i - h_{ii}Y_i}{1 - h_{ii}}
= \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{u}\,\frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}} ,
$$

where the numerator collapsed to $\hat{Y}_i - Y_i = -e_i$. Now form the deleted prediction:

$$
Y_i - \hat{Y}_{(i)} = Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{b}_{(i)}
= (Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{b}) + \mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{u}\,\frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}}
= e_i + \frac{h_{ii}\,e_i}{1 - h_{ii}} = \frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}} .
$$

So each deleted residual is just the ordinary residual divided by $1 - h_{ii}$, and

$$
\mathrm{PRESS} = \sum_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}}\right)^2 .
$$

High-leverage points (large $h_{ii}$) get their residuals magnified, which is right: those are
the cases the model props itself up on, and it predicts them worst when they are removed.
$\blacksquare$

predicting held-out cases (PRESS). When someone shows you a model with a dazzling $R^2$, ask

curve-fitting, in statistics and in machine learning alike.
:::

## 12.3 The dangers of automatic selection

### Intuition

It is tempting to automate the search: let the computer add and drop predictors by their
$p$-values until nothing improves. This is **stepwise selection** (Definition 12.7), and it is
available in one line in every statistics package. It is also the source of more bad science
than almost any other routine procedure, and you should understand exactly why before you ever
use it.

:::{admonition} Definition 12.7: Stepwise selection
:class: note definition
**Stepwise selection** is an automatic search (forward, backward, or both directions) that
repeatedly adds or drops predictors by their $p$-values until no single change improves the
model. It is prone to overfitting and produces invalid post-selection inference.
:::

Two things go wrong. The first is **overfitting** (Definition 12.8): a procedure that chases
the best fit on your sample will seize on accidents of that sample, patterns that are noise and
will not repeat. The second, subtler and worse, is **post-selection inference** (Definition
12.9): once you have chosen a model because it fit well, the $p$-values, confidence intervals,
and $F$ tests that software prints for that model are wrong. They were derived assuming the
model was fixed in advance, before you looked at the data. Choosing the model by looking at the
data breaks that assumption, and the reported significance becomes fiction.

:::{admonition} Definition 12.8: Overfitting
:class: note definition
**Overfitting** is fitting the noise in the training sample: the model chases accidents of the
data at hand, so it fits that sample well but predicts new data poorly.
:::

:::{admonition} Definition 12.9: Post-selection inference
:class: note definition
**Post-selection inference** is inference ($p$-values, confidence intervals, $F$ tests)
reported for a model chosen from the same data. It is invalid because the standard
distributions assume the model was fixed before the data were seen, which selection violates.
:::

### A demonstration with pure noise

The cleanest way to see the disaster is to run selection on data with no signal at all. Take a
response and forty predictors that are all independent random noise, unrelated to the response
and to each other. Honest testing should almost never find anything. Selection finds plenty.

```r
set.seed(4210)
```

We picked the five predictors with the smallest individual $p$-values out of forty pure-noise
columns, fit a model on just those five, and got an overall $F$-test $p$-value of $0.017$. By
the usual rule, that model is "significant at the 0.05 level," and its $R^2 = 0.135$ looks
respectable. Every bit of it is an illusion: there is no relationship in the data at all. The
selection step manufactured the significance by cherry-picking, and the $F$ test, blind to
the cherry-picking, reported it as real.

This is not a one-off fluke of the seed. @fig-ch12-postselection repeats the whole experiment
2000 times. Under honest testing the overall $F$ $p$-value should be uniform on $[0, 1]$, so
only 5 percent should fall below $0.05$. After selection, 94 percent do.

### What to do instead

None of this means variable selection is forbidden. It means three things. First, prefer the
criterion-based methods of Section 12.2 (compare whole models by $C_p$, BIC, or
cross-validated error) over $p$-value-driven stepwise search, and report that you searched.
Second, never quote the $p$-values from a selected model as if the model had been fixed in
advance; recall from @ch08-general-linear-test that the $F$ test's distribution assumes the
comparison was chosen before seeing the data. Third, and most reliably, judge the final model
on data it was not chosen with. That is validation, the subject of the next section, and it is
the only honest referee for a model you built by searching.

::::{admonition} Try it 12.3
:class: important
A classmate runs backward stepwise selection, arrives at a five-predictor model, and reports
that "all five predictors are significant at $p < 0.01$, so the model is excellent." What is
wrong with this claim, and what evidence would actually support the model?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
The $p$-values are contaminated by the selection: the predictors were kept *because* they had
small $p$-values, so of course the survivors look significant. Those $p$-values no longer have
their advertised meaning, and "all five significant" is nearly guaranteed by the procedure,
not by a real effect (the noise demonstration got a significant model from nothing at all).
Honest support would come from out-of-sample evidence: a low cross-validated error, or good
prediction on a held-out test set that played no part in choosing the model.
:::
::::

(ch12-cross-validation)=
## 12.4 Validation: train, test, and cross-validate

### Intuition

The residuals from a fitted model tell you how well it fits the data it was built on, which is
always flattering. To know how well a model predicts, you must test it on data it has never
seen. The idea echoes the out-of-sample thinking behind the bootstrap in @ch05-bootstrap and
the prediction intervals of @ch03-prediction-interval: honest error estimates come from
treating some data as genuinely new.

The simplest version splits the data once: a **training set** (Definition 12.10) to fit the
model, and a **test set**, held back, to measure its error. The split must be made before any
modeling, and the test set must never influence a single choice. A more efficient version,
**$k$-fold cross-validation** (Definition 12.11), splits the data into $k$ equal folds, then
rotates: each fold takes a turn as the test set while the other $k - 1$ folds train the model.
Averaging the $k$ test errors uses every case for testing exactly once, so it wastes no data.
@fig-ch12-kfold-diagram shows the rotation.

:::{admonition} Definition 12.10: Training and test sets
:class: note definition
A **training set** is the part of the data used to fit a model; a **test set** is a held-back
part used only to measure the fitted model's prediction error. The split is made before any
modeling, and the test set influences no modeling choice.
:::

:::{admonition} Definition 12.11: $k$-fold cross-validation
:class: note definition
**$k$-fold cross-validation** splits the data into $k$ equal folds and rotates, letting each
fold serve once as the test set while the other $k-1$ folds train the model. Averaging the $k$
test errors tests every case exactly once, wasting no data.
:::

```{figure} figures/fig_ch12_kfold_diagram.png
:name: fig-ch12-kfold-diagram
:alt: A schematic grid for five-fold cross-validation. Five rows, one per round, each split into five colored blocks. In round one the first block is orange (test) and the rest blue (train); in round two the second block is the test fold, and so on down the diagonal, so each of the five folds is the test set exactly once.
Five-fold cross-validation. The data are split into five folds; each round holds out one fold for testing (orange) and trains on the other four (blue). Every case is tested exactly once, and the five test errors are averaged.
```

We measure error with the **root mean squared error** (Definition 12.12), the typical size of
a prediction miss, in the units of the response (percentage points of body fat). A smaller one
means the model is closer, on average, to the truth for a new man.

:::{admonition} Definition 12.12: Root mean squared error
:class: note definition
The **root mean squared error** over $m$ held-out cases is
$$
\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2},
$$
the typical size of a prediction miss, expressed in the units of the response.
:::

### $k$-fold cross-validation, written by hand

A single split is noisy: a lucky or unlucky test set can mislead. Cross-validation averages
over $k$ splits. It is worth writing the loop yourself once, so the mechanism holds no
mystery. Assign each case a fold number, then loop: train on the other folds, predict the
held-out fold, collect the squared errors.

:::{admonition} Example 12.6: Ten-fold cross-validation by hand
:class: note
**Question.** Compare the full and candidate models by 10-fold cross-validated RMSE on the
whole dataset.

**Intuition.** Rotate through ten folds, predicting each once. The averaged error is a stable
estimate of prediction accuracy, and it should again favor the smaller model.

**Formula.** $\mathrm{CV\ RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathrm{MSE}_k}$, where
$\mathrm{MSE}_k$ is the mean squared error on fold $k$.

**Computation.**

```r
set.seed(4210)
K <- 10
folds <- sample(rep(1:K, length.out = n))
cv_rmse <- function(form) {
  sq_err <- numeric(K)
  for (k in 1:K) {
    fit_k <- lm(form, data = fat[folds != k, ])
    pred_k <- predict(fit_k, newdata = fat[folds == k, ])
    sq_err[k] <- mean((fat$brozek[folds == k] - pred_k)^2)
  }
  sqrt(mean(sq_err))
}
c(full_CV = cv_rmse(formula(full)), cand_CV = cv_rmse(candform))
```

:class: tip
The single most reliable guardrail against fooling yourself is to set aside data you will not
touch until the very end, and to judge the final model only on that. It costs you nothing but
discipline, and it catches overfitting, data leakage, and post-selection optimism all at once.
Every serious predictive project, from a course paper to a production machine-learning system,
lives or dies on whether its held-out evaluation was truly held out. Build the habit now:
decide your test set first, and do not peek.
:::

::::{admonition} Try it 12.4

Explain why training RMSE always decreases (or stays equal) as you add predictors, but
cross-validated RMSE can increase. Which one estimates how the model will do on a new man, and
why?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
Adding a predictor gives least squares more freedom, so it can only reduce (never increase)
the sum of squared residuals on the training data; training RMSE therefore falls monotonically.
But a predictor that only fits training-set noise will make predictions on held-out data
worse, so cross-validated RMSE can rise once you pass the useful predictors. Cross-validated
RMSE estimates performance on a new man, because it always scores predictions on cases that
did not train the model. Training RMSE cannot, because the model has already seen those cases.
:::
::::

(ch12-shrinkage)=
## 12.5 A look at shrinkage: ridge and lasso

### Intuition

Selection is a blunt instrument: a predictor is either in (full coefficient) or out (zero).
**Shrinkage** (Definition 12.13) offers a gentler alternative. Instead of deleting predictors,
it keeps them all but pulls their coefficients toward zero, trading a little bias for a large
drop in variance. When predictors are collinear, ordinary least squares can produce enormous
coefficients that cancel each other (the negative weight from Example 12.1). Shrinkage refuses
to let coefficients grow that large, which stabilizes them.

:::{admonition} Definition 12.13: Shrinkage
:class: note definition
**Shrinkage** pulls a model's coefficients toward zero by adding a penalty on their size,
trading a little bias for a large drop in variance. It keeps every predictor rather than
deleting any.
:::

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
The bias-variance trade-off is the single idea under every method in this chapter. A flexible
model (many predictors, no shrinkage) has low bias but high variance; a rigid one (few
predictors, heavy shrinkage) has high bias but low variance. Prediction error is their sum plus
irreducible noise, so the best predictor lives in between, as @fig-ch12-bias-variance shows.
Selection and shrinkage are both ways of aiming for that sweet spot.
:::

```{figure} figures/fig_ch12_bias_variance.png
:name: fig-ch12-bias-variance
:alt: A conceptual plot of error against model complexity. Squared bias (green, dashed) falls as complexity rises; variance (orange, dotted) rises; irreducible noise is a flat line. Their sum, the expected prediction error (blue), is a U-shaped curve with a marked sweet spot in the middle.
The bias-variance trade-off. As a model grows more flexible, bias falls but variance rises, and expected prediction error is their sum: a U-shape with a sweet spot. Selection and shrinkage are both ways of aiming for that sweet spot instead of the low-bias, high-variance extreme.
```

### Formula

Ordinary least squares minimizes the residual sum of squares. **Ridge** (Definition 12.14) and
**lasso** (Definition 12.15) add a penalty on the size of the coefficients.

:::{admonition} Definition 12.14: Ridge regression
:class: note definition
**Ridge regression** minimizes least squares plus an $L_2$ penalty on the slopes:
$$
\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\text{ridge}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol{\beta})^2 + \lambda \sum_{k=1}^{q} \beta_k^2 .
$$
It shrinks every coefficient toward zero but rarely to exactly zero.
:::

:::{admonition} Definition 12.15: Lasso
:class: note definition
The **lasso** minimizes least squares plus an $L_1$ penalty on the slopes:
$$
\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\text{lasso}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol{\beta})^2 + \lambda \sum_{k=1}^{q} |\beta_k| .
$$
Because its penalty has corners on the axes, it can set coefficients exactly to zero, doing
variable selection and shrinkage at once.
:::

- The first term is the usual least-squares loss; it wants a good fit.
- The second term is the penalty; it wants small coefficients. The sum runs over the $q$
  slope coefficients (not the intercept), and predictors are standardized first so the penalty
  treats them fairly.
- $\lambda \ge 0$ is the tuning parameter that sets the strength of the penalty. At
  $\lambda = 0$ both reduce to least squares; as $\lambda \to \infty$ all coefficients are
  crushed to zero. We choose $\lambda$ by cross-validation.

Ridge uses the squared penalty ($\sum \beta_k^2$, the $L_2$ norm); lasso uses the
absolute-value penalty ($\sum |\beta_k|$, the $L_1$ norm). The difference sounds minor and is
not. Ridge shrinks every coefficient toward zero but rarely to exactly zero. Lasso can set
coefficients exactly to zero, so it does variable selection and shrinkage at once. The reason
is geometric, shown in @fig-ch12-shrinkage-geometry: the lasso's constraint region is a
diamond with corners on the axes, and the best-fitting contour tends to touch a corner, where
a coefficient is zero. Ridge's constraint region is a smooth disk with no corners.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch12_shrinkage_geometry.png
:name: fig-ch12-shrinkage-geometry
:alt: Two panels showing elliptical least-squares loss contours around the OLS estimate. Left, the lasso constraint is a diamond (L1) and the solution star sits at the top corner on the vertical axis, where the first coefficient is zero. Right, the ridge constraint is a circle (L2) and the solution star sits off the axes, so both coefficients are nonzero but shrunk.
Why lasso zeros coefficients and ridge does not. The solution is where a growing loss contour first meets the constraint region. The lasso diamond has corners on the axes, so contours often meet it at a corner (a coefficient set to zero); the smooth ridge disk has no corners, so it only shrinks.
```

### The plain summary of shrinkage

Ridge and lasso answer the collinearity and selection problems in one move. Ridge keeps every
predictor but shrinks the coefficients so no two collinear predictors can blow up against each
other; it is the natural tool when you believe many predictors each contribute a little. Lasso
shrinks and selects at once, zeroing the coefficients it does not need, so it is the natural
tool when you believe only a few predictors matter and you want the model to find them. Both
replace the discrete, unstable question "which predictors are in?" with a smooth, tunable dial
$\lambda$, set by cross-validation. That is why shrinkage, not stepwise selection, is the
modern default when prediction is the goal.

::::{admonition} Try it 12.5
:class: important
As $\lambda$ increases from $0$ toward infinity in ridge regression, describe what happens to
the coefficients, to the model's variance, and to its bias. What does $\lambda = 0$ give, and
what does a very large $\lambda$ give?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
At $\lambda = 0$ the penalty vanishes and ridge is ordinary least squares: lowest bias,
highest variance. As $\lambda$ grows, the coefficients shrink toward zero, the model's
variance falls (the estimates stabilize, less sensitive to the sample), and its bias rises
(the shrunk coefficients are systematically too small in magnitude). As $\lambda \to \infty$
all slope coefficients go to zero and the model predicts the mean response for everyone:
zero variance, maximum bias. Cross-validation chooses the $\lambda$ that balances the two for
the smallest prediction error.
:::
::::

## 12.7 Frequently asked questions

**Q1. Does multicollinearity bias my coefficients?** No. Least squares stays unbiased under
collinearity: on average the coefficients are still correct. What collinearity does is inflate
their variance, so any single sample's estimates can be wildly off, with flipped signs and
huge standard errors. The average is fine; the individual estimate is unreliable.

**Q2. If collinearity does not hurt prediction, why care about it?** It depends on your goal.
If you only want to predict body fat for men like those in the data, a collinear model predicts
fine and you can ignore the VIFs. If you want to interpret coefficients (to say which
measurement matters and by how much), collinearity makes those coefficients meaningless, and
you must fix it by dropping, combining, or shrinking predictors.

**Q3. Which selection criterion should I use?** For interpretation, BIC and its heavier
penalty give you a lean, defensible model. For prediction, cross-validated error is the most
direct answer, and Mallows $C_p$ is a fast, well-motivated stand-in. Do not agonize over small
differences: report that the criteria pointed to a range of models (here, four to eight
predictors) and pick within that range for a reason you can state.

**Q4. Why is the $C_p$ of the full model always equal to the number of its parameters?** By
construction. $C_p = \mathrm{SSE}_p/\hat{\sigma}^2 - (n - 2p)$, and for the full model
$\hat{\sigma}^2 = \mathrm{SSE}_{\text{full}}/(n - p_{\text{full}})$, so
$\mathrm{SSE}_{\text{full}}/\hat{\sigma}^2 = n - p_{\text{full}}$ and $C_p = (n - p_{\text{full}})
- (n - 2p_{\text{full}}) = p_{\text{full}}$. The full model is the reference point, so its
$C_p$ lands exactly on the $C_p = p$ line.

**Q5. Is a lower PRESS always better?** As a comparison of models on the same data, yes: lower
PRESS means better leave-one-out prediction. But PRESS is still an in-sample estimate in the
sense that it uses the same 252 men to build and to test (just never the same man for both at
once), so it can be optimistic if the whole dataset is unusual. A truly held-out test set, or
$k$-fold cross-validation repeated with different seeds, is a stronger check.

**Q6. Can I just trust the model my software's `step()` function returns?** No. Stepwise
selection overfits and its reported $p$-values are invalid (Section 12.3). If you use it,
treat its output as a candidate to be validated on held-out data, never as a finished model,
and never quote its coefficients' significance as if the model had been chosen in advance.

**Q7. Lasso and stepwise both drop predictors. What is the difference?** Stepwise makes hard,
unstable in-or-out decisions by $p$-value and reports dishonest inference. Lasso makes the
decision continuously through a single penalty $\lambda$ chosen by cross-validation, shrinking
coefficients smoothly and turning them off only as the penalty grows. Lasso is more stable, has
a coherent bias-variance justification, and is built for prediction. When in doubt, prefer it.

**Q8. Why standardize predictors before ridge or lasso?** The penalty $\sum \beta_k^2$ or
$\sum |\beta_k|$ adds up coefficients measured in each predictor's own units. Without
standardizing, a predictor measured in small units (and so carrying a large coefficient) would
be penalized more than an identical predictor in large units. Standardizing puts every
predictor on the same scale so the penalty is fair. Both `glmnet` and the scaled scikit-learn
fit here do this.

infla la varianza del coeficiente por ese factor. Para el peso, $\mathrm{VIF} = 33.5$.

Para elegir entre modelos usamos criterios que premian la predicción, no el ajuste: $R^2$
ajustado, el **$C_p$ de Mallows (Mallows' Cp)** (que derivamos, con $C_p \approx p$ para un
modelo sin sesgo), AIC, BIC y **PRESS**, cuyo atajo del residuo eliminado,
$\mathrm{PRESS} = \sum (e_i/(1 - h_{ii}))^2$, demostramos. El modelo de ocho predictores
elegido tiene $C_p = 6.19$ y menor PRESS que el modelo completo.

Advertimos sobre los peligros de la **selección paso a paso (stepwise selection)**: sobreajusta,
y los valores $p$ posteriores a la selección son falsos (una demostración con puro ruido produce
modelos "significativos" el 94 por ciento de las veces). La solución honesta es la **validación
(validation)**: división en entrenamiento y prueba, y **validación cruzada de $k$ particiones
(k-fold cross-validation)**, que escribimos a mano. El modelo pequeño predice mejor fuera de la
muestra. Finalmente, presentamos la **contracción (shrinkage)**: ridge y lasso encogen los
coeficientes hacia cero, equilibrando sesgo y varianza; el lasso además selecciona variables.
Es la respuesta moderna cuando el objetivo es predecir.

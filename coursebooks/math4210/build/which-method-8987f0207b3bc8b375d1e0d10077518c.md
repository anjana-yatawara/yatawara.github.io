# Appendix F. Which method when

This appendix is a map. You bring a dataset and a question; it points you to the
method this book teaches for that situation, and to the section where the method
lives. It does not re-teach anything. Every arrow ends at an `@anchor` you can
follow back into the chapter that explains the details, states the assumptions,
and shows the R and Python code.

Read it as a companion to the workflow spine of @ch02-workflow: **ASK** (what is
the question and what are the data), **EXPLORE** (plot and summarize), **FIT**
(estimate a model), **CHECK** (diagnostics and assumptions), **USE** (interpret,
predict, decide, communicate). This guide mostly helps at ASK and FIT, points you
to the right CHECK tools, and tells you what to do when a check fails.

Work through it in three questions, in order:

1. What kind of thing is your response variable?
2. What are you actually trying to find out?
3. Which assumptions hold, and what do you do when one breaks?

## F.1 Start with the response: the master fork

The first fork is the type of the response variable $Y$, the thing you want to
explain or predict. Almost everything else follows from it. Find your row.

| Your response $Y$ is... | Example | Method family | Where |
|---|---|---|---|
| A continuous number | work hours, savings rate, body fat percent | Linear regression | @ch02-slr-model, @ch07-ls-matrix |
| Binary (yes/no, 0/1) | O-ring failure, disease present | Logistic regression | @ch13-logistic-model |
| A count (0, 1, 2, ...) | species on an island, absences | Poisson regression / GLM | @ch14-poisson-model |
| Continuous but time-ordered | monthly passengers, quarterly sales | Regression with autocorrelation and trend | @ch15-forecasting |

In words: continuous responses are the home ground of ordinary least squares
(most of this book); binary and count responses need the generalized linear model
of @ch14-glm, which contains linear and logistic and Poisson regression as three
members of one family; time order is not a response type so much as a warning that
the independence assumption is in danger, handled in Chapter 15.

A common wrong turn: fitting ordinary linear regression to a 0/1 response because
the software runs without complaint. @ch13-logistic-model shows why that gives
fitted "probabilities" below 0 and above 1, and what to do instead.

## F.2 Then ask what you want to find out

Once the response type has chosen a method family, your question chooses what you
do inside it. The table reads within the continuous-response world first, since
that is where the book spends most of its time; the last rows cross into the other
families.

| Your question | What you use | Where |
|---|---|---|
| How are two continuous variables related? Fit a line. | Simple linear regression | @ch02-least-squares |
| How strong and trustworthy is that slope? | $t$ test and confidence interval for $b_1$ | @ch03-slope-inference |
| How strong is the association, direction-free? | Pearson correlation $r$ | @ch04-correlation |
| How much of the spread does the line explain? | ANOVA table and $R^2$ | @ch03-anova-table, @ch03-r2 |
| Predict the mean response vs. one new case? | Confidence interval vs. prediction interval | @ch03-prediction-interval |
| Does a whole set of predictors matter together? | Multiple regression and the general linear test | @ch08-general-linear-test |
| What does one coefficient mean with others present? | "Holding the others fixed" reading | @ch08-holding-fixed |
| A group or category predictor? | Dummy coding | @ch11-dummy-coding |
| Do slopes differ across groups? | Interaction terms | @ch11-interactions |
| A curved relationship? | Polynomial terms | @ch11-polynomial |
| Which predictors should I keep? | Selection criteria and cross-validation | @ch12-criteria, @ch12-cross-validation |
| Predictors that overlap heavily? | VIF, then remedies or shrinkage | @ch12-vif, @ch12-shrinkage |
| Is my classical $p$-value believable in small samples? | Permutation test, bootstrap | @ch05-permutation-slope, @ch05-bootstrap |
| A binary outcome, and odds interpretation? | Logistic regression, odds ratios | @ch13-odds-ratio |
| A count or a rate? | Poisson regression, offsets, rate ratios | @ch14-poisson-model, @ch14-offset |
| Direct vs. indirect effects along a chain? | Path analysis | @ch16-path-diagrams |

Two questions this book is careful about, because software will answer them even
when it should not: "keep or drop these predictors" (@ch12-selection warns that
choosing predictors and then testing them on the same data inflates your
confidence), and "does X cause Y" (@ch16-limits and the correlation-is-not-
causation framing of @ch04-traps set the limits of what any regression can claim
from observational data).

## F.3 Continuous response, in detail

This is the largest branch, so it gets its own walk-through. Follow the questions
top to bottom.

**One predictor or many?** With a single continuous predictor, fit the simple
linear model $Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \varepsilon_i$ (@ch02-slr-model), do
inference on the slope with @ch03-slope-inference, and if you only want a
direction-free measure of association use correlation (@ch04-r-and-slope shows
that $r$ and the slope carry the same sign and are two views of one fit). With two
or more predictors, move to the matrix-form model $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\beta +
\varepsilon$ of @ch07-ls-matrix and interpret coefficients with @ch08-interpret.

**Are some predictors categorical?** A group label (treatment vs. control, three
regions) enters through dummy coding (@ch11-dummy-coding). If you suspect the
effect of one predictor changes across groups, add an interaction
(@ch11-interactions). If the relationship bends, use polynomial terms and center
first (@ch11-polynomial).

**Do you have many predictors, possibly overlapping?** Check for
multicollinearity with VIF (@ch12-vif); it does not bias the fit, but it inflates
standard errors and makes single coefficients unstable. To choose a subset, use
adjusted $R^2$, Mallows' $C_p$, AIC, BIC, or PRESS (@ch12-criteria), and judge the
result honestly with a train/test split or $k$-fold cross-validation
(@ch12-cross-validation). When predictors are badly tangled, ridge and lasso
shrinkage (@ch12-shrinkage) trade a little bias for much steadier estimates.

**Do you need to predict, and how sure must you be?** For the average response at
a given $X$, use a confidence interval for the mean; for a single new observation,
use the wider prediction interval (@ch03-prediction-interval). The gap between the
two is one of the most-missed distinctions in the course.

## F.4 When an assumption breaks: detect, then remedy

Ordinary least squares earns its good properties (unbiased, minimum variance among
linear estimators) from the Gauss-Markov conditions of @ch07-gauss-markov: the
mean is linear in the predictors, the errors have constant variance, and they are
uncorrelated. Normality is added only for exact $t$ and $F$ inference. Chapter 9
is the toolbox for finding out which of these fails; Chapter 10 is the toolbox for
fixing it. This table pairs the two.

| Assumption in doubt | How you detect it | What you do about it |
|---|---|---|
| Linearity (wrong mean shape) | Residuals-vs-fitted plot shows a curve; @ch08-avplots | Transform $X$, add polynomial terms (@ch11-polynomial), or transform $Y$ (@ch10-box-cox) |
| Constant variance (homoscedasticity) | Fanning residual plot; Breusch-Pagan or Brown-Forsythe test in Chapter 9 | Transform $Y$ (@ch10-log-interpretation, @ch10-box-cox) or fit weighted least squares (@ch10-wls) |
| Normal errors | QQ plot bends at the tails; Shapiro-Wilk | For inference only: transform $Y$, or switch to permutation and bootstrap (@ch05-permutation-slope, @ch05-bootstrap) |
| Independent errors | Time order in the data; Durbin-Watson (@ch15-durbin-watson) | Model the correlation: Cochrane-Orcutt, trend and seasonal terms (@ch15-autocorrelation, @ch15-trend-season) |
| No wild outliers or influence | Studentized residuals, Cook's distance (@ch09-studentized, @ch09-cooks-distance) | Investigate the case; consider robust regression (@ch10-huber) |
| A few high-leverage points dominate the fit | Leverage values $h_{ii}$ from the hat matrix (@ch09-leverage) | Check for extrapolation; refit without the point and compare |
| Right response type at all | Fitted values leave the valid range; variance grows with the mean | Change families: logistic for 0/1 (@ch13-logistic-model), Poisson for counts (@ch14-poisson-model) |

Two habits keep you out of trouble. First, always CHECK before you USE: a slope
with a tiny $p$-value is worthless if the residual plot shows the model is the
wrong shape. Second, when a transformation fixes one problem it often changes the
interpretation; @ch10-log-interpretation shows how a log turns a slope into a
percent-change statement, which is a feature, not a nuisance.

There is also the honest limit case. When the sample is small and you cannot trust
the normal-theory $t$ and $F$ at all, do not pretend. The permutation test and the
bootstrap (@ch05-permutation-slope, @ch05-bootstrap) ask for far less and give you
a $p$-value or interval you can defend. @ch05-thin-ice is the section that tells
you when the classical machinery is standing on thin ice.

## F.5 A worked mini-decision

Here is the guide used once, start to finish, on a real thread from the book: the
Galapagos species data, where each island records the number of plant species it
holds along with its area, elevation, and distance from other islands.

**Question 1: what kind of response?** The response is a count of species: 0, 1,
2, and up, never negative, never fractional. The master fork of Section F.1 sends
counts to the Poisson / GLM family (@ch14-poisson-model), not straight to linear
regression.

**But the book tried linear first, on purpose.** In Chapter 9 the same data is fit
with ordinary least squares, and the diagnostics of Section F.4 fire loudly:
Isabela, a huge island, sits at an extreme leverage value, and the residual
variance grows with the fitted count, breaking constant variance. That is
the "variance grows with the mean" and "high-leverage points" rows of the remedy
table.

**Follow the remedy arrows.** The constant-variance failure first suggests a
transformation (@ch10-box-cox), and @ch10-transform-limits shows that a log helps
but does not truly fix a count response: islands with zero or few species strain
any transformation of $Y$.

**Land on the right family.** The clean resolution is the one the response type
pointed to all along: model the counts with Poisson regression and a log link
(@ch14-poisson-model), where the variance is allowed to grow with the mean by
design. @ch14-gala-resolution closes the thread, and @ch14-overdispersion adds the
one more check you owe a count model: whether the spread is even larger than
Poisson allows, in which case you widen to quasi-Poisson.

The lesson of the walk-through is the shape of the guide itself. The response type
named the destination at Section F.1; the questions and the diagnostics explained
why the detour through linear regression was worth taking and where it had to stop.

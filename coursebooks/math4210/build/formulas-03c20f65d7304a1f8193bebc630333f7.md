# Appendix A: Formula Summary

This appendix collects every named formula from Chapters 1 through 16, in the order each
chapter introduces it. Each formula keeps the ALRM notation used throughout the book: $Y_i$
for a response, $X_i$ for a predictor, $\beta_0, \beta_1, \dots$ for population parameters,
$b_0, b_1, \dots$ for their least-squares estimates, $e_i$ for a residual, $\mathbf{X}$ for the
design matrix, and a prime for transpose. One plain-language line under each formula states
what it claims, so this page works as a quick lookup during homework and exams rather than a
replacement for the chapter's derivation. For full context, definitions, and worked examples,
follow the chapter link in each heading.

## A.1 Chapter 1: Regression as a way of thinking

**Anatomy of a statistical model**

$$Y = f(X) + \varepsilon$$

Any response splits into a systematic part $f(X)$ that depends on the predictor and a random
part $\varepsilon$ that does not.

**Simple linear regression function**

$$E\{Y\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X$$

The average response at a given $X$ falls on a straight line with intercept $\beta_0$ and
slope $\beta_1$.

**Fitted line (estimates)**

$$\hat{Y} = b_0 + b_1 X$$

The fitted line uses sample estimates $b_0$ and $b_1$ in place of the unknown $\beta_0$ and
$\beta_1$ to predict a response from $X$.

**Galton's fitted height line**

$$\widehat{\text{child}} = 22.64 + 0.637\,\text{midparent}$$

A child's predicted height rises by about 0.637 inch for each extra inch of average parent
height, the historical example that gave regression its name.

## A.2 Chapter 2: Simple linear regression

**SLR model**

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \varepsilon_i$$

Each observed response is the true line at $X_i$ plus a random error $\varepsilon_i$.

**Error assumptions**

$$E\{\varepsilon_i\}=0,\ \operatorname{Var}\{\varepsilon_i\}=\sigma^2,\ \operatorname{Cov}\{\varepsilon_i,\varepsilon_j\}=0$$

Errors average to zero, share one variance $\sigma^2$ across all observations, and do not
correlate with each other.

**Mean response**

$$E\{Y\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X$$

The population line traces the average of $Y$ at each value of $X$.

**Least-squares criterion**

$$Q(b_0,b_1)=\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - b_0 - b_1 X_i)^2$$

Least squares picks the intercept and slope that make the sum of squared vertical gaps to the
data as small as possible.

**Deviation sums**

$$S_{xx}=\sum (X_i-\bar X)^2,\ S_{xy}=\sum (X_i-\bar X)(Y_i-\bar Y),\ S_{yy}=\sum (Y_i-\bar Y)^2$$

$S_{xx}$ measures spread in $X$, $S_{yy}$ measures spread in $Y$, and $S_{xy}$ measures how $X$
and $Y$ move together; every least-squares formula below is built from these three numbers.

**Normal equations**

$$\sum (Y_i - b_0 - b_1 X_i)=0,\quad \sum X_i (Y_i - b_0 - b_1 X_i)=0$$

Setting the calculus derivatives of $Q$ to zero gives two linear equations in $b_0$ and $b_1$
whose solution is the least-squares line.

**Slope estimate**

$$b_1 = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$$

The estimated slope is the covariance of $X$ and $Y$ scaled by the spread of $X$.

**Intercept estimate**

$$b_0 = \bar Y - b_1 \bar X$$

The estimated intercept forces the fitted line through the point of averages $(\bar X, \bar Y)$.

**Fitted value**

$$\hat Y_i = b_0 + b_1 X_i$$

The fitted value is where the estimated line sits above (or below) observation $i$'s predictor
value.

**Residual**

$$e_i = Y_i - \hat Y_i$$

The residual is the vertical gap between an observed response and its fitted value.

**Residual identities**

$$\sum e_i = 0,\ \sum X_i e_i = 0,\ \sum \hat Y_i e_i = 0,\ \sum \hat Y_i = \sum Y_i$$

The least-squares residuals sum to zero, are uncorrelated with $X$ and with the fitted values,
and preserve the total of $Y$, four automatic checks on any regression fit.

**Error sum of squares**

$$\mathrm{SSE}=\sum e_i^2 = S_{yy} - b_1^2 S_{xx}$$

$\mathrm{SSE}$ totals the squared residuals, the leftover variation the line does not explain.

**Error variance estimate**

$$s^2 = \mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n-2)$$

Dividing $\mathrm{SSE}$ by $n-2$, the degrees of freedom left after estimating two parameters,
gives an unbiased estimate of the error variance $\sigma^2$.

**Unbiasedness of MSE**

$$E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \sigma^2$$

On average, across repeated samples, $\mathrm{MSE}$ hits the true error variance exactly.

**Mean and variance of $b_1$**

$$E\{b_1\}=\beta_1,\ \operatorname{Var}\{b_1\}=\sigma^2/S_{xx}$$

The slope estimate is unbiased, and it grows less variable as the predictor spreads out more.

**Mean and variance of $b_0$**

$$E\{b_0\}=\beta_0,\ \operatorname{Var}\{b_0\}=\sigma^2(1/n + \bar X^2/S_{xx})$$

The intercept estimate is unbiased, with variance that grows if $\bar X$ sits far from zero.

**Estimated standard errors**

$$s\{b_1\}=\sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}},\ s\{b_0\}=\sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}(1/n+\bar X^2/S_{xx})}$$

Replacing $\sigma^2$ with $\mathrm{MSE}$ in the variance formulas gives the standard errors
software reports.

**$b_1$ as a linear estimator**

$$b_1=\sum k_i Y_i,\ k_i=(X_i-\bar X)/S_{xx}$$

The slope estimate is a weighted sum of the responses, which is the fact the Gauss-Markov proof
builds on.

**Normal likelihood**

$$L=\prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1 X_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Under normal errors, this product measures how plausible a candidate $(\beta_0, \beta_1,
\sigma^2)$ is given the observed data.

**MLE of variance**

$$\hat\sigma^2_{\text{MLE}} = \mathrm{SSE}/n$$

Maximizing the likelihood over $\sigma^2$ gives a variance estimate that divides by $n$ instead
of $n-2$, which is why it is slightly biased low.

## A.3 Chapter 3: Inference for simple linear regression

**Sampling distribution of $b_1$**

$$b_1 \sim N(\beta_1, \sigma^2/S_{xx})$$

Across repeated samples, the slope estimate follows a normal distribution centered at the true
slope.

**Sampling distribution of $b_0$**

$$b_0 \sim N(\beta_0, \sigma^2(1/n + \bar X^2/S_{xx}))$$

The intercept estimate is likewise normal and unbiased, with the variance derived in Chapter 2.

**$t$ statistic for the slope**

$$(b_1 - \beta_1)/s\{b_1\} \sim t_{n-2}$$

Standardizing the slope estimate by its estimated standard error gives a $t$ distribution with
$n-2$ degrees of freedom, the basis for every slope interval and test.

**Confidence interval for the slope**

$$b_1 \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}\, s\{b_1\}$$

This interval is a range of plausible values for the true slope $\beta_1$, built from the
estimate plus or minus a $t$ multiple of its standard error.

**Slope test statistic**

$$t^{\ast} = b_1/s\{b_1\}$$

This ratio measures how many standard errors the estimated slope sits from zero, used to test
whether $X$ predicts $Y$ at all.

**Estimated standard error of the slope**

$$s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}}$$

This is the number software reports next to $b_1$, an estimate of how much the slope would
bounce around across repeated samples.

**Estimated standard error of the intercept**

$$s\{b_0\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}(1/n + \bar X^2/S_{xx})}$$

This is the corresponding uncertainty measure for the intercept estimate.

**Variance of the fitted mean**

$$s^2\{\hat Y_h\} = \mathrm{MSE}(1/n + (X_h - \bar X)^2/S_{xx})$$

The uncertainty in a fitted mean response grows the farther $X_h$ sits from the center of the
data.

**Confidence interval for a mean response**

$$\hat Y_h \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}\, s\{\hat Y_h\}$$

This interval traps the true average response at $X_h$, not a single new observation.

**Variance of a prediction**

$$s^2\{\mathrm{pred}\} = \mathrm{MSE}(1 + 1/n + (X_h - \bar X)^2/S_{xx})$$

Predicting one new observation carries an extra unit of variance beyond estimating the mean,
because a single case also carries its own random error.

**Prediction interval for a new observation**

$$\hat Y_h \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}\, s\{\mathrm{pred}\}$$

This wider interval is built to cover one future observation at $X_h$, not the population
average.

**ANOVA decomposition**

$$\mathrm{SSTO} = \mathrm{SSR} + \mathrm{SSE}$$

Total variation in $Y$ splits exactly into the part the line explains ($\mathrm{SSR}$) and the
part it leaves over ($\mathrm{SSE}$).

**Mean squares**

$$\mathrm{MSR} = \mathrm{SSR}/1,\ \mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n-2)$$

Dividing each sum of squares by its degrees of freedom converts it into an average squared
contribution, ready to compare in an $F$ ratio.

**Regression sum of squares**

$$\mathrm{SSR} = \sum (\hat Y_i - \bar Y)^2 = b_1^2 S_{xx}$$

$\mathrm{SSR}$ measures how far the fitted values swing away from the overall mean, the credit
the line earns for tracking $Y$.

**Expected mean squares**

$$E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \sigma^2,\ E\{\mathrm{MSR}\} = \sigma^2 + \beta_1^2 S_{xx}$$

$\mathrm{MSR}$ only exceeds $\mathrm{MSE}$ on average when the true slope is nonzero, which is
the logic behind the $F$ test.

**$F$ test statistic**

$$F^{\ast} = \mathrm{MSR}/\mathrm{MSE} \sim F_{1,\,n-2}$$

A large ratio of explained to unexplained variance is evidence against the null hypothesis that
$\beta_1 = 0$.

**$F$ equals $t$ squared**

$$F^{\ast} = (t^{\ast})^2$$

In simple regression the $F$ test for the slope and the two-sided $t$ test for the slope are
the same test written two ways.

**Coefficient of determination**

$$R^2 = \mathrm{SSR}/\mathrm{SSTO} = 1 - \mathrm{SSE}/\mathrm{SSTO}$$

$R^2$ is the proportion of total variation in $Y$ that the line explains, between 0 and 1.

**$R$-squared equals $r$-squared**

$$R^2 = r^2,\ r = S_{xy}/\sqrt{S_{xx}S_{yy}}$$

In simple regression, the coefficient of determination is exactly the squared Pearson
correlation between $X$ and $Y$.

**Working-Hotelling band**

$$\hat Y_h \pm W\, s\{\hat Y_h\},\ W^2 = 2 F_{1-\alpha;\,2,\,n-2}$$

This band covers the whole regression line at every $X_h$ simultaneously, wider at each single
point than the pointwise confidence interval.

## A.4 Chapter 4: Correlation

**Sample covariance**

$$s_{xy} = S_{xy}/(n-1) = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum (X_i-\bar X)(Y_i-\bar Y)$$

Covariance measures the average product of how far $X$ and $Y$ each stray from their own means,
positive when they tend to rise together.

**Pearson correlation**

$$r = \frac{s_{xy}}{s_x s_y} = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} S_{yy}}}$$

Correlation rescales covariance by both standard deviations so the result never depends on the
units of $X$ or $Y$.

**Bounds of $r$**

$$-1 \le r \le 1$$

Correlation always lands between perfect negative and perfect positive linear association.

**Correlation and slope**

$$b_1 = r\,\frac{s_y}{s_x}, \qquad r = b_1\,\frac{s_x}{s_y}$$

The regression slope and the correlation carry the same sign and convert into each other once
you rescale by the ratio of standard deviations.

**Determination equals $r$ squared**

$$R^2 = \mathrm{SSR}/\mathrm{SSTO} = r^2$$

Squaring the correlation gives the share of variation the line explains.

**Product of the two regression slopes**

$$b_1 \cdot b_1' = r^2, \quad b_1' = S_{xy}/S_{yy}$$

Regressing $Y$ on $X$ and regressing $X$ on $Y$ give two different lines whose slopes multiply
to $r^2$, a reminder that "the" regression line depends on which variable you predict.

**Correlation $t$ test**

$$t^{\ast} = \frac{r\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-r^2}} \sim t_{n-2}\ \text{under } H_0:\rho=0$$

This statistic tests whether the true correlation $\rho$ is zero, and it equals the slope's $t$
test exactly.

**Fisher $z$ transformation**

$$z = \operatorname{arctanh}(r) = \tfrac12\ln\frac{1+r}{1-r} \approx N\!\left(\operatorname{arctanh}\rho,\ \tfrac{1}{n-3}\right)$$

This transform straightens out $r$'s skewed sampling distribution into an approximately normal
one, which makes confidence intervals for $\rho$ possible.

**Confidence interval for $\rho$**

$$\tanh\!\left(\operatorname{arctanh}(r) \pm z_{1-\alpha/2}/\sqrt{n-3}\right)$$

Build the interval on the $z$ scale, then transform both endpoints back to the correlation
scale.

**Variance of $r$ (large sample)**

$$\operatorname{Var}(r) \approx (1-\rho^2)^2/n$$

Correlation estimates get more precise with more data and, for a fixed $n$, are least variable
when the true correlation is near zero.

**Bivariate normal density**

$$f(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_X\sigma_Y\sqrt{1-\rho^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}\left[\frac{(x-\mu_X)^2}{\sigma_X^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y} + \frac{(y-\mu_Y)^2}{\sigma_Y^2}\right]\right)$$

This surface describes two jointly normal variables at once; its five parameters are the two
means, two standard deviations, and the correlation $\rho$.

**Bivariate normal conditional mean**

$$E\{Y \mid X=x\} = \mu_Y + \rho\,\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(x-\mu_X)$$

Under bivariate normality, the average of $Y$ given $X = x$ is exactly a straight line in $x$,
which is why simple linear regression is the natural model here.

**Bivariate normal conditional variance**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{Y \mid X=x\} = \sigma_Y^2(1-\rho^2)$$

Knowing $X$ shrinks the spread of $Y$ by a factor of $1-\rho^2$, the same factor that shows up
as $1 - R^2$ in regression.

**Spearman rank correlation**

$$r_S = \text{Pearson correlation of } \operatorname{rank}(X_i),\ \operatorname{rank}(Y_i)$$

Replacing each value with its rank before computing Pearson's $r$ gives a correlation that
detects any monotone relationship, not only a straight-line one.

## A.5 Chapter 5: Randomization and bootstrap inference for regression

**Slope estimate**

$$b_1 = \frac{\sum (X_i-\bar X)(Y_i-\bar Y)}{\sum (X_i-\bar X)^2} = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$$

The same least-squares slope from Chapter 2 is the statistic this chapter re-derives its
uncertainty for, without assuming normal errors.

**No-association null**

$$H_0: X \text{ and } Y \text{ unrelated } (\beta_1 = 0)$$

The permutation test's null hypothesis is that the pairing between $X_i$ and $Y_i$ carries no
information, so shuffling one against the other should not matter.

**Permuted slope**

$$b_1^{\pi} = \sum (X_i-\bar X)(Y_{\pi(i)}-\bar Y)/S_{xx} = S_{xy}^{\pi}/S_{xx}$$

Recomputing the slope after randomly reshuffling the $Y$ values against the $X$ values gives one
draw from the null distribution.

**Exact permutation $p$-value**

$$p_{\text{exact}} = \#\{\pi : |b_1^{\pi}| \ge |b_1|\}/n!$$

The exact $p$-value is the fraction of all possible shuffles that produce a slope as extreme as
the one actually observed.

**Monte Carlo permutation $p$-value**

$$p_{\text{perm}} = (M+1)/(B+1)$$

Since checking all $n!$ shuffles is usually impossible, this estimate uses $B$ random shuffles,
with $M$ of them at least as extreme as the observed slope.

**Permutation resolution floor**

$$\min p_{\text{perm}} = 1/(B+1)$$

With only $B$ permutations, the smallest reportable $p$-value is $1/(B+1)$, so a very small true
$p$-value needs many permutations to resolve.

**Case-resampling bootstrap slope**

$$b_1^{\ast b} = S_{xy}^{\ast}/S_{xx}^{\ast} \text{ from } n \text{ rows drawn with replacement}$$

Each bootstrap replicate refits the slope on a resample of whole $(X_i, Y_i)$ pairs drawn with
replacement from the original data.

**Residual-resampling bootstrap**

$$Y_i^{\ast} = \hat Y_i + e_{s(i)}^{\ast}, \quad \hat Y_i = b_0 + b_1 X_i, \quad e_i = Y_i - \hat Y_i$$

This alternative bootstrap keeps the $X$ values fixed and builds new responses by adding
randomly resampled residuals to the fitted line.

**Bootstrap standard error**

$$s_{\text{boot}}\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{B-1}\sum_{b=1}^{B}(b_1^{\ast b} - \bar b_1^{\ast})^2}$$

The spread of the bootstrap slope estimates across replicates estimates the standard error of
$b_1$ without any normal-error formula.

**Percentile interval**

$$(q_{\alpha/2},\ q_{1-\alpha/2})$$

This interval simply reads off the lower and upper quantiles of the bootstrap distribution of
$b_1^{\ast}$.

**Basic (reflection) interval**

$$(2 b_1 - q_{1-\alpha/2},\ 2 b_1 - q_{\alpha/2})$$

This interval reflects the bootstrap quantiles around the original estimate, which corrects for
skew that the plain percentile interval can miss.

**Classical $t$ interval for slope**

$$b_1 \pm t^{\ast}_{0.975,\,n-2}\, s\{b_1\}$$

The Chapter 3 formula reappears here as the benchmark the permutation and bootstrap intervals
are compared against.

## A.6 Chapter 6: Matrix algebra for regression

**Model in matrix form**

$$\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$$

Stacking every observation into vectors and matrices writes the whole regression model, with
any number of predictors, in one equation.

**Design matrix dimensions**

$$\mathbf{Y}\ (n\times 1),\ \mathbf{X}\ (n\times p),\ \boldsymbol{\beta}\ (p\times 1)$$

$\mathbf{X}$ has one row per observation and one column per parameter, including a column of
ones for the intercept, so $p$ counts parameters and $n$ counts observations.

**Matrix product entry**

$$(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B})_{ij} = \sum_{\ell} A_{i\ell} B_{\ell j}$$

Each entry of a matrix product is the dot product of a row of $\mathbf{A}$ with a column of
$\mathbf{B}$.

**Transpose of a product**

$$(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B})' = \mathbf{B}'\mathbf{A}'$$

Transposing a product reverses the order of the factors.

**Cross-product matrix**

$$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})_{jk} = \sum_i X_{ij} X_{ik}$$

Each entry of $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is a sum of products between two columns of $\mathbf{X}$,
summarizing how the predictors relate to each other and to themselves.

**Cross-product vector**

$$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y})_j = \sum_i X_{ij} Y_i$$

This vector summarizes how each predictor column relates to the response.

**Symmetry of $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$**

$$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})' = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$$

The cross-product matrix always equals its own transpose, a fact the least-squares derivation
relies on.

**2x2 determinant and inverse**

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc,\quad \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$$

For a two-by-two matrix, the determinant $ad - bc$ tells you whether an inverse exists, and this
formula hand-computes it when it does.

**Matrix normal equations**

$$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\,\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$$

The two-equation normal equations from Chapter 2 collapse into this single matrix equation that
works for any number of predictors.

**Least-squares estimator**

$$\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$$

Inverting $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ and multiplying by $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ solves the normal
equations in one step, giving every least-squares coefficient at once.

**Hat matrix**

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$$

$\mathbf{H}$ is the matrix that turns the observed responses directly into fitted values,
"putting a hat on" $\mathbf{Y}$.

**Fitted values and residuals**

$$\widehat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y},\quad \mathbf{e} = (\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}$$

The hat matrix produces the fitted values directly, and what remains after subtracting them
from $\mathbf{Y}$ is the residual vector.

**Idempotency of $\mathbf{H}$**

$$\mathbf{H}\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H},\quad \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p$$

Applying the hat matrix twice does nothing more than applying it once, and its diagonal entries
sum to $p$, the number of parameters.

**SSE as a quadratic form**

$$\mathrm{SSE} = \mathbf{Y}'(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e}$$

The error sum of squares can be written directly as a quadratic form in $\mathbf{Y}$, or
equivalently as the squared length of the residual vector.

**SS decomposition (matrix)**

$$(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H}) + (\mathbf{H}-\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{J}) = \mathbf{I}-\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{J}$$

This matrix identity is the geometric version of $\mathrm{SSTO} = \mathrm{SSR} + \mathrm{SSE}$:
three projection matrices that add up exactly.

**Variance estimate**

$$\mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n-p)$$

With $p$ parameters instead of two, the error variance estimate now divides by $n-p$ degrees of
freedom.

**Expectation of a linear map**

$$E\{\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}+\mathbf{c}\} = \mathbf{A}E\{\mathbf{W}\}+\mathbf{c}$$

Expectation passes through a fixed matrix and a fixed vector added afterward, the matrix
analogue of $E\{aW + c\} = aE\{W\} + c$.

**Covariance of a linear map**

$$\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}\} = \mathbf{A}\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{W}\}\mathbf{A}'$$

Transforming a random vector by a fixed matrix sandwiches the original covariance matrix between
that matrix and its transpose.

**Unbiasedness of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$E\{\mathbf{b}\} = \boldsymbol{\beta}$$

On average, the least-squares coefficient vector recovers the true parameter vector exactly.

**Covariance of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$$

This matrix holds the variance of every coefficient on its diagonal and the covariance between
pairs of coefficients off the diagonal.

**Estimated covariance of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$s^2\{\mathbf{b}\} = \mathrm{MSE}\,(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$$

Substituting $\mathrm{MSE}$ for $\sigma^2$ gives the covariance matrix software actually reports.

**Sampling law of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$\mathbf{b} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\beta},\ \sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1})$$

Under normal errors, the whole coefficient vector follows a multivariate normal distribution
centered at the truth.

**Multivariate normal of $\mathbf{Y}$**

$$\mathbf{Y} \sim N(\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta},\ \sigma^2\mathbf{I})$$

The response vector itself is multivariate normal, with mean given by the regression surface and
independent equal-variance errors.

## A.7 Chapter 7: The general linear model

**General linear model**

$$\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$$

The same matrix model from Chapter 6 is now the standing model for every remaining chapter,
covering any number and kind of predictor.

**Error moments**

$$E\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \mathbf{0},\ \operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}$$

Errors average to zero, share one variance, and do not correlate with each other, the matrix
form of the Chapter 2 assumptions.

**Sum of squares objective**

$$Q(\mathbf{b}) = (\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b})'(\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b})$$

This is the squared length of the residual vector, the quantity least squares minimizes over
$\mathbf{b}$.

**Normal equations**

$$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$$

Setting the gradient of $Q(\mathbf{b})$ to zero gives this equation, whose solution is the
least-squares estimate.

**Least-squares estimator**

$$\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$$

This is the same closed-form solution from Chapter 6, now the working formula for every model
in the rest of the book.

**Orthogonality of residual**

$$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$$

The residual vector is perpendicular to every column of $\mathbf{X}$, the algebraic reason the
residuals are uncorrelated with each predictor.

**Hat matrix**

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$$

$\mathbf{H}$ projects the response vector onto the column space of $\mathbf{X}$, the geometric
meaning behind "fitting" a regression.

**Fitted values**

$$\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$$

Applying the hat matrix to the data produces the fitted values, the closest point to
$\mathbf{Y}$ that the model can reach.

**Residual vector**

$$\mathbf{e} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}$$

The residuals are what is left of $\mathbf{Y}$ after removing the part the model can explain.

**Hat matrix trace**

$$\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p,\ \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = n - p$$

The hat matrix's diagonal sums to $p$, the number of fitted parameters, which leaves $n-p$
degrees of freedom for estimating error.

**Mean of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$E\{\mathbf{b}\} = \boldsymbol{\beta}$$

Least squares is unbiased for any design matrix $\mathbf{X}$ of full column rank.

**Covariance of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$$

This is the general-model version of the coefficient covariance matrix, used to build every
standard error, confidence interval, and test in multiple regression.

**Sampling distribution of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$\mathbf{b} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\beta},\ \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1})$$

Under normal errors, the coefficient vector is exactly multivariate normal, which underlies
every $t$ and $F$ procedure in multiple regression.

**Error sum of squares**

$$\mathrm{SSE} = \mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y}'(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}$$

$\mathrm{SSE}$ is the squared length of the residual vector, written two equivalent ways.

**Error variance estimator**

$$s^2 = \mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n - p)$$

Dividing $\mathrm{SSE}$ by $n-p$ gives an unbiased estimate of $\sigma^2$ for a model with $p$
parameters.

**Unbiasedness of MSE**

$$E\{\mathrm{SSE}\} = (n - p)\sigma^2$$

This expectation is the algebraic reason dividing by $n-p$, not $n$, makes $\mathrm{MSE}$
unbiased.

**Quadratic form expectation**

$$E\{\mathbf{Y}'\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}\} = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) + \boldsymbol{\mu}'\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu}$$

This general tool computes the expected value of any quadratic form in a random vector,
the technique behind proving $E\{\mathrm{SSE}\} = (n-p)\sigma^2$.

**Estimated covariance of $\mathbf{b}$**

$$s^2\{\mathbf{b}\} = \mathrm{MSE}\,(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$$

Every coefficient's standard error, in R's or Python's regression output, is the square root of
a diagonal entry of this matrix.

**Coefficient of determination**

$$R^2 = 1 - \mathrm{SSE}/\mathrm{SSTO}$$

$R^2$ still measures the proportion of total variation explained, now for a model with any
number of predictors.

## A.8 Chapter 8: Multiple regression in practice

**Multiple linear regression model**

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{i1} + \cdots + \beta_{p-1} X_{i,p-1} + \varepsilon_i$$

Each response depends on several predictors at once, with $p-1$ slopes and one intercept, for
$p$ parameters total.

**Partial slope (least squares)**

$$\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$$

Each entry of $\mathbf{b}$ is a partial slope: the effect of one predictor holding the others in
the model fixed.

**Error variance estimate**

$$\mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n-p)$$

This is the multiple-regression version of the error variance estimate from Chapter 7, with $p$
counting every predictor plus the intercept.

**Extra sum of squares**

$$\mathrm{SSR}(X_2 \mid X_1) = \mathrm{SSE}(X_1) - \mathrm{SSE}(X_1, X_2) = \mathrm{SSR}(X_1,X_2) - \mathrm{SSR}(X_1)$$

This measures how much extra variation $X_2$ explains once $X_1$ is already in the model.

**Sequential decomposition of SSR**

$$\mathrm{SSR}(X_1,X_2,X_3) = \mathrm{SSR}(X_1) + \mathrm{SSR}(X_2\mid X_1) + \mathrm{SSR}(X_3\mid X_1,X_2)$$

The total explained variation splits into a chain of extra contributions, one predictor added at
a time in a chosen order.

**General linear test statistic**

$$F^{*} = \frac{[\mathrm{SSE}(R) - \mathrm{SSE}(F)]/(df_R - df_F)}{\mathrm{SSE}(F)/df_F}$$

Comparing a reduced model $R$ against a full model $F$, this ratio tests whether the extra
predictors in $F$ improve the fit by more than chance.

**Pythagoras for nested models**

$$\mathrm{SSE}(R) = \mathrm{SSE}(F) + \lVert \widehat{\mathbf{Y}}_F - \widehat{\mathbf{Y}}_R\rVert^2$$

The reduced model's error splits into the full model's error plus a right-angle piece measuring
the extra distance the bigger model's fit travels.

**One-coefficient test equals $t$ squared**

$$F^{*} = b_k^2 / s^2\{b_k\} = t_k^2$$

Testing one coefficient with the general linear test gives the same answer as the usual $t$
test, squared.

**Added-variable slope equals coefficient**

$$\text{slope of } e_Y \text{ on } e_X = b_k$$

Regressing the residuals of $Y$ against the residuals of $X_k$, after removing every other
predictor from both, recovers $X_k$'s own coefficient $b_k$.

**Coefficient of determination**

$$R^2 = 1 - \mathrm{SSE}/\mathrm{SSTO}$$

$R^2$ measures the share of total variation the full set of predictors explains together.

**Adjusted $R$ squared**

$$R^2_a = 1 - \frac{n-1}{n-p}(1 - R^2)$$

This version penalizes adding predictors that do not pull their weight, so it can fall even when
plain $R^2$ rises.

**Standardized coefficient**

$$b_k^{*} = b_k\,(s_{X_k}/s_Y)$$

Rescaling a coefficient by the ratio of standard deviations puts predictors measured in
different units on a common scale for comparing their impact.

## A.9 Chapter 9: Model diagnostics

**Hat matrix**

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$$

The same projection matrix from Chapter 7 now supplies the diagonal entries that flag unusual
predictor values.

**Leverage value**

$$h_{ii} = \mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i$$

An observation's leverage value measures how far its predictor values sit from the center of
the predictor space, and how much pull it has on its own fitted value.

**Leverage value bounds and sum**

$$0 \le h_{ii} \le 1,\quad \sum_i h_{ii} = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p$$

Every leverage value falls between zero and one, and the leverage values across all observations
always add up to the number of parameters.

**Leverage value in simple regression**

$$h_{ii} = \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_i-\bar X)^2}{S_{xx}}$$

In simple regression, the leverage value grows with squared distance from $\bar X$, confirming
that points far from the center of $X$ carry more influence.

**Leverage value cutoff**

$$h_{ii} > 2p/n$$

This rule flags an observation as a high-leverage point when its leverage value exceeds twice
the average leverage value of $p/n$.

**Residual covariance**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{e}\} = \sigma^2(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H}),\ \operatorname{Var}\{e_i\}=\sigma^2(1-h_{ii})$$

Residuals do not all share the same variance; high-leverage points have smaller residual
variance because the fit is pulled closer to them.

**Semistudentized residual**

$$e_i/s$$

Dividing a residual by the overall residual standard deviation gives a rough check for outliers
that ignores the varying leverage values across observations.

**Standardized residual**

$$r_i = e_i/(s\sqrt{1-h_{ii}})$$

This version corrects for each observation's own leverage value, giving residuals that are more
directly comparable across the dataset.

**Studentized deleted residual**

$$t_i = e_i/(s_{(i)}\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}) \sim t(n-p-1)$$

This residual refits the model without observation $i$ before standardizing it, which gives an
exact $t$ distribution for flagging outliers.

**Deleted variance identity**

$$(n-p-1)s_{(i)}^2 = (n-p)\mathrm{MSE} - e_i^2/(1-h_{ii})$$

This shortcut computes the error variance from the model with observation $i$ removed, without
actually refitting.

**Deleted residual**

$$d_i = e_i/(1-h_{ii}),\ \operatorname{Var}\{d_i\}=\sigma^2/(1-h_{ii})$$

This is the gap between observation $i$'s response and the prediction a model trained without it
would make.

**Bonferroni outlier cutoff**

$$|t_i| > t(1-\alpha/(2n);\, n-p-1)$$

Testing every observation for being an outlier at once inflates the false-positive rate, so this
cutoff tightens the threshold by a factor of $n$ to compensate.

**Leave-one-out coefficient update**

$$\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{b}_{(i)} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i e_i/(1-h_{ii})$$

This formula gives the exact shift in every coefficient caused by removing one observation,
without refitting the model.

**Sherman-Morrison downdate**

$$(\mathbf{A}-\mathbf{u}\mathbf{u}')^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} + \frac{\mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{u}\mathbf{u}'\mathbf{A}^{-1}}{1-\mathbf{u}'\mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{u}}$$

This linear algebra identity updates a matrix inverse after removing one row, the engine behind
every leave-one-out formula in this chapter.

**Cook's distance**

$$D_i = \frac{\sum_j(\hat Y_j-\hat Y_{j(i)})^2}{p\,\mathrm{MSE}} = \frac{r_i^2}{p}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{1-h_{ii}}$$

Cook's distance combines how large a residual is with how large its leverage value is, into a
single number measuring an observation's overall influence on all the fitted values.

**DFFITS**

$$\mathrm{DFFITS}_i = t_i\sqrt{h_{ii}/(1-h_{ii})}$$

This measures how many standard errors observation $i$'s own fitted value shifts when that
observation is removed.

**DFBETAS**

$$\mathrm{DFBETAS}_{k(i)} = (b_k - b_{k(i)})/(s_{(i)}\sqrt{(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}_{kk}})$$

This measures how many standard errors one specific coefficient shifts when observation $i$ is
removed, pinpointing which parameter a case influences most.

**Durbin-Watson statistic**

$$D = \frac{\sum_{t=2}^n (e_t-e_{t-1})^2}{\sum_{t=1}^n e_t^2} \approx 2(1-\hat\rho)$$

This statistic compares consecutive residuals to detect leftover autocorrelation, with values
near 2 indicating none.

**Lack-of-fit decomposition**

$$\mathrm{SSE} = \mathrm{SSPE} + \mathrm{SSLF}$$

When some predictor values repeat, the error sum of squares splits into pure error, unavoidable
scatter at repeated $X$'s, and lack of fit, systematic bias from a wrong functional form.

**Pure error sum of squares**

$$\mathrm{SSPE} = \sum_{j=1}^c \sum_k (Y_{jk}-\bar Y_j)^2$$

This totals the scatter of responses around their own group mean at each repeated predictor
value, variation no model could ever explain.

**Lack-of-fit sum of squares**

$$\mathrm{SSLF} = \sum_{j=1}^c n_j(\bar Y_j - \hat Y_j)^2$$

This totals the gap between each group's actual mean response and what the fitted line predicts
there, the part attributable to a wrong model shape.

**Lack-of-fit $F$ test**

$$F^\ast = \frac{\mathrm{SSLF}/(c-2)}{\mathrm{SSPE}/(n-c)} \sim F(c-2,\,n-c)$$

A large ratio here is evidence that the straight-line model misses real curvature or structure in
the data.

## A.10 Chapter 10: Remedial measures and transformations

**Log-log model**

$$\log Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \log X_i + \varepsilon_i$$

Taking logs of both variables fits a model where the relationship is a straight line on the log
scale, appropriate for multiplicative, curved relationships.

**Log-linear model**

$$\log Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \varepsilon_i$$

Taking a log of only the response fits exponential growth or decay in the original units.

**Log-log exact factor**

$$X \to cX \ \Rightarrow\ Y \to c^{\beta_1} Y$$

Multiplying $X$ by a constant multiplies the predicted $Y$ by that constant raised to the power
$\beta_1$, the defining property of a log-log model.

**Log-log elasticity (approx)**

$$\text{percent change in } Y \approx \beta_1 \times (\text{percent change in } X)$$

The slope in a log-log model is approximately the percent change in $Y$ per one percent change
in $X$, an elasticity.

**Log-linear exact factor**

$$X \to X+1 \ \Rightarrow\ Y \to e^{\beta_1} Y,\ \text{percent} = 100(e^{\beta_1}-1)$$

Adding one unit to $X$ multiplies predicted $Y$ by $e^{\beta_1}$, which converts to a percent
change.

**Box-Cox family**

$$Y^{(\lambda)} = (Y^{\lambda}-1)/\lambda \ (\lambda \neq 0),\ \log Y \ (\lambda = 0)$$

This one family of transformations includes the raw scale ($\lambda=1$), the log scale
($\lambda=0$), and everything between, so the data can pick the best power.

**Box-Cox full log-likelihood**

$$\ell = -\tfrac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \tfrac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum (Y_i^{(\lambda)} - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol{\beta})^2 + (\lambda-1)\sum \log Y_i$$

This likelihood scores how well a given transformation power $\lambda$ makes the transformed
data fit a normal-errors regression.

**Box-Cox profile log-likelihood**

$$\ell_p(\lambda) = -\tfrac{n}{2}\log(\mathrm{RSS}(\lambda)/n) + (\lambda-1)\sum_{i=1}^n \log Y_i$$

Maximizing over $\beta$ and $\sigma^2$ for each $\lambda$ leaves this profile, which is scanned
over $\lambda$ to find the best transformation.

**Box-Cox confidence interval**

$$\{\lambda : \ell_p(\lambda) \ge \ell_p(\hat\lambda) - \tfrac{1}{2}\chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}\}$$

Every $\lambda$ whose profile likelihood stays within this cutoff of the maximum is a plausible
transformation, not just the single best one.

**WLS variance model**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{\varepsilon_i\} = \sigma^2 / w_i,\ \operatorname{Cov}\{\varepsilon_i,\varepsilon_j\}=0$$

When error variance differs across observations but a known weight $w_i$ tracks it, this model
replaces the constant-variance assumption of ordinary least squares.

**Weighted least squares criterion**

$$Q_w(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i (Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol{\beta})^2$$

Weighted least squares minimizes a sum of squared errors where noisier observations, with
smaller weight, count for less.

**WLS estimator**

$$\mathbf{b}_W = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{Y}$$

This closed-form solution generalizes ordinary least squares by inserting the weight matrix
$\mathbf{W}$ into the normal equations.

**WLS variance**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{b}_W\} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X})^{-1}$$

This is the weighted-regression analogue of the ordinary least squares coefficient covariance.

**Whitening transform**

$$\mathbf{Y}^{\ast} = \mathbf{W}^{1/2}\mathbf{Y},\ \mathbf{X}^{\ast} = \mathbf{W}^{1/2}\mathbf{X},\ \operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{W}^{1/2}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\}=\sigma^2\mathbf{I}$$

Rescaling both $\mathbf{Y}$ and $\mathbf{X}$ by the square root of the weights turns weighted
least squares into an ordinary least squares problem on the transformed data.

**Known-variance weights**

$$w_i = 1/\mathrm{sd}_i^2$$

When each observation's error standard deviation is known or estimated separately, its weight is
the reciprocal of that variance, so noisier points count less.

**Huber loss**

$$\rho_c(u) = u^2/2 \ (|u|\le c),\ c|u| - c^2/2 \ (|u|>c)$$

This loss behaves like squared error for typical residuals but switches to a gentler
absolute-value penalty for large ones, limiting an outlier's pull on the fit.

**Huber weight**

$$w(u) = \min(1, c/|u|),\ c = 1.345$$

Robust regression re-expresses the Huber loss as a weighted least squares problem where large
residuals get automatically downweighted.

**Robust regression estimating equations**

$$\sum_i w(u_i)(Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol{\beta})\mathbf{x}_i = \mathbf{0}$$

These equations, solved iteratively since the weights depend on the fit, define the robust
regression estimate.

## A.11 Chapter 11: Categorical predictors and interactions

**Reference-cell indicators**

$$X_j = 1 \text{ if level } j,\ 0 \text{ otherwise},\ j = 1,\dots,k-1$$

A categorical predictor with $k$ levels becomes $k-1$ zero-one indicator columns, one level held
back as the reference.

**One-factor reference-cell model**

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{i1} + \cdots + \beta_{k-1} X_{i,k-1} + \varepsilon_i$$

This model fits a separate mean response for each category using indicator predictors inside the
ordinary regression framework.

**Group mean readings**

$$E\{Y\mid\text{ref}\}=\beta_0,\ E\{Y\mid\text{level }j\}=\beta_0+\beta_j$$

The intercept is the reference group's mean, and each coefficient shifts the mean up or down for
its own level.

**Indicator coefficient**

$$\beta_j = E\{Y\mid\text{level }j\} - E\{Y\mid\text{ref}\}$$

Every indicator coefficient is a difference in means between one level and the reference level.

**Rank-deficiency dependence**

$$X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_k = \mathbf{1}$$

Using all $k$ indicator columns instead of $k-1$ makes them add up to the intercept column
exactly, which is why one level must be dropped to keep $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ invertible.

**Parallel-lines (ANCOVA) model**

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 D_i + \varepsilon_i$$

This model fits two parallel lines, one per group, that share a common slope $\beta_1$ but have
different intercepts shifted by $\beta_2$.

**Interaction model**

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 D_i + \beta_3 X_i D_i + \varepsilon_i$$

Adding a product term lets the slope itself differ between groups, not just the intercept.

**Group slopes under interaction**

$$\text{slope}(D=0)=\beta_1,\ \text{slope}(D=1)=\beta_1+\beta_3$$

The interaction coefficient $\beta_3$ is exactly the difference in slope between the two groups.

**Between-group gap under interaction**

$$\text{gap}(X) = \beta_2 + \beta_3 X$$

Once slopes differ, the vertical gap between the two group lines is no longer constant; it
changes with $X$.

**General linear test (parallel vs separate)**

$$F = \frac{(\mathrm{SSE}_R - \mathrm{SSE}_F)/(df_R - df_F)}{\mathrm{SSE}_F/df_F}$$

This is the Chapter 8 general linear test applied to compare a parallel-lines model against a
model with an interaction term.

**$t$-$F$ equivalence for one term**

$$t^2 = F \quad (\text{1 numerator df})$$

Testing a single interaction coefficient with a $t$ test or with the general linear $F$ test
gives the same conclusion.

**Quadratic polynomial model**

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 X_i^2 + \varepsilon_i$$

Adding a squared term lets the fitted curve bend, while the model stays linear in the parameters
and fits with ordinary least squares.

**Centered predictor**

$$x_i = X_i - \bar X$$

Subtracting the mean before squaring reduces the correlation between $X_i$ and $X_i^2$, which
stabilizes the numerical fit of a polynomial model.

**Centered coefficient map**

$$\beta_0^{\ast}=\beta_0+\beta_1\bar X+\beta_2\bar X^2,\ \beta_1^{\ast}=\beta_1+2\beta_2\bar X,\ \beta_2^{\ast}=\beta_2$$

These equations translate the coefficients of a centered quadratic model back into the
coefficients of the original, uncentered one.

**Hinge (piecewise) predictor**

$$(X - c)_+ = \max(X - c,\ 0)$$

This term stays at zero until $X$ passes the breakpoint $c$, then rises linearly, letting the
slope change at $c$ while the model stays linear in its parameters.

**One-way ANOVA model**

$$Y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_i + \varepsilon_{ij}$$

Classic one-way ANOVA is a special case of regression with a categorical predictor, where
$\tau_i$ is the effect of group $i$.

**ANOVA null hypothesis**

$$H_0: \tau_1 = \cdots = \tau_k = 0 \ \Leftrightarrow\ \beta_1 = \cdots = \beta_{k-1} = 0$$

Testing whether all group effects are zero in the ANOVA formulation is exactly the general linear
test that all indicator coefficients are zero in the regression formulation.

**ANOVA / regression $F$**

$$F = \mathrm{MSR}/\mathrm{MSE}$$

The familiar ANOVA $F$ statistic is the same ratio of explained to unexplained mean squares used
throughout the book.

## A.12 Chapter 12: Multicollinearity, variable selection, and validation

**Variance inflation factor**

$$\mathrm{VIF}_k = \frac{1}{1 - R_k^2}$$

$\mathrm{VIF}_k$ measures how much $X_k$'s coefficient variance is inflated because $X_k$ can be
predicted from the other predictors, where $R_k^2$ comes from regressing $X_k$ on them.

**Inflated coefficient variance**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{b_k\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}(1 - R_k^2)} = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{kk}}\,\mathrm{VIF}_k$$

This shows directly how multicollinearity multiplies a coefficient's variance by its VIF beyond
what its own spread $S_{kk}$ would give alone.

**Adjusted $R$-squared**

$$R^2_{a,p} = 1 - \frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p/(n-p)}{\mathrm{SSTO}/(n-1)}$$

This model-selection criterion rewards a model with $p$ parameters for explaining variation while
penalizing it for using more parameters to do so.

**Mallows $C_p$**

$$C_p = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}_p}{\hat{\sigma}^2} - (n - 2p)$$

A candidate subset model is judged well-specified, neither underfit nor overly complex, when its
$C_p$ lands near $p$.

**AIC**

$$\mathrm{AIC}_p = n\ln(\mathrm{SSE}_p/n) + 2p$$

This criterion balances fit against complexity using a fixed penalty of two per parameter,
smaller values indicating a better tradeoff.

**BIC**

$$\mathrm{BIC}_p = n\ln(\mathrm{SSE}_p/n) + p\ln n$$

This criterion penalizes each added parameter more heavily than AIC once $n$ is at least eight,
favoring smaller models as sample size grows.

**PRESS statistic**

$$\mathrm{PRESS}_p = \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \hat{Y}_{(i)})^2$$

This totals the squared errors from predicting each observation using a model fit without that
observation, a built-in cross-validation score.

**PRESS deleted-residual shortcut**

$$\mathrm{PRESS} = \sum_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}}\right)^2$$

This computes PRESS from a single fit's residuals and leverages, without refitting the model $n$
times.

**Deleted residual**

$$Y_i - \hat{Y}_{(i)} = \frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}}$$

The gap between an observation's response and the prediction a model without that observation
would make can be recovered algebraically from the ordinary residual and leverage value.

**Root mean squared error**

$$\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{m}\sum (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2}$$

RMSE reports typical prediction error in the same units as $Y$, easier to interpret than a sum of
squares.

**$k$-fold cross-validated RMSE**

$$\mathrm{CV\ RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathrm{MSE}_k}$$

Splitting the data into $K$ folds, fitting on the rest, and averaging the held-out error across
folds gives an honest estimate of prediction accuracy on new data.

**Ridge estimator**

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\text{ridge}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \sum_i (Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol{\beta})^2 + \lambda\sum_k \beta_k^2$$

Ridge regression trades a little bias for less variance by shrinking every coefficient toward
zero, controlled by the penalty strength $\lambda$.

**Lasso estimator**

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\text{lasso}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \sum_i (Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol{\beta})^2 + \lambda\sum_k |\beta_k|$$

Lasso shrinks coefficients like ridge but can push some all the way to zero, performing variable
selection automatically.

**One-predictor ridge solution**

$$\hat{\beta}_{\text{ridge}} = \frac{\sum_i x_i Y_i}{\sum_i x_i^2 + \lambda}$$

With a single centered predictor, ridge regression has this simple closed form, showing directly
how larger $\lambda$ shrinks the estimate toward zero.

**Sherman-Morrison rank-one downdate**

$$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X} - \mathbf{x}_i\mathbf{x}_i')^{-1} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} + \frac{(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i\mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}}{1 - h_{ii}}$$

This identity, the same tool from Chapter 9, lets cross-validation formulas remove one
observation's contribution without refitting the whole model.

## A.13 Chapter 13: Logistic regression

**Odds**

$$\text{odds} = \pi/(1-\pi)$$

Odds compares the probability of an event to the probability it does not happen, a ratio rather
than a proportion.

**Logit (log-odds)**

$$\operatorname{logit}(\pi) = \log[\pi/(1-\pi)]$$

The logit stretches a probability, confined between 0 and 1, onto the whole real line, which
makes it possible to model with an ordinary linear predictor.

**Logistic regression model**

$$\operatorname{logit}(\pi_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{i1} + \dots + \beta_{p-1} X_{i,p-1}$$

Logistic regression fits a straight line, or plane, to the log-odds of an event rather than to
the probability itself.

**Logistic (mean) function**

$$\pi_i = 1/(1 + e^{-\eta_i})$$

This function converts the linear predictor $\eta_i$ back into a probability between 0 and 1.

**Binomial log-likelihood**

$$\ell(\beta) = \sum_i [Y_i \eta_i - m_i \log(1 + e^{\eta_i})] + C$$

This function scores how well a candidate coefficient vector explains the observed successes and
failures; maximizing it gives the logistic regression fit.

**Score equations**

$$\mathbf{X}'(\mathbf{Y} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) = \mathbf{0}, \quad \mu_i = m_i \pi_i$$

At the maximum likelihood estimate, the residuals (observed minus expected counts) are
uncorrelated with every predictor, the logistic analogue of $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$.

**Fisher information / Hessian**

$$-\partial^2\ell/\partial\beta\partial\beta' = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X}, \quad \mathbf{W} = \operatorname{diag}(m_i \pi_i(1-\pi_i))$$

This matrix measures the curvature of the log-likelihood, and its inverse gives the approximate
covariance matrix of the coefficient estimates.

**IRLS update**

$$\beta^{(t+1)} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{z}, \quad z_i = \eta_i + (Y_i - \mu_i)/w_i$$

Logistic regression has no closed-form solution, so software fits it by repeating a weighted
least squares step until the coefficients stop changing.

**Odds ratio**

$$\text{OR} = e^{c\beta_j} \text{ per } c \text{ units}$$

Exponentiating a logistic coefficient converts it from a log-odds change into a multiplicative
factor on the odds, the number reported and interpreted in practice.

**Wald statistic**

$$z_j = b_j / s\{b_j\} \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim} N(0,1)$$

This is the logistic regression analogue of the $t$ statistic, testing whether one coefficient is
zero using its estimate and standard error.

**Deviance**

$$D = 2[\ell_{\text{sat}} - \ell(\hat\beta)] = 2\sum_i [Y_i \log(Y_i/\hat\mu_i) + (m_i - Y_i)\log((m_i - Y_i)/(m_i - \hat\mu_i))]$$

Deviance measures how far the fitted model's likelihood falls short of a perfect fit, the
logistic regression analogue of $\mathrm{SSE}$.

**Likelihood-ratio statistic**

$$G^2 = D_{\text{reduced}} - D_{\text{full}} \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim} \chi^2_q$$

Comparing the deviance of two nested logistic models tests whether the extra predictors in the
larger model improve the fit, the logistic analogue of the $F$ test.

**Sensitivity**

$$\text{sens} = \text{TP}/(\text{TP} + \text{FN})$$

Sensitivity is the proportion of actual positive cases the model correctly flags.

**Specificity**

$$\text{spec} = \text{TN}/(\text{TN} + \text{FP})$$

Specificity is the proportion of actual negative cases the model correctly clears.

**AUC**

$$\text{AUC} = P(\hat\pi_{\text{pos}} > \hat\pi_{\text{neg}})$$

AUC is the probability that a randomly chosen positive case gets a higher predicted probability
than a randomly chosen negative case, a single number summarizing classification quality across
every threshold.

## A.14 Chapter 14: Poisson regression and the GLM idea

**Poisson distribution**

$$P(Y=y) = \frac{e^{-\mu}\mu^{y}}{y!}$$

This distribution models count data: the probability of observing exactly $y$ events when the
average rate is $\mu$.

**Poisson mean and variance**

$$E\{Y\} = \mu,\quad \operatorname{Var}\{Y\} = \mu$$

A defining feature of the Poisson distribution is that its variance always equals its mean, a
constraint Poisson regression inherits and later chapters relax.

**Poisson log-linear model**

$$Y_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu_i),\ \log\mu_i = \eta_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta$$

Poisson regression models counts by fitting a linear predictor to the log of the mean count,
which keeps predicted counts positive.

**Mean on response scale**

$$\mu_i = e^{\eta_i} = e^{\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}$$

Exponentiating the linear predictor converts it back to a predicted count, always positive by
construction.

**Rate ratio**

$$\frac{\mu(X_j+1)}{\mu(X_j)} = e^{\beta_j}$$

Adding one unit to a predictor multiplies the predicted count by $e^{\beta_j}$, the Poisson
regression analogue of an odds ratio.

**Poisson log-likelihood**

$$\ell(\boldsymbol\beta) = \sum_{i=1}^n [ y_i\,\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta - e^{\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta} - \log(y_i!) ]$$

Maximizing this function over $\boldsymbol\beta$ gives the Poisson regression coefficient
estimates.

**Poisson score equations**

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \mu_i)\mathbf{x}_i = \mathbf{0}$$

At the maximum likelihood estimate, the observed counts minus the fitted counts are uncorrelated
with every predictor, the same balancing property seen in Chapter 7 and Chapter 13.

**Offset (rate model)**

$$\log\mu_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta + \log t_i,\quad \mu_i = \lambda_i t_i$$

Adding a fixed offset term $\log t_i$ models a rate per unit of exposure $t_i$, so two
observations with different exposure times remain comparable.

**Pearson residual**

$$r_i^{P} = \frac{Y_i - \hat\mu_i}{\sqrt{\hat\mu_i}}$$

This residual rescales the raw count residual by the Poisson standard deviation, so residuals
across observations with different means are comparable.

**Estimated dispersion**

$$\hat\phi = \frac{1}{n-p}\sum_{i=1}^n (r_i^{P})^2 = \frac{X^2}{n-p}$$

This checks the Poisson mean-equals-variance assumption; a value well above 1 signals
overdispersion.

**Quasi-Poisson variance**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{Y_i\} = \phi\,\mu_i$$

This relaxes the strict Poisson assumption by allowing the variance to be a multiple $\phi$ of
the mean rather than exactly equal to it.

**Quasi-Poisson standard error**

$$s_{\text{quasi}}\{b_j\} = \sqrt{\hat\phi}\,s_{\text{Poisson}}\{b_j\}$$

Multiplying the ordinary Poisson standard errors by the square root of the dispersion estimate
corrects them for overdispersion.

**Negative binomial variance**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{Y\} = \mu + \mu^2/\theta$$

The negative binomial distribution lets variance grow faster than the mean, an alternative to
Poisson regression for more strongly overdispersed counts.

**Generalized linear model**

$$Y_i \sim \text{exponential family},\ \eta_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta,\ g(\mu_i) = \eta_i$$

The GLM framework unifies ordinary, logistic, and Poisson regression as one family: a
distribution for $Y$, a linear predictor, and a link function $g$ connecting them.

## A.15 Chapter 15: Regression with time: autocorrelation and forecasting

**Trend-plus-season model**

$$\log Y_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 t + \sum_{m=2}^{12}\gamma_m D_{mt} + \varepsilon_t$$

This model fits a straight-line trend on the log scale plus monthly indicator shifts, capturing
both growth and a repeating seasonal pattern.

**Monthly growth from log slope**

$$(e^{\beta_1}-1)\times 100\%$$

Exponentiating the trend slope in a log-scale time model converts it into a percent growth rate
per period.

**AR(1) error model**

$$\varepsilon_t = \rho\,\varepsilon_{t-1} + u_t,\ u_t \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0,\sigma_u^2),\ |\rho|<1$$

Each error depends on the previous one through the correlation $\rho$, plus fresh independent
noise, which is how consecutive residuals in time-ordered data end up related.

**Variance of mean under AR(1)**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{\bar Y\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\left(1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\left(1 - \frac{k}{n}\right)\rho^{k}\right)$$

Positive autocorrelation inflates the true variance of a sample mean well beyond the usual
$\sigma^2/n$, which is why ignoring autocorrelation makes standard errors too small.

**AR(1) error variance**

$$\operatorname{Var}\{\varepsilon_t\} = \sigma_u^2/(1-\rho^2)$$

The overall variance of an AR(1) error series depends on both the innovation variance and how
strongly errors carry over from one period to the next.

**AR(1) autocorrelation at lag $k$**

$$\operatorname{Corr}\{\varepsilon_t,\varepsilon_{t-k}\} = \rho^{k}$$

Correlation between errors $k$ periods apart decays geometrically, fading out as the gap grows.

**Durbin-Watson statistic**

$$D = \sum_{t=2}^{n}(e_t - e_{t-1})^2 \big/ \sum_{t=1}^{n} e_t^2$$

This is the same diagnostic introduced in Chapter 9, now the main tool for detecting
autocorrelation in a time-ordered regression.

**Durbin-Watson approximation**

$$D \approx 2(1 - r_1)$$

The Durbin-Watson statistic is approximately twice one minus the lag-one residual correlation,
which is why values near 2 indicate no autocorrelation.

**Lag-one residual autocorrelation**

$$r_1 = \sum_{t=2}^{n} e_t e_{t-1} \big/ \sum_{t=1}^{n} e_t^2$$

This estimates how strongly one residual predicts the next, the sample version of $\rho$.

**Cochrane-Orcutt $\rho$ estimate**

$$\hat\rho = \sum_{t=2}^{n} e_t e_{t-1} \big/ \sum_{t=2}^{n} e_{t-1}^2$$

This estimates the AR(1) correlation from ordinary least squares residuals as the first step of
a two-step correction procedure.

**Quasi-differencing transform**

$$Y_t - \rho Y_{t-1} = (\mathbf{x}_t - \rho\mathbf{x}_{t-1})'\boldsymbol\beta + u_t$$

Subtracting $\rho$ times the previous period from both response and predictors removes the
autocorrelation, leaving a model that ordinary least squares can fit correctly.

**Test root mean squared error**

$$\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n_{\text{test}}}\sum_{t\in\text{test}}(Y_t - \hat Y_t)^2}$$

This measures typical forecast error, in the original units, on data the model never saw during
fitting.

**Test mean absolute percentage error**

$$\mathrm{MAPE} = \frac{100\%}{n_{\text{test}}}\sum_{t\in\text{test}}\frac{|Y_t - \hat Y_t|}{Y_t}$$

This measures typical forecast error as a percentage of the actual value, useful for comparing
accuracy across series with different scales.

## A.16 Chapter 16: Path analysis and a look ahead

**Income equation (standardized)**

$$\text{income}^\ast = p_{IE}\,\text{education}^\ast + d_I$$

In standardized form, education's effect on income is a single path coefficient $p_{IE}$, plus a
disturbance $d_I$ for everything else that affects income.

**Prestige equation (standardized)**

$$\text{prestige}^\ast = p_{PE}\,\text{education}^\ast + p_{PI}\,\text{income}^\ast + d_P$$

Prestige depends on both education and income at once, each with its own standardized path
coefficient.

**Standardized (path) coefficient**

$$p_k = b_k\, s_{X_k}/s_Y$$

A path coefficient is an ordinary regression slope rescaled by the ratio of standard deviations,
the same standardization from Chapter 8 applied to a system of equations.

**Direct effect**

$$\text{direct} = p_{PE}$$

The direct effect of education on prestige is simply its own path coefficient in the prestige
equation, holding income fixed.

**Indirect effect**

$$\text{indirect} = p_{IE}\, p_{PI}$$

The indirect effect of education on prestige, routed through income, multiplies the two path
coefficients along that chain.

**Total effect**

$$\text{total} = p_{PE} + p_{IE}\, p_{PI}$$

Adding the direct and indirect effects gives education's total influence on prestige, direct plus
everything that flows through income.

**Total effect equals correlation (exogenous cause)**

$$r_{\text{education},\text{prestige}} = p_{PE} + p_{PI}\, p_{IE}$$

For a variable with no causes inside the diagram, its simple correlation with an outcome equals
the sum of every path connecting them, a check on the whole path model.

**Correlation decomposition (income, prestige)**

$$r_{\text{income},\text{prestige}} = p_{PI} + p_{IE}\, p_{PE}$$

Income and prestige's correlation splits into income's direct path to prestige plus a piece
shared through their common cause, education.

**Reduced form of prestige**

$$\text{prestige}^\ast = (p_{PE} + p_{PI}p_{IE})\text{education}^\ast + (p_{PI}d_I + d_P)$$

Substituting the income equation into the prestige equation collapses the two-step path diagram
into education's total effect on prestige in a single equation.

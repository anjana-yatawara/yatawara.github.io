We return to the Dwaine Studios cities of @ch06. The company runs a chain of
portrait studios in mid-sized cities, the kind of place families visit for
graduation and holiday photos, and it wants a rule for guessing how much a new
studio will sell before it signs a lease. Two facts about a city are easy to look
up in advance: the number of young people (population aged 16 or younger, in
thousands) and the per-capita disposable income (in thousands of dollars). The
question is whether those two numbers together predict annual studio sales well
enough to guide the expansion.

In Chapter 6 you already assembled this fit by hand: you built the design matrix,
formed $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$, inverted the small
matrix, and solved for the coefficients $\mathbf{b}$. What that arithmetic could
not tell you is why the recipe $\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$
is the right one, or how far to trust the numbers it returns. This chapter proves
why, and turns the fit into inference. @fig-ch07-dwaine-scatter shows the raw
picture: sales climb with each predictor on its own, but neither plot alone tells
you what the two predictors do together, and that "together" is the whole point of
multiple regression.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_dwaine_scatter.png
:name: fig-ch07-dwaine-scatter
:alt: Two side-by-side scatterplots for the 21 Dwaine Studios cities. One panel plots sales in thousands of dollars against disposable income in thousand dollars; the other plots the same sales against target population in thousands. Each panel has an upward-sloping fitted line, and both clouds of points trend clearly upward, with sales ranging from roughly 140 to 290 thousand dollars.
Dwaine Studios sales rise with both the young-population count and disposable income when each is viewed alone. Multiple regression asks a harder question: how much does each predictor add once the other is already in the model?
```

Simple linear regression, from Chapters 2 and 3, handles one predictor. Real
questions almost always come with several. We could try to rebuild the whole
theory predictor by predictor, but that path gets ugly fast: two predictors, then
three, each with its own normal equations and variance formulas. Instead we do
what @ch06 set up. We write the model once, in matrix form, so that one predictor
or ten predictors look identical on the page. Every result from Chapter 2, the
least-squares estimates, their unbiasedness, their variances, the Gauss-Markov
optimality, the $n-2$ divisor, reappears here in a single clean form that works
for any number of predictors at once. This chapter is where regression stops being
a collection of special cases and becomes one general method.

This is the most abstract chapter in the course, and worth a word on how to read
it. The geometry in @ch07-geometry is the idea to hold onto: once you picture
fitting as dropping a perpendicular from the data onto the space of possible fits,
every proof that follows reduces to one of two facts about the hat matrix. Hold
that picture and the algebra becomes bookkeeping.

:::{admonition} This lesson at a glance
:class: important
- **What we are doing:** Turning Chapter 6's matrix arithmetic into a working multiple regression: deriving the least-squares estimator, its sampling distribution, and the Gauss-Markov theorem in matrix form.
- **Why we are doing it:** Real questions come with several predictors, and rebuilding the theory one predictor at a time gets ugly; writing the model once in matrix form makes one predictor and ten look identical on the page.
- **Main objective:** Derive $\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ by calculus and by projection, prove the hat-matrix and Gauss-Markov results, and recover every Chapter 2 formula as the $p = 2$ special case.
- **What changed from the last chapters:** Chapter 6 supplied the matrix tools but stopped at the arithmetic; this chapter uses them to redo all of Chapter 2's theory at once for any number of predictors, fitting the Dwaine model whose $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ you already built (@ch06).
:::

:::{admonition} Learning objectives
:class: tip
By the end of this chapter you will be able to:
- **Write** any linear regression as $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ and identify the design matrix, coefficient vector, and error vector.
- **Derive** the least-squares estimator $\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ two ways: by calculus and by orthogonal projection.
- **Interpret** least squares geometrically as projecting $\mathbf{Y}$ onto the column space of $\mathbf{X}$, with the residual perpendicular to that space.
- **Prove** that the hat matrix $\mathbf{H}$ is symmetric and idempotent with trace $p$, and use it to write fitted values and residuals.
- **Derive** the mean and covariance of $\mathbf{b}$ and state its sampling distribution under normal errors.
- **Prove** that $s^2 = \mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n-p)$ is unbiased for $\sigma^2$, using the trace of $\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H}$.
- **State and prove** the Gauss-Markov theorem in matrix form: $\mathbf{b}$ is the best linear unbiased estimator.
- **Recover** every simple-regression formula from Chapter 2 as the special case $p = 2$.
:::

## 7.1 The model and least squares in matrix form

### Intuition

In Chapter 2 a single predictor gave one slope and one intercept. Add a second
predictor and you have two slopes plus an intercept; the mean response is now a
tilted plane instead of a line, as @fig-ch07-reg-plane shows for Dwaine. With more
predictors it is a flat sheet in a space you cannot picture. Writing separate
symbols for every coefficient and separate sums for every normal equation would
bury the idea under bookkeeping. The fix is to stack the observations into vectors
and matrices and let one line of matrix algebra carry all the bookkeeping for us.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_reg_plane.png
:name: fig-ch07-reg-plane
:alt: A three-dimensional scatterplot of the 21 Dwaine cities. The floor axes are target population in thousands and disposable income in thousand dollars; the vertical axis is sales in thousands of dollars. A translucent blue plane tilts upward across the box, and each green data point is joined to the plane by a short dashed vertical stem, the residual. Points sit above and below the plane with no systematic gap.
With one predictor the fit is a line; with two it is a tilted plane. Each city's sales is a green dot, the plane is the model's guess, and each dashed stem is a residual. Multiple regression finds the plane whose stems are smallest in total squared length.
```

The systematic part of each observation is a weighted sum of that city's predictor
values, the same weights ($\beta_0, \beta_1, \dots$) reused for every city. Stacking
the cities vertically turns "the same weights applied to every row" into a single
matrix-times-vector product. @fig-ch07-design-matrix shows the four objects and
their shapes.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_design_matrix.png
:name: fig-ch07-design-matrix
:alt: A schematic of the equation Y equals X beta plus epsilon drawn as four labeled boxes. A tall green n-by-1 box of responses Y1 through Yn equals a wide blue n-by-p design matrix X whose first column is all ones and whose other columns hold predictor values, times a purple p-by-1 box of coefficients beta-0 through beta-p-minus-1, plus a red n-by-1 box of errors epsilon-1 through epsilon-n.
The general linear model as stacked arrays. The design matrix X carries a leading column of ones so that the intercept beta-0 is handled exactly like any other coefficient.
```

### Formula

The **general linear model** (Definition 7.1) collects all $n$ observations into one
matrix equation.

:::{admonition} Definition 7.1: General linear model
:class: note definition
The **general linear model** for $n$ observations and $p$ parameters is

$$
\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon},
$$

where $\mathbf{Y}$ is the $n \times 1$ vector of responses, $\mathbf{X}$ the
$n \times p$ design matrix, $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ the $p \times 1$ vector of
coefficients, and $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ the $n \times 1$ vector of random errors.
:::

In words: each observation's response equals a weighted sum of that observation's
predictor values plus a random error, and the same weights are reused for every row.
The four objects are:

- $\mathbf{Y}$ is the $n \times 1$ vector of responses, one entry per observation.
- $\mathbf{X}$ is the $n \times p$ **design matrix** (Definition 7.2). Its first column is all ones (for the intercept); each remaining column holds one predictor's values. Row $i$ is city $i$'s data.
- $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is the $p \times 1$ vector of unknown parameters $(\beta_0, \beta_1, \dots, \beta_{p-1})'$.
- $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ is the $n \times 1$ vector of random errors.
- $p$ is the number of parameters, counting the intercept; with $k$ predictors, $p = k + 1$. Here $n = 21$ and $p = 3$.

:::{admonition} Definition 7.2: Design matrix
:class: note definition
The **design matrix** $\mathbf{X}$ is the $n \times p$ matrix whose first column is
all ones (carrying the intercept) and whose remaining columns each hold one
predictor's values, so row $i$ is observation $i$'s predictor data.
:::

The error assumptions of Chapter 2 become two compact statements about the whole
vector at once:

$$
E\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \mathbf{0}, \qquad
\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I} .
$$

In words: every error averages to zero, and the covariance matrix is $\sigma^2$
times the identity. That one symbol packs both assumptions at once: each error has
the same variance $\sigma^2$ (down the diagonal), and distinct errors are
uncorrelated (off the diagonal). Because $\mathbf{X}$ and $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ are
fixed, $E\{\mathbf{Y}\} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}$ and
$\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{Y}\} = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}$ as well. We assume
throughout that $\mathbf{X}$ has full column rank $p$: no predictor is a copy of
another, or an exact linear combination of the others. That is exactly what makes
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ invertible (@ch06-inverse).

To fit the model we reuse the least-squares idea from @ch02-least-squares: pick the
coefficient vector $\mathbf{b}$ that makes the fitted values closest to the data in
total squared error. Writing a candidate vector as $\mathbf{b}$, the sum of squared
errors is

$$
Q(\mathbf{b}) = \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{b})^2
= (\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b})'(\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}) ,
$$

where $\mathbf{x}_i'$ is row $i$ of $\mathbf{X}$. In words: $Q$ is the squared length
of the residual vector, the same quantity Chapter 2 minimized, now written as one
dot product. The minimizer is the **least-squares estimator** (Theorem 7.3)

$$
\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} .
$$

In words: gather the cross-products of the predictors ($\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$) and
of the predictors with the response ($\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$), and multiply by the
inverse of the first. We derive this next, then check it on Dwaine.

### Derivation by calculus

:::{admonition} Theorem 7.3: The least-squares estimator
:class: important theorem
Under the general linear model with $\mathbf{X}$ of full column rank, the sum of
squared errors $Q(\mathbf{b})$ has a unique minimizer, the solution of the normal
equations $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$:

$$
\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} .
$$
:::

**Proof.** Expand $Q$ using the transpose
rules of @ch06-matrix-mult. Since $\mathbf{b}'\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ is a scalar, it
equals its own transpose $\mathbf{Y}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$, so the two middle terms
combine:

$$
Q(\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{Y}'\mathbf{Y} - 2\,\mathbf{b}'\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}
+ \mathbf{b}'\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} .
$$

We minimize by taking the gradient with respect to $\mathbf{b}$ and setting it to
zero. The gradient is just the vector of partial derivatives, one per entry of
$\mathbf{b}$, and setting it to zero finds the bottom of the bowl. Two
vector-derivative rules do the work: for a constant vector $\mathbf{a}$,
$\nabla_{\mathbf{b}}(\mathbf{a}'\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{a}$; and for a symmetric matrix
$\mathbf{M}$, $\nabla_{\mathbf{b}}(\mathbf{b}'\mathbf{M}\mathbf{b}) = 2\mathbf{M}\mathbf{b}$.
These are the vector twins of the scalar rules $\frac{d}{db}(ab) = a$ and
$\frac{d}{db}(mb^2) = 2mb$ you already know. The matrix $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is
symmetric, so

$$
\nabla_{\mathbf{b}} Q = -2\,\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} + 2\,\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} .
$$

Setting the gradient to zero and dividing by 2 gives the **normal equations**
(Definition 7.4)

$$
\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} .
$$

Because $\mathbf{X}$ has full column rank, $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is invertible
(@ch06-inverse), so we may multiply on the left by
$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$:

$$
\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} .
$$

This stationary point is the global minimum, not a saddle or maximum, because the
matrix of second derivatives of $Q$ is $2\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$, which is positive
definite; a quadratic with a positive-definite Hessian is strictly convex and has a
single lowest point. $\blacksquare$

:::{admonition} Definition 7.4: Normal equations
:class: note definition
The **normal equations** are the linear system

$$
\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y},
$$

whose solution is the least-squares estimator. Equivalently, they state that the
residual $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$ is orthogonal to every
column of $\mathbf{X}$.
:::

These are the same normal equations as Chapter 2, now stated once for any number of
predictors. In @ch02-least-squares the two scalar normal equations were
$\sum e_i = 0$ and $\sum X_i e_i = 0$; the single matrix equation
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ contains both of those and
one more for each extra predictor, as we will see in @ch07-slr-special.

(ch07-geometry)=
## 7.2 The geometry of least squares

### Intuition

The calculus derivation finds the answer but hides why it is the answer. There is a
picture that makes least squares obvious, and it is worth carrying for the rest of
the course. Think of the response $\mathbf{Y}$ as a single arrow (a point) in
$n$-dimensional space, one axis per observation. As the coefficient vector
$\mathbf{b}$ ranges over all possible values, the fitted vector
$\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$ traces out a flat subspace: every linear
combination of the columns of $\mathbf{X}$. This subspace is the **column space** of
$\mathbf{X}$ (Definition 7.5), and it holds every prediction the model can possibly make.

:::{admonition} Definition 7.5: Column space
:class: note definition
The **column space** of $\mathbf{X}$ is the set of all vectors $\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$
as $\mathbf{b}$ ranges over every coefficient vector: a $p$-dimensional flat subspace
of $n$-dimensional space that holds every fitted vector the model can produce.
:::

The data point $\mathbf{Y}$ almost never sits exactly in that subspace, because real
data has noise. So the fitting problem is: which point in the column space is closest
to $\mathbf{Y}$? The closest point on a flat surface to an outside point is the foot
of the perpendicular dropped from the point to the surface. That foot is
$\hat{\mathbf{Y}}$, and the perpendicular segment is the residual vector
$\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \hat{\mathbf{Y}}$. @fig-ch07-projection draws exactly this
for the smallest case that fits on a page, three observations and two columns.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_projection.png
:name: fig-ch07-projection
:alt: A three-dimensional diagram. A tilted translucent blue plane is the column space of X, spanned by two dark arrows labeled columns of X. A green arrow labeled Y points from the origin up out of the plane. A blue arrow labeled Y-hat equals HY lies in the plane. A red dashed segment labeled e equals Y minus Y-hat connects the tip of Y-hat up to the tip of Y and meets the plane at a right angle, marked by a small red square.
Least squares as projection. The fitted vector Y-hat is the point of the column space closest to Y, reached by dropping a perpendicular; the residual e is that perpendicular, meeting the column space at a right angle.
```

### Formula

The geometric condition is that the residual is orthogonal to every column of
$\mathbf{X}$:

$$
\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}, \qquad \text{where } \mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} .
$$

In words: each column of $\mathbf{X}$, dotted with the residual vector, gives zero, so
the residual points in a direction the model cannot represent. Expanding
$\mathbf{X}'(\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{0}$ gives back the normal
equations $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$, so the geometry
and the calculus lead to the identical solution.

### Derivation by projection

**Derivation (least squares as orthogonal projection).** Let $\mathbf{b}$ solve the
normal equations, so $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$ with
$\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$. Take any other candidate coefficient
vector $\mathbf{b}^{\ast}$, with fitted vector $\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}^{\ast}$. Split the
gap from $\mathbf{Y}$ to that candidate by routing through $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$:

$$
\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}^{\ast}
= \underbrace{(\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b})}_{\mathbf{e}}
+ \underbrace{\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}^{\ast})}_{\mathbf{g}} .
$$

The second piece $\mathbf{g} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}^{\ast})$ lives in the
column space. Its squared length adds without a cross term, because the cross term
vanishes by orthogonality:

$$
\mathbf{e}'\mathbf{g} = \mathbf{e}'\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}^{\ast})
= (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e})'(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}^{\ast}) = \mathbf{0}'(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}^{\ast}) = 0 .
$$

Therefore, by the Pythagorean theorem in $n$ dimensions,

$$
\|\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}^{\ast}\|^2
= \|\mathbf{e}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{g}\|^2 \; \ge \; \|\mathbf{e}\|^2 ,
$$

with equality only when $\|\mathbf{g}\|^2 = 0$, that is
$\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}^{\ast} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$. Since $\mathbf{X}$ has full column
rank, this forces $\mathbf{b}^{\ast} = \mathbf{b}$. So the normal-equations solution
gives the unique smallest sum of squared errors, and $\hat{\mathbf{Y}}$ is the closest
point of the column space to $\mathbf{Y}$. $\blacksquare$

This is the same algebraic identity as the Chapter 2 proof in @ch02-least-squares,
now read as geometry: the cross term vanishes there because $\sum e_i = 0$ and
$\sum X_i e_i = 0$, which is precisely $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$ written
out. The projection view (in @ch06-projection) is the one to remember, because
diagnostics in Chapter 9 are all about which observations sit in awkward corners of
the column space.

### R and Python

:::{admonition} Example 7.2: The residual is orthogonal to the predictors
:class: note
**Question.** Do the Dwaine residuals really satisfy $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$?

**Intuition.** Compute the fitted vector $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$ and
the residual $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \hat{\mathbf{Y}}$, then form
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e}$. Each of its three entries should be zero (up to
floating-point dust), one per column of $\mathbf{X}$.

**Formula.** $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$.

**Computation.**

```r
n <- nrow(X)
p <- ncol(X)
yhat <- X %*% b
e <- Y - yhat
round(t(X) %*% e, 6)          # should be the zero vector
```
```text
     [,1]
[1,]    0
[2,]    0
[3,]    0
```

```python
n, p = X.shape
yhat = X @ b
e = Y - yhat
print(np.round(X.T @ e, 6))   # should be the zero vector
```
```text
[0. 0. 0.]
```

**Interpretation.** All three dot products are zero. The first (from the column of
ones) is $\sum e_i = 0$; the other two say the residuals are uncorrelated with each
predictor. The residual vector carries exactly the part of $\mathbf{Y}$ that the
predictors cannot explain, which is why plotting it against the fitted values is the
first diagnostic we draw, in @fig-ch07-residual-fitted.
:::

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_residual_fitted.png
:name: fig-ch07-residual-fitted
:alt: A scatterplot of the 21 Dwaine residuals in thousands of dollars on the vertical axis against fitted sales on the horizontal axis, with a dashed horizontal line at zero. The points scatter above and below zero with no funnel shape and no bend.
The Dwaine residuals against fitted values form a flat, patternless band around zero, the picture that supports the constant-variance and linearity assumptions. Chapter 9 turns this habit into a full diagnostic toolkit.
```

:::{admonition} Durable skill: See a fit as a projection
:class: tip
Once you picture fitting as dropping a perpendicular from the data onto the space of
possible fits, a lot of regression stops needing memorization. The residual is
orthogonal to the fit because the perpendicular is the shortest route. Adding a
predictor enlarges the column space, so the residual can only get shorter. Two
predictors that point in nearly the same direction span a thin sliver of space, which
is why their coefficients are unstable. You will meet this picture again in the
leverage values (Chapter 9), in added-variable plots (@ch08-avplots), and in any method that decomposes data into
"fit plus leftover." To practice, next time you fit a model, ask what space the fit
lives in and what direction the residual points.
:::

## 7.3 The hat matrix

### Intuition

Section 7.2 pictured the fit as a projection of $\mathbf{Y}$ onto the column space.
That projection is carried out by a single matrix, and giving it a name pays off for
the rest of the chapter. The fitted vector is a fixed linear function of the data:
plug the formula for $\mathbf{b}$ into $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{b}$ and you get
$\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$. The
matrix sitting in front of $\mathbf{Y}$ turns the raw responses into fitted values,
so it earns the name **hat matrix** (Definition 7.6): it puts the hat on $\mathbf{Y}$. It is the single
most useful matrix in the rest of the course. Its diagonal entries are the leverages
that flag unusual predictor values (Chapter 9), and its algebra makes the variance
results below fall out in a line.

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 7.6: Hat matrix
:class: note definition
The **hat matrix** is the $n \times n$ matrix

$$
\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}',
$$

which depends only on the predictors and maps the responses to the fitted values,
$\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$: it puts the hat on $\mathbf{Y}$.
:::

The hat matrix and the fitted and residual vectors are

$$
\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}', \qquad
\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}, \qquad
\mathbf{e} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y} .
$$

- $\mathbf{H}$ is $n \times n$ and depends only on the predictors, not on $\mathbf{Y}$.
- $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$: each fitted value is a fixed weighted average of all the responses, with weights read off a row of $\mathbf{H}$.
- $\mathbf{e} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}$: the residual is what is left after projecting, so $\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}$ is itself a projection, onto the space perpendicular to the columns of $\mathbf{X}$.

@fig-ch07-hat-heatmap shows the Toluca hat matrix as a grid of weights; the diagonal
entries, outlined, are the leverages $h_{ii}$.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_hat_heatmap.png
:name: fig-ch07-hat-heatmap
:alt: A 25-by-25 heatmap of the Toluca hat matrix, with red for positive entries and blue for negative, most entries near zero. The 25 diagonal cells are outlined in green, marking the leverage values h-i-i, which are the largest entries in each row.
The Toluca hat matrix H turns responses into fitted values. Reading across row i gives the weights that build the i-th fitted value; the outlined green diagonal entries are the leverage values that Chapter 9 uses to find unusual observations.
```

### Derivation (properties of H)

:::{admonition} Theorem 7.7: Properties of the hat matrix
:class: important theorem
For a design matrix $\mathbf{X}$ of full column rank, the hat matrix
$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$ is symmetric
($\mathbf{H}' = \mathbf{H}$) and idempotent ($\mathbf{H}\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}$),
with $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p$. Consequently $\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}$ is
also symmetric and idempotent, with $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = n - p$.
:::

**Proof.** Write
$\mathbf{A} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$, which is symmetric because
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is (the inverse of a symmetric matrix is symmetric, by
@ch06-inverse).

*Symmetric.* Using $(\mathbf{BCD})' = \mathbf{D}'\mathbf{C}'\mathbf{B}'$ from
@ch06-matrix-mult,

$$
\mathbf{H}' = \big(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{X}'\big)' = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{A}'\mathbf{X}' = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{X}' = \mathbf{H} .
$$

*Idempotent.* Projecting twice equals projecting once. The middle
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ cancels its inverse:

$$
\mathbf{H}\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\,\mathbf{A}\mathbf{X}'
= \mathbf{X}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{X}' = \mathbf{H} .
$$

@fig-ch07-idempotent shows why: once $\mathbf{H}$ has landed a vector in the column
space, projecting again cannot move it.

*Trace $p$.* The trace of a matrix is the sum of its diagonal entries. Use the cyclic property of the trace, $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{B}\mathbf{C}) = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{C}\mathbf{B})$ (a standard identity: both sides equal $\sum_{i,j} B_{ij} C_{ji}$), to slide $\mathbf{X}$ around:

$$
\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = \operatorname{tr}\!\big(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{X}'\big)
= \operatorname{tr}\!\big(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\big)
= \operatorname{tr}\!\big((\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})\big)
= \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I}_p) = p .
$$

Consequently $\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}$ is also symmetric and idempotent, with
$\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = n - p$. That trace is the degrees of
freedom for error, and it is the reason the divisor in the variance estimate is
$n - p$, proved in @ch07-sigma2. $\blacksquare$

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_idempotent.png
:name: fig-ch07-idempotent
:alt: A two-dimensional diagram. A blue line through the origin is labeled the column space. A green arrow y points up and to the right; a blue arrow Hy lies along the line; a red dashed segment e connects the tip of Hy to the tip of y, meeting the line at a right angle. A small annotation notes that projecting Hy a second time does not move it.
Why H is idempotent. Projecting the vector y onto the column space lands it at Hy inside that space; projecting a second time leaves it exactly where it is, so HH equals H.
```

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
Almost every result left in this chapter rests on two facts about the hat matrix: it
is a projection (symmetric and idempotent), and its trace counts the parameters, so
$\operatorname{tr}\mathbf{H} = p$ and $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = n - p$.
The first fact makes the fitted values and residuals behave; the second fixes the
divisor in the variance estimate. Hold those two and the rest is bookkeeping.
:::

## 7.4 The sampling distribution of b

### Intuition

The vector $\mathbf{b}$ came from one sample of 21 cities. A different 21 cities would
give a slightly different $\mathbf{b}$, so $\mathbf{b}$ is random, with a mean and a
covariance across the samples we could have drawn. Two questions repeat from Chapter
2, now for a whole vector: is $\mathbf{b}$ right on average, and how much does it vary?
The answers are clean because $\mathbf{b}$ is a fixed matrix times $\mathbf{Y}$, and
@ch06-random-vectors tells us exactly how means and covariances pass through a linear
map.

### Formula

The least-squares vector is unbiased with a covariance matrix built from
$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$:

$$
E\{\mathbf{b}\} = \boldsymbol{\beta}, \qquad
\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} .
$$

In words: on average $\mathbf{b}$ hits the true coefficient vector, and its
covariance matrix is $\sigma^2$ times the inverse cross-product matrix. The diagonal
entries are the variances of the individual coefficients; the off-diagonal entries
are the covariances between them, which is why two coefficients can be correlated
(the tilted cloud in @fig-ch07-coef-ellipse). Replacing $\sigma^2$ by its estimate
$\mathrm{MSE}$ gives the **estimated covariance matrix**
$s^2\{\mathbf{b}\} = \mathrm{MSE}\,(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$, whose diagonal square
roots are the standard errors software prints.

### Derivation (mean and covariance of b)

:::{admonition} Theorem 7.8: Mean and covariance of the least-squares estimator
:class: important theorem
Under the general linear model with $E\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \mathbf{0}$ and
$\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$, the least-squares
estimator is unbiased with covariance

$$
E\{\mathbf{b}\} = \boldsymbol{\beta}, \qquad
\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} .
$$

If in addition $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim N(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2\mathbf{I})$, then
$\mathbf{b} \sim N\!\big(\boldsymbol{\beta},\ \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\big)$.
:::

**Proof.** Write
$\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}$ with the fixed matrix
$\mathbf{A} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$. For a fixed matrix times a
random vector, @ch06-random-vectors gives $E\{\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}\} = \mathbf{A}\,E\{\mathbf{Y}\}$
and $\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}\} = \mathbf{A}\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{Y}\}\mathbf{A}'$.

*Mean.* Since $E\{\mathbf{Y}\} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}$,

$$
E\{\mathbf{b}\} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\,\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
= (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})\boldsymbol{\beta} = \boldsymbol{\beta} .
$$

*Covariance.* Since $\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{Y}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$,

$$
\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\} = \mathbf{A}\,(\sigma^2\mathbf{I})\,\mathbf{A}'
= \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\,\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}
= \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} ,
$$

where the middle $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ cancels one inverse and the symmetry of
$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$ handles the transpose. $\blacksquare$

If we add the normality assumption $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim N(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2\mathbf{I})$,
then $\mathbf{Y}$ is multivariate normal, and a fixed linear map of a multivariate
normal is again multivariate normal (@ch06-mvn). So

$$
\mathbf{b} \sim N\!\big(\boldsymbol{\beta},\ \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\big) .
$$

In words: under normal errors each coefficient is normally distributed around its
true value, and this is what powers the $t$ tests and confidence intervals of Chapter
8. @fig-ch07-coef-ellipse confirms the covariance by simulation: refitting many
datasets scatters the two Dwaine slopes into a tilted ellipse that matches
$\sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_coef_ellipse.png
:name: fig-ch07-coef-ellipse
:alt: A scatterplot of 4000 simulated pairs of slope estimates, the target-population slope b1 on the horizontal axis and the disposable-income slope b2 on the vertical axis. The cloud is an elongated tilted ellipse sloping downward from upper left to lower right, centered near b1 equals 1.45 and b2 equals 9.4. A red curve traces the theoretical 95 percent ellipse and matches the cloud.
Simulating 4000 refits scatters the two Dwaine slope estimates into a tilted ellipse. The downward tilt is a negative covariance: the two slope estimates trade off, because the predictors are correlated. The red theoretical ellipse from sigma-squared times the inverse cross-product matrix matches the simulated cloud.
```

(ch07-sigma2)=
## 7.5 Estimating the error variance

### Intuition

The coefficients describe the plane; the model still has one more unknown, the error
variance $\sigma^2$, which sets how far the data scatter off the plane. As in Chapter
2 we estimate it from the residuals, but now we must find the right divisor for a
model with $p$ parameters instead of $2$. The answer is $n - p$, and the reason is
the trace we computed in @ch07-hat-matrix: fitting $p$ coefficients uses up $p$
directions of the data, leaving $n - p$ directions for the residual to live in.
@fig-ch07-df-split keeps the bookkeeping in view: the $n$ directions of the data
split into a fit part and a residual part, and we average the squared residual over
its own $n - p$ directions, not all $n$.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_df_split.png
:name: fig-ch07-df-split
:alt: A horizontal bar of total length n equals 21 representing the directions of the data. A short blue segment of length p equals 3 on the left is labeled spent on the fit, the column space. A long red segment of length n minus p equals 18 on the right is labeled left for the residual, the error space. A double arrow beneath the whole bar marks the total of 21 directions of data.
The n directions of the data split into p directions spent fitting the plane and n minus p left for the residual. Dividing SSE by n minus p, not by n, averages over exactly the directions the residual is free to fill, which is what makes MSE unbiased.
```

### Formula

The **error sum of squares** and the estimator of $\sigma^2$ are

$$
\mathrm{SSE} = \mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y}'(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}, \qquad
s^2 = \mathrm{MSE} = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}}{n - p}, \qquad s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}} .
$$

- $\mathrm{SSE}$ is the squared length of the residual vector, the variation the model did not explain.
- $\mathrm{MSE}$ divides $\mathrm{SSE}$ by its degrees of freedom $n - p$, and estimates $\sigma^2$.
- $s$ is the estimated error standard deviation, in the units of $Y$.

### Derivation (why the divisor is n - p)

:::{admonition} Theorem 7.9: Unbiasedness of MSE
:class: important theorem
Under the general linear model with
$\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$ and $\mathbf{X}$
of full column rank,

$$
E\{\mathrm{SSE}\} = (n - p)\sigma^2, \qquad \text{so} \qquad E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \sigma^2 .
$$

The divisor $n - p$, and no other, makes $\mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n - p)$ an
unbiased estimator of the error variance $\sigma^2$.
:::

We need one fact about the expected value of a quadratic form. It is provable at our
level, so we prove it, then apply it.

**Lemma (expectation of a quadratic form).** For any random vector $\mathbf{Y}$ with
mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, and any fixed symmetric
matrix $\mathbf{A}$,

$$
E\{\mathbf{Y}'\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}\} = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) + \boldsymbol{\mu}'\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu} .
$$

*Proof.* The scalar $\mathbf{Y}'\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}$ equals its own trace, and the
trace is cyclic, so $\mathbf{Y}'\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y} = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}\mathbf{Y}')$.
Taking expectations and using $E\{\mathbf{Y}\mathbf{Y}'\} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma} + \boldsymbol{\mu}\boldsymbol{\mu}'$ (@ch06-random-vectors),

$$
E\{\mathbf{Y}'\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}\} = \operatorname{tr}\!\big(\mathbf{A}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} + \boldsymbol{\mu}\boldsymbol{\mu}')\big)
= \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) + \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu}\boldsymbol{\mu}')
= \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) + \boldsymbol{\mu}'\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu} ,
$$

since $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu}\boldsymbol{\mu}') = \boldsymbol{\mu}'\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu}$ is a scalar. $\blacksquare$

**Proof of Theorem 7.9.** Apply the lemma with
$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}$, $\boldsymbol{\mu} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}$,
and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$. The two pieces are:

*Trace piece.* $\operatorname{tr}\!\big((\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\sigma^2\mathbf{I}\big) = \sigma^2 \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = \sigma^2 (n - p)$, using the trace from @ch07-hat-matrix.

*Mean piece.* The mean term vanishes, because $(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{X} - \mathbf{H}\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{X} - \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{0}$ (the hat matrix leaves the columns of $\mathbf{X}$ unchanged, since they already lie in the column space). Therefore

$$
\boldsymbol{\mu}'(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\boldsymbol{\mu} = \boldsymbol{\beta}'\mathbf{X}'(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} = \boldsymbol{\beta}'\mathbf{0}\,\boldsymbol{\beta} = 0 .
$$

Combining, $E\{\mathrm{SSE}\} = \sigma^2(n - p) + 0 = (n - p)\sigma^2$, so

$$
E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \frac{E\{\mathrm{SSE}\}}{n - p} = \sigma^2 .
$$

The divisor $n - p$, and no other, makes $\mathrm{MSE}$ unbiased. $\blacksquare$

This is the Chapter 2 result $E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \sigma^2$ generalized: there $p = 2$
gave the divisor $n - 2$; here any model divides by $n$ minus the number of
parameters. @fig-ch07-np-divisor shows what goes wrong with the naive divisor $n$:
dividing by $n$ instead of $n - p$ biases the estimate low, and the gap is largest
when $p$ is a big fraction of $n$.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_np_divisor.png
:name: fig-ch07-np-divisor
:alt: Two overlaid histograms of 5000 simulated estimates of sigma-squared for the Dwaine model. The blue histogram, dividing SSE by n minus p, is centered on the true value near 121. The orange histogram, dividing by n, is shifted left and centered near 104, biased low. A red dashed vertical line marks the true sigma-squared at 121.
Dividing SSE by n - p (blue) centers the variance estimate on the truth; dividing by n (orange) pulls it systematically low. The bias is the price of estimating p coefficients before measuring the residuals.
```

The two predictors together explain about $91.7\%$ of the variation in Dwaine sales.
Chapter 8 unpacks this number, warns about how it can only rise when predictors are

(ch07-gauss-markov)=
## 7.6 The Gauss-Markov theorem

### Intuition

We chose least squares because squared error is a natural penalty, and Chapter 2
rewarded that choice: in simple regression, no other linear unbiased estimator beats
$b_1$. The same reward holds for the full vector, and the matrix proof is barely
longer than the scalar one. The claim is that any competitor built as a fixed linear
combination of the responses, and required to be right on average, must have a
covariance matrix at least as large as $\mathbf{b}$'s. No normality is needed; the
mean-zero, constant-variance, uncorrelated error assumptions are enough.
@fig-ch07-gauss-markov shows the payoff on Toluca: least squares and a competing
unbiased estimator are both centered on the truth, but least squares scatters far
less.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_gauss_markov.png
:name: fig-ch07-gauss-markov
:alt: Two overlaid histograms of 5000 simulated Toluca slope estimates. The blue histogram, least squares, is tall and narrow and centered on the true slope 3.5702. The orange histogram, a two-point competitor using only the lightest and heaviest lots, is short and wide, also centered near 3.5702 but far more spread out. A red dashed line marks the true slope.
Both the least-squares slope (blue, narrow) and a two-point competitor (orange, wide) are unbiased, centered on the true slope. Least squares has the smaller spread, exactly what the Gauss-Markov theorem guarantees.
```

### Formula

:::{admonition} Theorem 7.10: Gauss-Markov theorem
:class: important theorem
Under the model $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$
with $E\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \mathbf{0}$ and
$\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$, and $\mathbf{X}$
of full column rank, the least-squares estimator
$\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ is the **best linear
unbiased estimator** of $\boldsymbol{\beta}$: for any fixed vector $\mathbf{c}$, the
estimator $\mathbf{c}'\mathbf{b}$ has the smallest variance among all estimators that
are linear in $\mathbf{Y}$ and unbiased for $\mathbf{c}'\boldsymbol{\beta}$.
:::

### Derivation (b is BLUE)

**Proof.** Let $\mathbf{b}^{\ast} = \mathbf{C}\mathbf{Y}$ be any linear estimator
with a fixed $p \times n$ matrix $\mathbf{C}$. Its mean is
$E\{\mathbf{C}\mathbf{Y}\} = \mathbf{C}\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}$. For
$\mathbf{b}^{\ast}$ to be unbiased for $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ for every possible
$\boldsymbol{\beta}$, we need

$$
\mathbf{C}\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{I}_p .
$$

Write $\mathbf{C} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}' + \mathbf{D}$, defining
$\mathbf{D}$ as the difference from the least-squares matrix. The unbiasedness
condition $\mathbf{C}\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{I}$ becomes

$$
\mathbf{I} = \big[(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}' + \mathbf{D}\big]\mathbf{X}
= \mathbf{I} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{X}, \qquad \text{so} \qquad \mathbf{D}\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{0} .
$$

Now compute the covariance. Since $\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{Y}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$,
$\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}^{\ast}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{C}\mathbf{C}'$. Expand
$\mathbf{C}\mathbf{C}'$ with $\mathbf{C} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}' + \mathbf{D}$.
The two cross terms vanish because $\mathbf{D}\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{0}$ (and its
transpose $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{D}' = \mathbf{0}$):

$$
(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{D}' = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}(\mathbf{D}\mathbf{X})' = \mathbf{0} .
$$

What survives is

$$
\mathbf{C}\mathbf{C}' = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{D}'
= (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{D}' .
$$

Therefore

$$
\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}^{\ast}\} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} + \sigma^2\mathbf{D}\mathbf{D}'
= \operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\} + \sigma^2\mathbf{D}\mathbf{D}' .
$$

For any fixed $\mathbf{c}$, the variance of the scalar $\mathbf{c}'\mathbf{b}^{\ast}$ is

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{c}'\mathbf{b}^{\ast}\} = \mathbf{c}'\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\}\mathbf{c} + \sigma^2 \mathbf{c}'\mathbf{D}\mathbf{D}'\mathbf{c}
= \operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{c}'\mathbf{b}\} + \sigma^2\|\mathbf{D}'\mathbf{c}\|^2 .
$$

The added term $\sigma^2\|\mathbf{D}'\mathbf{c}\|^2 \ge 0$ is a squared length, so
every linear unbiased competitor has variance at least that of $\mathbf{c}'\mathbf{b}$,
with equality only when $\mathbf{D}'\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$; taking $\mathbf{c}$ to
range over the coordinate vectors forces $\mathbf{D} = \mathbf{0}$, that is, the
competitor is $\mathbf{b}$ itself. Least squares is BLUE. $\blacksquare$

Setting $\mathbf{X} = [\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{x}]$ and $\mathbf{c} = (0, 1)'$ recovers the
Chapter 2 statement that $b_1$ is BLUE (@ch02-gauss-markov-slr); the matrix proof did
every coefficient, and every linear combination of coefficients, at once.

:::{admonition} Durable skill: Prove the general case, use it forever
:class: tip
The scalar Gauss-Markov proof in Chapter 2 took a page and covered one estimator. The
matrix proof here is about the same length and covers every linear regression you will
ever fit, with any number of predictors, plus every linear combination of coefficients
(predictions included). That is the payoff of the right notation: a single argument at
the general level retires an unbounded list of special cases. When you find yourself
proving the same kind of thing twice, look for the notation that lets you prove it
once. To practice, take a result you know for two predictors and rewrite its proof so
that nothing in it mentions the number two.
:::

## 7.7 Simple regression as a special case

### Intuition

Everything in Chapters 2 and 3 should drop out of the matrix formulas when there is
one predictor, and it does. Seeing it happen once turns the general formulas from
abstractions into trusted tools: the same machine that fit Dwaine's plane fits
Toluca's line, and returns the exact numbers you already know. @fig-ch07-contains-slr
is the whole idea in one picture: one formula, two jobs.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch07_contains_slr.png
:name: fig-ch07-contains-slr
:alt: A flow diagram. A central purple box holds the one matrix formula b equals X-prime-X inverse times X-prime-Y. Two arrows point down from it. The left blue box, labeled p equals 2 Toluca line, shows the design matrix with a ones column and one predictor collapsing to the Chapter 2 scalar formulas b1 equals Sxy over Sxx and b0 equals Ybar minus b1 Xbar. The right green box, labeled p equals 3 Dwaine plane, shows the design matrix with two predictors and the fitted coefficients minus 68.86, 1.4546, 9.3655.
One matrix formula does two jobs. Set p equals 2 and it collapses into the exact Chapter 2 scalar formulas for the Toluca line; set p equals 3 and it fits the Dwaine plane. The general model does not replace simple regression, it contains it.
```

### Derivation (matrix formulas reduce to Chapter 2)

**Derivation (the $p = 2$ case).** With one predictor, the design matrix is
$\mathbf{X} = [\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{x}]$, so

$$
\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X} = \begin{pmatrix} n & \sum X_i \\ \sum X_i & \sum X_i^2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad
\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} = \begin{pmatrix} \sum Y_i \\ \sum X_i Y_i \end{pmatrix} .
$$

where $S_{xx} = \sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2$ and $S_{xy} = \sum (X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})$
are the Chapter 2 sums of squares and cross-products, and $\bar{X}, \bar{Y}$ are the
sample means. The inverse of a $2 \times 2$ matrix divides by its determinant
$\det(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}) = n\sum X_i^2 - (\sum X_i)^2 = n\,S_{xx}$ (@ch06-inverse),

$$
(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} = \frac{1}{n\,S_{xx}}\begin{pmatrix} \sum X_i^2 & -\sum X_i \\ -\sum X_i & n \end{pmatrix} .
$$

Multiplying by $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ and simplifying (each entry collapses using
$\sum X_i = n\bar{X}$ and the definitions of $S_{xx}, S_{xy}$) gives

$$
\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} = \begin{pmatrix} \bar{Y} - b_1\bar{X} \\ S_{xy}/S_{xx} \end{pmatrix} ,
$$

which are exactly the Chapter 2 estimators $b_0 = \bar{Y} - b_1\bar{X}$ and
$b_1 = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$. The covariance matrix reduces the same way: the bottom-right
entry of $\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$ is $\sigma^2 n / (n S_{xx}) = \sigma^2/S_{xx}$,
matching $\operatorname{Var}\{b_1\}$, and the top-left entry is
$\sigma^2 \sum X_i^2/(n S_{xx}) = \sigma^2(1/n + \bar{X}^2/S_{xx})$, matching
$\operatorname{Var}\{b_0\}$ from @ch02-gauss-markov-slr. $\blacksquare$

Example 7.5 already confirmed this numerically: the matrix pipeline returned Toluca's
$b_0 = 62.3659$, $b_1 = 3.5702$, and standard errors $26.1774$ and $0.3470$, the same
figures Chapter 2 produced from the scalar formulas. The general linear model does not
replace simple regression; it contains it.

## 7.8 Chapter summary

This chapter turned Chapter 6's matrix arithmetic into a full multiple regression. One
equation, $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$,
carries any number of predictors. Least squares projects $\mathbf{Y}$ onto the column
space of $\mathbf{X}$, giving $\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$
by calculus and by geometry alike. The hat matrix $\mathbf{H}$ packages that projection,
and its two properties, being a projection (symmetric and idempotent) with trace $p$,
drive the sampling distribution of $\mathbf{b}$, the $n - p$ divisor in $\mathrm{MSE}$,
and the Gauss-Markov optimality. Every Chapter 2 formula returns as the $p = 2$ special
case.

**Key results at a glance.**

| Result | Statement or formula | Valid when |
|---|---|---|
| Least-squares estimator (Theorem 7.3) | $\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$ minimizes $Q(\mathbf{b})$ | $\mathbf{X}$ full column rank |
| Normal equations (Definition 7.4) | $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$, equivalently $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$ | any least-squares fit |
| Hat matrix properties (Theorem 7.7) | $\mathbf{H}$ symmetric, idempotent, $\operatorname{tr}\mathbf{H} = p$, $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{H}) = n-p$ | $\mathbf{X}$ full column rank |
| Mean and covariance of $\mathbf{b}$ (Theorem 7.8) | $E\{\mathbf{b}\} = \boldsymbol{\beta}$, $\operatorname{Cov}\{\mathbf{b}\} = \sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$ | $E\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\}=\mathbf{0}$, $\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\}=\sigma^2\mathbf{I}$ |
| Sampling distribution (Theorem 7.8) | $\mathbf{b} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\beta}, \sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1})$ | errors also normal |
| Unbiasedness of MSE (Theorem 7.9) | $E\{\mathrm{SSE}\} = (n-p)\sigma^2$, so $E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \sigma^2$ | $\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\}=\sigma^2\mathbf{I}$ |
| Gauss-Markov (Theorem 7.10) | $\mathbf{b}$ is the best linear unbiased estimator of $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ | $E\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\}=\mathbf{0}$, $\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\}=\sigma^2\mathbf{I}$; no normality needed |
| Coefficient of determination | $R^2 = 1 - \mathrm{SSE}/\mathrm{SSTO}$ (Dwaine $0.9167$) | descriptive, any fit |

## 7.9 Frequently asked questions

**Q1. Why does the design matrix need a column of ones?** The column of ones lets the
intercept $\beta_0$ be handled like any other coefficient: its "predictor value" is
$1$ for every observation, so $\beta_0 \cdot 1$ is the same baseline for every row.
Without it the fitted plane would be forced through the origin. The first normal
equation, coming from that column, is exactly $\sum e_i = 0$.

**Q2. What does full column rank mean, and what breaks without it?** Full column rank
means no predictor column is a linear combination of the others (including the ones
column). If one is, say a predictor recorded in both meters and feet, then
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is singular and $(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$ does not exist,
so there is no unique $\mathbf{b}$: infinitely many coefficient vectors fit equally
well. Software then drops a column or returns an error. Near-violations (highly
correlated predictors) are multicollinearity, the subject of Chapter 12.

**Q3. Is the hat matrix worth computing for real data?** As a full $n \times n$ matrix,
usually not; software gets fitted values and residuals more cheaply. But its diagonal
entries $h_{ii}$, the leverage values, are computed and used constantly in
diagnostics, and its algebra (symmetric, idempotent, trace $p$) is what makes the
theory work. Think of $\mathbf{H}$ as a proof tool and a source of the leverages, not
a thing you print.

**Q4. Why is the divisor $n - p$ and not $n - 1$ or $n$?** Each parameter you estimate
uses one degree of freedom before the residuals are measured. A sample variance
estimates one quantity (the mean) and divides by $n - 1$; a regression with $p$
parameters divides by $n - p$. The trace derivation in @ch07-sigma2 shows
$E\{\mathrm{SSE}\} = (n - p)\sigma^2$ exactly, so $n - p$ is the unique divisor making
$\mathrm{MSE}$ unbiased. Dividing by $n$ (the maximum-likelihood choice) is biased low.

**Q5. Do I need normal errors for any of this?** No, for most of it. The estimator
$\mathbf{b}$, its unbiasedness, its covariance $\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$,
the unbiasedness of $\mathrm{MSE}$, and the Gauss-Markov optimality all hold with only
mean-zero, constant-variance, uncorrelated errors. Normality is an extra assumption,
used to get the exact multivariate normal sampling distribution of $\mathbf{b}$ and the
$t$ and $F$ tests of Chapter 8.

**Q6. The Dwaine coefficients from the matrix formula and from `lm` matched exactly.
Is that always guaranteed?** Yes, up to tiny floating-point differences. Both compute
the same $(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$; `lm` and `statsmodels`
use a more numerically stable routine (a QR or SVD decomposition) instead of forming
the inverse explicitly, which matters only for badly conditioned designs. For
well-behaved data like these, every printed digit agrees.

**Q7. Why are the two Dwaine slope estimates negatively correlated in
@fig-ch07-coef-ellipse?** Because the two predictors are themselves positively
correlated across cities. When a sample happens to make the population slope come out
high, the fit compensates by making the income slope come out low to avoid
double-counting the shared signal. That trade-off is exactly the off-diagonal entry of
$\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$, and it is why you cannot read one coefficient's
uncertainty without the whole covariance matrix.

divisor in $\mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n - p)$ is $n - p$ and not $n$. Then answer a
"what would change if": suppose an analyst kept adding predictors to the Dwaine data
until the model had $p = 21$ parameters, one for every observation. Describe what happens
to $\mathrm{SSE}$, to the divisor, and to $\mathrm{MSE}$, and explain why a fit with
$\mathrm{SSE} = 0$ is a warning rather than a success.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The divisor is $n - p$ because fitting $p$ coefficients uses up $p$ of the $n$
independent directions in the data before the residuals are measured. Formally,
$\mathrm{SSE} = \mathbf{Y}'(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}$ and
$\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = n - p$, so $E\{\mathrm{SSE}\} = (n - p)\sigma^2$;
dividing by $n - p$, and no other number, makes $\mathrm{MSE}$ an unbiased estimator of
$\sigma^2$. Dividing by $n$ would systematically underestimate the error variance.

If $p = 21 = n$, the column space of $\mathbf{X}$ fills the whole $21$-dimensional
response space, so the projection $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$ equals
$\mathbf{Y}$ exactly and every residual is zero, giving $\mathrm{SSE} = 0$. The divisor
$n - p = 21 - 21 = 0$, so $\mathrm{MSE} = 0/0$ is undefined: there are no error degrees
of freedom left. A zero $\mathrm{SSE}$ does not mean the model is perfect; it means the
model has enough parameters to interpolate the data exactly, so it has learned the noise
along with the signal and can say nothing about $\sigma^2$ or about a new city. Perfect
in-sample fit with zero degrees of freedom is the signature of overfitting.

A weak answer says "$n - p$ adjusts for the parameters" without connecting it to
unbiasedness or the trace, and treats $\mathrm{SSE} = 0$ as a good outcome instead of
recognizing that zero error degrees of freedom leave nothing to estimate the variance
with.
:::

**EP 7.3 (a student claims X, evaluate).** A student writes: "Adding a predictor can
never increase $\mathrm{SSE}$, and it always raises $R^2$. So the best strategy is to
keep adding predictors until $R^2$ is as close to $1$ as possible." Using the projection
picture of least squares, evaluate the two factual claims and the strategy they support.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The two factual claims are true. Adding a column to $\mathbf{X}$ enlarges its column
space, and projecting $\mathbf{Y}$ onto a larger subspace can only bring the foot of the
perpendicular $\hat{\mathbf{Y}}$ closer to $\mathbf{Y}$, never farther, so
$\mathrm{SSE} = \|\mathbf{Y} - \hat{\mathbf{Y}}\|^2$ cannot rise; the old fit is still
available as a special case where the new coefficient is zero. Because
$R^2 = 1 - \mathrm{SSE}/\mathrm{SSTO}$ and $\mathrm{SSTO}$ is fixed, a non-increasing
$\mathrm{SSE}$ means a non-decreasing $R^2$.

The strategy, however, is wrong. Since $R^2$ rises even for predictors that are pure
noise, a high $R^2$ bought by piling on predictors is not evidence of a good model; in
the limit $p = n$ the fit interpolates the data with $R^2 = 1$ and $\mathrm{SSE} = 0$
while explaining nothing out of sample. Each added predictor also spends a degree of
freedom, shrinking $n - p$ and inflating the variances $\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$
of the coefficients, especially when the new predictor is correlated with those already
present. The right question is not whether $\mathrm{SSE}$ dropped but whether it dropped
by more than noise would predict, which is exactly what the general linear test of
Chapter 8 measures.

A weak answer either wrongly disputes the two true facts or accepts the strategy, missing
that a guaranteed rise in $R^2$ carries no information about whether a predictor belongs
in the model.
:::

**EP 7.4 (interpret computed output in context).** The leverage values $h_{ii}$ are the
diagonal entries of the hat matrix $\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$
for the Dwaine fit. The six largest, and a check on their sum, are below.

```text
row 20  targtpop=  82.7  dispoinc= 19.1  h_ii=0.2788
row 13  targtpop=  88.4  dispoinc= 17.4  h_ii=0.2390
row 15  targtpop=  52.5  dispoinc= 17.8  h_ii=0.2095
row  3  targtpop=  91.3  dispoinc= 18.2  h_ii=0.1737
row  5  targtpop=  46.9  dispoinc= 17.3  h_ii=0.1620
row 19  targtpop=  89.6  dispoinc= 18.1  h_ii=0.1611
sum(h_ii) = 3.0000     mean(targtpop)=62.02  mean(dispoinc)=17.14
```

Explain what a large $h_{ii}$ says about a city, and interpret why row 20 has the largest
leverage value even though its target population ($82.7$) is not the largest in the data.
Then explain why the leverage values sum to exactly $3$, and use the rule-of-thumb cutoff
$2p/n = 0.286$ to say whether any Dwaine city should be called a high-leverage point.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
A large $h_{ii}$ means the city sits far from the center of the predictor cloud, so its
predictor values are unusual and its own response pulls its fitted value strongly toward
itself. Row 20 has the largest leverage value not because of any single coordinate but
because of the combination: its disposable income $19.1$ is the highest in the data,
well above the mean of $17.14$, and its target population $82.7$ is also well above the
mean of $62.02$, so it sits in a far corner of the two-dimensional predictor space.
The leverage value measures distance from the joint center, so a city can have a higher
leverage value than one with a larger population alone if it is more extreme on both predictors
together.

The leverage values sum to $3$ because $\sum_i h_{ii} = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p = 3$,
proved in @ch07-hat-matrix: the trace of the projection matrix counts the parameters, so
the total of the leverage values the fit distributes across cities is fixed at $p$ regardless of the
data. Against the cutoff $2p/n = 0.286$, the largest leverage value ($0.2788$) falls just
below it, so by this rule of thumb no Dwaine city is flagged as a high-leverage point;
the design is fairly balanced, with no city dominating the predictor space.

A weak answer reads the leverage value off target population alone, misses that it measures joint
distance from the center of both predictors, and cannot explain the sum-to-$p$ property
as the trace of $\mathbf{H}$.
:::

**EP 7.5 (what would change if).** An analyst re-expresses target population in hundreds
of people instead of thousands, multiplying every `targtpop` value by $10$, and refits.
The two coefficient tables are below.

```text
original                              rescaled
             Estimate Std. Error       Estimate Std. Error
(Intercept)  -68.8571    60.0170       -68.8571    60.0170
targtpop       1.4546     0.2118        0.1455     0.0212
dispoinc       9.3655     4.0640        9.3655     4.0640
original  MSE=121.16  R2=0.9167     rescaled  MSE=121.16  R2=0.9167
```

Explain, in full sentences and with reference to the model algebra, why the target-
population coefficient and its standard error both divide by $10$ while its $t$ value,
the fitted values, $\mathrm{MSE}$, and $R^2$ are all unchanged. What does this tell you
about reading the size of a coefficient as a measure of a predictor's importance?

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
Rescaling a predictor by a factor of $10$ replaces its column $\mathbf{x}$ by
$10\mathbf{x}$. For the fitted plane to make the same predictions, the coefficient on
that column must divide by $10$, since $(10\mathbf{x})(\beta/10) = \mathbf{x}\beta$: the
product that enters $\hat{\mathbf{Y}}$ is unchanged, so the fitted values, the residuals,
$\mathrm{SSE}$, $\mathrm{MSE}$, and $R^2$ are all identical to the original fit. The
standard error divides by $10$ for the same reason the estimate does, because it is
measured in the coefficient's own units (dollars per hundred people instead of dollars
per thousand people), so the ratio $t = b / s\{b\}$ is dimensionless and does not move;
the evidence that the slope differs from zero is exactly as strong as before.

The lesson is that the raw magnitude of a coefficient is not a measure of a predictor's
importance, because you can make a coefficient ten times larger or smaller just by
changing the units of its predictor, with no change to the fit or to the strength of the
evidence. To compare importance you must look at scale-free quantities such as the $t$
value, or standardize the predictors first.

A weak answer states that the numbers change without tying the factor of $10$ to the
$\mathbf{x}\beta$ product staying fixed, and fails to draw the conclusion that
coefficient size, being unit-dependent, cannot rank predictors by importance.
:::

:::{admonition} Resumen del capítulo (en español)
:class: dropdown
Este capítulo presenta el **modelo lineal general (general linear model)**
$\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, que escribe
cualquier regresión, con uno o con muchos predictores, en una sola forma matricial.
La **matriz de diseño (design matrix)** $\mathbf{X}$ tiene una primera columna de unos
para el intercepto y una columna por cada predictor; $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ reúne los
parámetros y $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ los errores, con
$E\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \mathbf{0}$ y
$\operatorname{Cov}\{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$.

El estimador de **mínimos cuadrados (least squares)** es
$\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$, obtenido de dos
maneras: por cálculo, derivando las **ecuaciones normales (normal equations)**
$\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$; y por **proyección
ortogonal (orthogonal projection)**, donde $\hat{\mathbf{Y}}$ es el punto del espacio
de columnas de $\mathbf{X}$ más cercano a $\mathbf{Y}$ y el residuo
$\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \hat{\mathbf{Y}}$ es perpendicular a ese espacio. Para
Dwaine Studios, $\mathbf{b} = (-68.86,\ 1.4546,\ 9.3655)$.

La **matriz sombrero (hat matrix)** $\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$
cumple $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$ y es simétrica e idempotente con traza
$p$. De ahí sale que $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = n - p$, el divisor
correcto para estimar la varianza: $\mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{SSE}/(n - p)$ es
**insesgado (unbiased)**, lo cual se prueba con un argumento de traza. Para Dwaine,
$\mathrm{MSE} = 121.16$ y $R^2 = 0.9167$.

El vector $\mathbf{b}$ tiene media $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ y covarianza
$\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$; bajo errores normales sigue una distribución
normal multivariante. El **teorema de Gauss-Markov (Gauss-Markov theorem)** demuestra
que $\mathbf{b}$ es el mejor estimador lineal insesgado, sin necesitar normalidad.
Finalmente, la regresión simple del Capítulo 2 aparece como el caso $p = 2$: las
fórmulas matriciales reproducen $b_0 = 62.3659$, $b_1 = 3.5702$ y
$\mathrm{MSE} = 2383.72$ de la Toluca Company, dígito por dígito.
:::

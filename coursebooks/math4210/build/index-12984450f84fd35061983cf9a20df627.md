# 9. Model diagnostics

:::{div}
:class: lang-toggle
[Leer en espanol](./es.md)
:::

In 1973 two biologists, Michael Johnson and Peter Raven, tallied the number of
plant species on 30 islands of the Galapagos archipelago and recorded a handful
of geographic facts about each island: its area, its highest elevation, how far
it sits from the nearest island, how far from the central island of Santa Cruz,
and the area of the closest neighbor. The natural question is whether geography
predicts biological richness. Bigger, taller, more isolated islands might hold
more species. A multiple regression of species count on those five predictors
is the obvious first move, and it looks like a success: it explains about 77
percent of the variation in species counts.

Then you plot the residuals, and the model falls apart. @fig-ch09-gala-hook
shows the residuals against the fitted counts. Three problems jump out at once.
The spread of the residuals grows as the fitted count grows, a funnel that
breaks the constant-variance assumption. One island, Isabela, sits far from the
crowd. And for several small islands the model predicts a *negative* number of
species, which is impossible. A fit that reports a high $R^2$ can still be
wrong in every way that matters.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch09_gala_hook.png
:name: fig-ch09-gala-hook
:alt: Scatterplot of residuals against fitted species counts for the Galapagos regression. The points fan outward as the fitted count increases, forming a funnel. Isabela is labeled as a large negative residual. A shaded band on the left marks fitted values below zero, where the model predicts a negative number of species.
The Galapagos model earns a high R-squared yet fails on sight: the residual spread widens with the fitted count (nonconstant variance), the model predicts negative species counts for small islands (the shaded region), and Isabela is an extreme case.
```

This chapter is about the fourth stage of the modeling workflow, CHECK. You have
learned to ASK a question, EXPLORE the data, and FIT a model in matrix form
(@ch07-ls-matrix) with all its inference machinery (@ch08-general-linear-test).
Fitting is now the easy part; any computer will do it, and so will any student.
The scarce skill is judgment: deciding whether a fitted model can be trusted,
finding the observations that are quietly running the show, and naming exactly
which assumption a model breaks. That is what diagnostics do. By the end of the
chapter you will have a toolkit that turns "the fit looks fine" into a checklist
you can defend.

We work two real datasets side by side. The Galapagos species data (`gala`) is
the model that fails every test. The cross-country savings data (`savings`),
which you first met in the correlation chapter, is the model whose assumptions
mostly hold but whose fit is steered by a single country. Along the way the
Toluca production data returns one last time to answer a question we could not
settle before: is a straight line really the right shape?

:::{admonition} This lesson at a glance
:class: important
- **What we are doing:** Checking a fitted model with leverage values, the family of studentized residuals, influence measures (Cook's distance, DFFITS, DFBETAS), diagnostic plots and formal tests, and the lack-of-fit F test, worked on the gala and savings fits with the Toluca line returning to settle whether a straight line was ever the right shape.
- **Why we are doing it:** A model can post a high $R^2$ and still be wrong in every way that matters; the Galapagos fit even predicts negative species counts. Fitting is easy, and judging whether a fit can be trusted is the scarce skill.
- **Main objective:** Compute and read leverage values $h_{ii}$, studentized deleted residuals, and influence measures, and name exactly which assumption a model breaks and which case is quietly running the fit.
- **What changed from the last chapters:** Chapters 7 and 8 fit and interpreted a model as if every assumption held; this is the CHECK stage that tests them, reusing the hat matrix of @ch07-hat-matrix and the general linear test of @ch08-general-linear-test (which returns disguised as the lack-of-fit test).
:::

## 9.1 Leverage values and the hat matrix

### Intuition

Before we can judge a residual we have to know how much freedom the model gave
each point. Picture the fitted line as a see-saw. A point sitting near the
middle of the predictor values can move up or down quite a bit without shifting
the line much; the crowd around it holds the line in place. A point way out at
the edge of the predictor range is different: because it is the only observation
out there, the line has to follow it, so the fitted line at that point is pulled
toward the point's own response. That pull is measured by the **leverage value**
$h_{ii}$ (Definition 9.1). A high-leverage point is one whose predictor values are unusual, so the
fit is forced to pay it special attention regardless of its response.

The leverage value is a statement about the predictors only. It says nothing yet about
whether a point is fit well or badly. It measures potential, the capacity to
influence the line, and we will spend the next two sections turning potential
into a verdict.

### Formula

Recall the hat matrix from @ch07-hat-matrix. Stacking the observations into the
design matrix $\mathbf{X}$ (an $n \times p$ matrix whose first column is ones,
with $p$ the number of parameters including the intercept), the fitted values
are $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$ where

$$
\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}' .
$$

- $\mathbf{H}$ is the $n \times n$ hat matrix, the orthogonal projection onto the column space of $\mathbf{X}$; it "puts the hat on $\mathbf{Y}$."
- $\mathbf{X}'$ is the transpose of $\mathbf{X}$, and $(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$ is the inverse built in @ch06-inverse.

:::{admonition} Definition 9.1: Leverage value
:class: note definition
The **leverage value** of case $i$ is the $i$-th diagonal entry of the hat
matrix $\mathbf{H}$,

$$
h_{ii} = \mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i,
$$

where $\mathbf{x}_i'$ is the $i$-th row of $\mathbf{X}$ (the predictors for case
$i$, with a leading 1). It is a weighted squared distance of case $i$'s
predictors from the center of all the predictors, large exactly when case $i$
sits in a lonely part of predictor space.
:::

### Derivation (properties of the leverage values)

:::{admonition} Theorem 9.2: Properties of the leverage values
:class: important theorem
Under the linear model with hat matrix
$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$, the leverage
values satisfy

$$
0 \le h_{ii} \le 1, \qquad
\sum_{i=1}^n h_{ii} = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p,
$$

so their average is $p/n$. A common rule of thumb flags any case with
$h_{ii} > 2p/n$ as a high-leverage case worth a second look.
:::

**Proof.** In
@ch07-hat-matrix we proved that $\mathbf{H}$ is symmetric
($\mathbf{H}' = \mathbf{H}$) and idempotent ($\mathbf{H}\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}$),
with trace equal to $p$. Three facts follow directly.

First, because $\mathbf{H}$ is symmetric and idempotent, $\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{H}$
read on the diagonal gives

$$
h_{ii} = \sum_{j=1}^n h_{ij} h_{ji} = \sum_{j=1}^n h_{ij}^2 = h_{ii}^2 + \sum_{j \neq i} h_{ij}^2 .
$$

Rearranging, $h_{ii} - h_{ii}^2 = \sum_{j \neq i} h_{ij}^2 \ge 0$, so
$h_{ii}(1 - h_{ii}) \ge 0$. Since $h_{ii} = \sum_j h_{ij}^2 \ge 0$ as well, we get

$$
0 \le h_{ii} \le 1 .
$$

In words: a leverage value is trapped between 0 and 1, and a value near 1 means
the fit at that point is determined almost entirely by that point's own response.

Second, the leverage values add up to the trace of $\mathbf{H}$:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n h_{ii} = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p .
$$

Third, dividing by $n$, the *average* leverage value is $p/n$. A point is not carrying
its fair share of predictor space if its leverage value is well above that average,
which motivates the common rule of thumb: examine any case with

$$
h_{ii} > \frac{2p}{n} .
$$

The factor of 2 is a convention, not a law; it flags roughly the points worth a
second look. $\blacksquare$

For simple regression the leverage value has a clean closed form that shows
plainly what it measures.

:::{admonition} Theorem 9.3: Leverage values in simple regression
:class: important theorem
For simple linear regression with a single predictor, the leverage value of
case $i$ is

$$
h_{ii} = \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_i - \bar X)^2}{S_{xx}},
\qquad S_{xx} = \sum_i (X_i - \bar X)^2 .
$$

It is a baseline $1/n$ shared by every case plus a penalty that grows with the
squared distance of $X_i$ from the mean predictor value.
:::

**Proof.** With one predictor,
$\mathbf{x}_i' = (1, X_i)$ and, as computed in @ch07-hat-matrix,

$$
(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} = \frac{1}{n S_{xx}}
\begin{pmatrix} \sum X_i^2 & -\sum X_i \\ -\sum X_i & n \end{pmatrix},
\qquad S_{xx} = \sum (X_i - \bar X)^2 .
$$

Multiplying out the quadratic form $\mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i$
and simplifying with $\sum X_i = n\bar X$ gives

$$
h_{ii} = \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_i - \bar X)^2}{S_{xx}} .
$$

In words: the leverage value in simple regression is a baseline $1/n$ shared by everyone,
plus a penalty that grows with the squared distance of $X_i$ from the mean
predictor value. @fig-ch09-hii-curve draws this parabola on the Toluca lot
sizes. $\blacksquare$

```{figure} figures/fig_ch09_hii_curve.png
:name: fig-ch09-hii-curve
:alt: Leverage values plotted against lot size for the Toluca data. The leverage values trace an upward-opening parabola with its minimum at the mean lot size of 70, rising toward both ends of the lot-size range. A dashed vertical line marks the mean and a dotted horizontal line marks the two-p-over-n cutoff.
The leverage value in simple regression is a parabola in the predictor: smallest at the mean lot size and largest at the extremes. No Toluca run crosses the 2p/n line, so none has an unusual leverage value.
```

## 9.2 Four flavors of residual

### Intuition

The leverage value measured which points had the power to distort the fit. The next
question is which points the fit actually misses. For that we read the residual
$e_i = Y_i - \hat Y_i$, the model's leftover: the part of $Y_i$ the fit did not
capture. It is tempting to hunt for outliers by scanning the raw
residuals for big values. That is a trap, because raw residuals are not on a
level playing field. A high-leverage point drags the line toward itself, which
*shrinks* its own residual. So the very points most able to distort the fit tend
to have deceptively small raw residuals. To compare residuals fairly we have to
put them on a common scale, and that means dividing by the right standard
deviation, which depends on the leverage value.

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 9.4: The four residual scales
:class: note definition
For case $i$ with residual $e_i = Y_i - \hat Y_i$, leverage value $h_{ii}$, and
$s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}}$, the four residual scales, from crudest to sharpest,
are

$$
e_i, \qquad
\frac{e_i}{s}, \qquad
r_i = \frac{e_i}{s\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}}, \qquad
t_i = \frac{e_i}{s_{(i)}\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}} .
$$

- $e_i$ is the **raw residual**; $s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}}$ is the estimated error standard deviation.
- $e_i/s$ is the **semistudentized residual**: crude, because it ignores that different residuals have different variances.
- $r_i$ is the **standardized** (internally studentized) residual, dividing by the actual estimated standard deviation of $e_i$.
- $t_i$ is the **studentized deleted** (externally studentized) residual, where $s_{(i)}$ is the error standard deviation estimated from the fit with case $i$ removed.
:::

The scale factor $\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}$ is not decoration. It comes from an exact
variance formula for the residuals.

### Derivation (the variance of a residual)

:::{admonition} Theorem 9.5: Variance of a residual
:class: important theorem
Under the linear model with $\operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{Y}\} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$,
the residual vector $\mathbf{e} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}$ has

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{e}\} = \sigma^2(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}), \qquad
\operatorname{Var}\{e_i\} = \sigma^2(1 - h_{ii}), \qquad
\operatorname{Cov}\{e_i, e_j\} = -\sigma^2 h_{ij}.
$$

The residual at a high-leverage point has *smaller* variance, which is why
dividing by $\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}$ is needed to restore a common scale.
:::

**Proof.** Write the
residual vector as a linear map of the responses. Since
$\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$,

$$
\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{Y} - \hat{\mathbf{Y}} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\mathbf{Y}.
$$

Under the model $\operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{Y}\} = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}$ (the
errors are uncorrelated with common variance, from @ch06-random-vectors). The
matrix $\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}$ is symmetric and idempotent, just like
$\mathbf{H}$. Applying the covariance rule for a linear transform,

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{\mathbf{e}\} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})\sigma^2\mathbf{I}(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})'
= \sigma^2 (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H})(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}) = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}).
$$

Reading the $i$-th diagonal entry,

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{e_i\} = \sigma^2 (1 - h_{ii}), \qquad
\operatorname{Cov}\{e_i, e_j\} = -\sigma^2 h_{ij}.
$$

In words: the residual at a high-leverage point has *smaller* variance, because
the fit bends to meet it. That is exactly why a raw residual understates trouble
at high-leverage points, and why dividing by $\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}$ restores a
common scale. @fig-ch09-residual-shrink shows the shrinkage and its correction.
$\blacksquare$

```{figure} figures/fig_ch09_residual_shrink.png
:name: fig-ch09-residual-shrink
:alt: Two curves against the leverage value on the horizontal axis. A solid line showing one minus h falls from 1 to 0 as the leverage value grows, shaded underneath, labeled as the variance of a raw residual over sigma squared. A dashed line showing one over the square root of one minus h rises steeply, labeled as the standardizing factor.
As the leverage value grows, a raw residual keeps less of the error variance (solid curve), so it looks artificially small. Standardizing multiplies by the rising dashed factor to undo the shrinkage and put every residual on one scale.
```

### Derivation (the studentized deleted residual and the outlier test)

The standardized residual $r_i$ has a subtle flaw: if case $i$ really is an
outlier, it inflates $s$, the very quantity in its own denominator, masking
itself. The fix is to estimate the error standard deviation from the *other*
cases. Let $s_{(i)}$ be the root mean square error of the fit with case $i$
deleted. A useful algebraic identity, which avoids refitting $n$ times, is

$$
(n - p - 1)\,s_{(i)}^2 = (n - p)\,\mathrm{MSE} - \frac{e_i^2}{1 - h_{ii}} .
$$

In words: this builds the leave-one-out error variance from quantities the full
fit already gives you, by subtracting case $i$'s own squared-error contribution,
so you never actually refit.

:::{admonition} Theorem 9.6: Distribution of the studentized deleted residual
:class: important theorem
Under the linear model, when case $i$ is not an outlier, the studentized
deleted residual follows a $t$ distribution with $n - p - 1$ degrees of freedom:

$$
t_i = \frac{e_i}{s_{(i)}\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}} \;\sim\; t(n - p - 1).
$$

The most extreme case is flagged as an outlier when
$|t_i| > t(1 - \alpha/(2n);\, n - p - 1)$, a Bonferroni cutoff that holds the
family-wise false-alarm rate below $\alpha$.
:::

**Proof.** Consider the **deleted
residual** $d_i = Y_i - \hat Y_{i(i)}$, the gap between $Y_i$ and the prediction
at $\mathbf{x}_i$ from a model that never saw case $i$. Because that prediction
does not use $Y_i$, the two are independent. An identity we prove from the
leave-one-out update in @ch09-cooks-distance gives $d_i = e_i / (1 - h_{ii})$,
with variance

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{d_i\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{1 - h_{ii}} .
$$

Estimating $\sigma^2$ by the independent $s_{(i)}^2$ and dividing $d_i$ by its
estimated standard error yields

$$
t_i = \frac{d_i}{s_{(i)}/\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}}
= \frac{e_i}{s_{(i)}\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}}
\;\sim\; t(n - p - 1)
$$

under the model, when case $i$ is not an outlier. Because numerator and
denominator are independent, this is an exact $t$ ratio. $\blacksquare$

To test whether the most extreme case is a genuine outlier you look at
$\max_i |t_i|$. Since you are implicitly running $n$ tests, use a Bonferroni
cutoff: flag case $i$ when $|t_i| > t(1 - \alpha/(2n);\, n - p - 1)$. The
correction keeps the chance of a single false alarm across all $n$ cases below
$\alpha$.

:::{admonition} Example 9.2: Ranking the savings residuals four ways
:class: note
**Question.** In the savings model, how do Zambia and Chile look on each residual
scale, and is either a significant outlier?

**Intuition.** Fit the model, compute all four residual scales for the two most
extreme countries, then run the Bonferroni outlier test on the whole set.

**Formula.** $e_i$, $e_i/s$, $r_i$, $t_i$ as above, with cutoff
$t(1 - \alpha/(2n);\, n - p - 1)$.

**Computation.**


## 9.3 Influence: which points actually change the fit

### Intuition

A high leverage value is potential and a large residual is a symptom; **influence**
is the thing we actually care about, the amount the fit would change if a point were
gone (Definition 9.7). A point can be high-leverage yet have little influence, if it happens to fall
right on the trend the other points set. And a point with a large residual but an
ordinary leverage value may tug the line only a little. Influence is the product of the
two ingredients: an unusual predictor row *and* a response the model fits poorly.

@fig-ch09-leverage-influence makes the point with four small pictures. In each
one we drop a single red point into the same cloud and refit. Push the point far
out in $X$ but keep it on the trend, and the line does not budge: a high leverage
value, no influence. Give it a big vertical miss but an ordinary $X$, and the line
barely tilts: a plain outlier. Only when the point is *both* far out in $X$ *and*
off the trend does the line swing to chase it. That last panel is influence, and
it takes both ingredients at once.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch09_leverage_influence.png
:name: fig-ch09-leverage-influence
:alt: A two-by-two grid of scatterplots. Each panel shows the same base cloud of blue points with a fitted line, plus one added red point, and two lines, a solid fit that includes the red point and a dashed fit that excludes it. Top left, an ordinary red point on the trend: the two lines coincide, labeled harmless. Top right, a red point at an extreme predictor value but on the trend: the lines still coincide, labeled still harmless. Bottom left, a red point at an ordinary predictor value but far above the trend: the line tilts only slightly, labeled outlier with small tilt. Bottom right, a red point at an extreme predictor value and far below the trend: the solid line swings well away from the dashed line, labeled influential, the line bends.
Influence needs both ingredients. A point moves the fit (solid versus dashed line) only in the bottom-right panel, where the red point is both far out in the predictor and off the trend. An unusual X alone (top right) or a big miss alone (bottom left) leaves the line almost where it was.
```

:::{admonition} Definition 9.7: Influential observation
:class: note definition
An **influential observation** is a case whose removal would noticeably change
the fitted model, that is the fitted values or the estimated coefficients.
Influence combines two ingredients: an unusual predictor row (a high leverage
value) and a response the model fits poorly (a large residual).
:::

:::{admonition} A common misreading
:class: warning
A student looks at the savings residuals and says, "Zambia has the biggest
residual, so Zambia is the point most distorting the fit." That confuses a large
residual with influence. Zambia has an ordinary leverage value ($h = 0.064$), so its big
residual moves the fit only modestly. Libya, with a middling residual but the
highest leverage value in the data, is the country that actually bends the model. The
right question is never "who is fit worst?" but "who would change the answer if
they left?"
:::

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 9.8: Cook's distance
:class: note definition
**Cook's distance** measures the influence of case $i$ by how far *all* the
fitted values move when the case is deleted:

$$
D_i = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^n (\hat Y_j - \hat Y_{j(i)})^2}{p\,\mathrm{MSE}}
= \frac{r_i^2}{p}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{1 - h_{ii}} ,
$$

where $\hat Y_{j(i)}$ is the fitted value for case $j$ from the model refit
without case $i$, $r_i$ is the standardized residual, and $h_{ii}$ the leverage
value of case $i$.
:::

The second form makes the recipe explicit: influence equals a residual part
($r_i^2$) times a factor $h_{ii}/(1 - h_{ii})$ that grows with the leverage value.
Both must be sizable for $D_i$ to be large. A rough guide treats $D_i$ near or above 1 as worth serious
attention, and any case far above the rest as worth naming.

:::{admonition} Definition 9.9: DFFITS and DFBETAS
:class: note definition
Two companion measures localize influence. **DFFITS** rescales the change in a
single fitted value, and **DFBETAS** rescales the change in a single
coefficient:

$$
\mathrm{DFFITS}_i = t_i\sqrt{\frac{h_{ii}}{1 - h_{ii}}}, \qquad
\mathrm{DFBETAS}_{k(i)} = \frac{b_k - b_{k(i)}}{s_{(i)}\sqrt{(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}_{kk}}} .
$$

Common cutoffs are $|\mathrm{DFFITS}_i| > 2\sqrt{p/n}$ and
$|\mathrm{DFBETAS}_{k(i)}| > 2/\sqrt{n}$.
:::

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
The leverage value and the residual are not rivals but factors. A point moves
the fit only when it is both unusual in its predictors (a high leverage value)
and poorly fit in its response (a large residual); either ingredient alone
leaves the line largely undisturbed. Every influence measure in this chapter
combines the leverage value $h_{ii}$ with the size of the residual.
:::

### Derivation (the leave-one-out update and Cook's distance)

Every influence measure rests on one algebraic fact: you can compute the fit
without case $i$ from the full fit, no refitting required.

:::{admonition} Theorem 9.10: Leave-one-out coefficient update
:class: important theorem
Deleting case $i$ from the least-squares fit shifts the coefficient vector by

$$
\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}_{(i)} = \frac{(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i\, e_i}{1 - h_{ii}},
$$

so the fit without case $i$ can be recovered from the full fit alone, with no
refitting required.
:::

**Proof.** Deleting case $i$
removes the row $\mathbf{x}_i'$ from $\mathbf{X}$ and the value $Y_i$ from
$\mathbf{Y}$, so

$$
\mathbf{X}_{(i)}'\mathbf{X}_{(i)} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X} - \mathbf{x}_i\mathbf{x}_i',
\qquad
\mathbf{X}_{(i)}'\mathbf{Y}_{(i)} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{x}_i Y_i .
$$

The Sherman-Morrison formula gives the inverse of a rank-one downdate: for an
invertible $\mathbf{A}$ and vector $\mathbf{u}$ with $\mathbf{u}'\mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{u} \neq 1$,

$$
(\mathbf{A} - \mathbf{u}\mathbf{u}')^{-1}
= \mathbf{A}^{-1} + \frac{\mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{u}\mathbf{u}'\mathbf{A}^{-1}}{1 - \mathbf{u}'\mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{u}} ,
$$

which you can verify by multiplying the right side by $\mathbf{A} - \mathbf{u}\mathbf{u}'$
and collecting terms to get $\mathbf{I}$. Set $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$
and $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{x}_i$, so that $\mathbf{u}'\mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{u} = h_{ii}$.
Multiplying the updated inverse by $\mathbf{X}_{(i)}'\mathbf{Y}_{(i)}$ and
simplifying (using $\mathbf{x}_i'\mathbf{b} = \hat Y_i$ and $e_i = Y_i - \hat Y_i$)
collapses to the compact result

$$
\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}_{(i)} = \frac{(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i\, e_i}{1 - h_{ii}} .
$$

In words: dropping case $i$ shifts the coefficient vector by a multiple of
$(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i$, scaled by the case's residual and
inflated by $1/(1 - h_{ii})$ when the leverage value is high. Setting the shift's effect at
$\mathbf{x}_i$ itself gives $\hat Y_i - \hat Y_{i(i)} = h_{ii} e_i/(1 - h_{ii})$,
so the deleted residual is $d_i = Y_i - \hat Y_{i(i)} = e_i + h_{ii}e_i/(1-h_{ii}) = e_i/(1 - h_{ii})$,
the identity promised in @ch09-studentized. $\blacksquare$

:::{admonition} Theorem 9.11: Cook's distance identity
:class: important theorem
Cook's distance admits the closed form

$$
D_i = \frac{e_i^2\, h_{ii}}{p\,\mathrm{MSE}\,(1 - h_{ii})^2}
= \frac{r_i^2}{p}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{1 - h_{ii}},
$$

splitting influence into a squared standardized residual $r_i^2$ and a factor
$h_{ii}/(1 - h_{ii})$ built from the leverage value; both must be sizable for
$D_i$ to be large.
:::

**Proof.** The numerator of $D_i$ is a
quadratic form in that shift, because
$\hat{\mathbf{Y}} - \hat{\mathbf{Y}}_{(i)} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}_{(i)})$:

$$
\sum_j (\hat Y_j - \hat Y_{j(i)})^2
= (\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}_{(i)})'\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}_{(i)}).
$$

Substitute the deletion formula. The middle $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ cancels one
inverse on each side, leaving $\mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i = h_{ii}$:

$$
(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}_{(i)})'\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b}_{(i)})
= \frac{e_i^2}{(1 - h_{ii})^2}\,\mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i
= \frac{e_i^2\, h_{ii}}{(1 - h_{ii})^2} .
$$

Divide by $p\,\mathrm{MSE}$ and recognize $r_i^2 = e_i^2/(\mathrm{MSE}(1 - h_{ii}))$:

$$
D_i = \frac{e_i^2\, h_{ii}}{p\,\mathrm{MSE}\,(1 - h_{ii})^2}
= \frac{r_i^2}{p}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{1 - h_{ii}} .
$$

This is the promised split of $D_i$ into a squared standardized residual and a
term in the leverage value $h_{ii}$. $\blacksquare$

## 9.4 Reading the diagnostic plots

### Intuition

Formal numbers are sharp, but the fastest way to catch a broken model is to look
at it. Three plots do most of the work, and each targets one assumption. The
residual-versus-fitted plot checks the shape of the mean function and the
constancy of variance. The normal quantile-quantile plot checks the shape of the
error distribution. The scale-location plot checks constant variance again, more
sensitively. You learned to draw the first of these back in @ch02-residuals; now
you can read all three as a set.

@fig-ch09-resid-patterns is a field guide to the residual-versus-fitted plot.
Four shapes recur: a formless horizontal band (healthy), a funnel (variance
grows with the mean), a bend (the mean function is the wrong shape), and a lone
stray point (an outlier). Train your eye on these and most diagnostics become
pattern recognition.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch09_resid_patterns.png
The four residual patterns worth memorizing: a healthy formless band, a funnel of growing variance, a bend that signals the wrong mean structure, and a single outlier. Reading the plot is mostly matching it to one of these.
```

## 9.5 Formal tests for the assumptions

### Intuition

Plots persuade; tests put a number on the suspicion. Use them together, and lean
on the plots when the two disagree, because a test can miss a pattern a plot
makes obvious, and a test can flag a wrinkle too small to matter. There is one
test for each assumption: constant variance, normal errors, and independence.

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
A formal test and a diagnostic plot answer the same question with different
strengths. On a small sample a test can lack the power to confirm what a plot
shows plainly; on a large sample it can reject a departure too small to matter.
When the picture and the p-value disagree, trust the picture and let the test
sharpen, not overrule, your judgment.
:::

For **constant variance**, two tests are standard. The **Breusch-Pagan test**
regresses the squared residuals on the predictors and asks whether that
regression explains anything; if the spread depends on the predictors, it does.
The **Brown-Forsythe test** splits the data into groups, compares the spread of
residuals across groups using medians (so it resists outliers), and is a good
choice when you suspect variance changes with one particular predictor.

For **normality**, the **Shapiro-Wilk test** compares the ordered residuals to
what normal data would produce; a small p-value says the residuals are not
normal. A close relative is the **correlation test for normality**, which
computes the correlation between the ordered residuals and their normal scores
(the points in the quantile-quantile plot) and rejects normality when that
correlation is too far below 1.

For **independence** in time-ordered data, the **Durbin-Watson test** checks
whether consecutive errors are correlated.

### Formula

The Durbin-Watson statistic is

$$
D = \frac{\sum_{t=2}^n (e_t - e_{t-1})^2}{\sum_{t=1}^n e_t^2} \approx 2(1 - \hat\rho),
$$

where $\hat\rho$ is the estimated lag-1 autocorrelation of the residuals, the
correlation between each residual and the one before it in time order.

- $D$ ranges from 0 to 4. $D \approx 2$ means no autocorrelation ($\hat\rho \approx 0$).
- $D < 2$ signals positive autocorrelation (errors drift in runs, $\hat\rho > 0$).
- $D > 2$ signals negative autocorrelation (errors alternate in sign).

In words: Durbin-Watson compares how much neighboring residuals differ to how big
the residuals are overall; when neighbors are alike, the numerator is small and
$D$ drops below 2. The exact null distribution depends on the design matrix, so
classic tables give two bounds $d_L$ and $d_U$; modern software returns an exact
p-value instead. This diagnostic is the entry point for @ch15-autocorrelation,
where time-ordered errors are the whole subject and where models are built that
fix the problem rather than just detect it.

## 9.6 The lack-of-fit F test

### Intuition

Every diagnostic so far asks whether the errors misbehave. The lack-of-fit test
asks a different question: is the *shape* of the regression function right at
all? Suppose the true mean of $Y$ bends, but you fit a straight line. Then even
with perfect data the line will miss the true means. The trouble is that ordinary
SSE cannot tell "the model is wrong" from "the data are noisy," because both
inflate it. The way out is replication. If you have several observations at the
*same* predictor value, the spread among them measures pure noise, with no help
from any model. That gives a yardstick of irreducible error to compare the
model's misses against.

The Toluca data are built for this. The 25 runs used only 11 distinct lot sizes,
so most lot sizes were repeated. @fig-ch09-lackfit shows the idea: at each lot
size the runs scatter around their own group mean (pure error), and the group
means scatter around the fitted line (lack of fit).

```{figure} figures/fig_ch09_lackfit.png
:name: fig-ch09-lackfit
:alt: The Toluca scatterplot with the fitted line, the individual runs, and a diamond marking the mean of the runs at each lot size. Short vertical segments connect each group mean to the fitted line. The group means fall very close to the line, so the segments are short.
Lack of fit compares two spreads: the runs around their group means (pure error) and the group means around the line (lack of fit). The Toluca group means hug the line, so lack of fit is small.
```

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 9.12: Pure error and lack-of-fit sums of squares
:class: note definition
Let the predictor take $c$ distinct values, with $n_j$ observations at level $j$
and $n = \sum_j n_j$. The error sum of squares splits as

$$
\underbrace{\mathrm{SSE}}_{\text{df } n-2}
= \underbrace{\mathrm{SSPE}}_{\text{df } n-c}
+ \underbrace{\mathrm{SSLF}}_{\text{df } c-2},
\qquad
\mathrm{SSPE} = \sum_{j=1}^c \sum_{k} (Y_{jk} - \bar Y_j)^2,
\quad
\mathrm{SSLF} = \sum_{j=1}^c n_j (\bar Y_j - \hat Y_j)^2 .
$$

$\mathrm{SSPE}$, the **pure error** sum of squares, is the spread of
observations around their group means $\bar Y_j$ and needs no model;
$\mathrm{SSLF}$, the **lack-of-fit** sum of squares, is the gap between the
group means and the fitted line $\hat Y_j$.
:::

:::{admonition} Theorem 9.13: Lack-of-fit F test
:class: important theorem
Under the simple linear regression model with $c$ distinct predictor levels
($c > 2$) and replication, the lack-of-fit statistic follows an $F$
distribution:

$$
F^\ast = \frac{\mathrm{SSLF}/(c-2)}{\mathrm{SSPE}/(n-c)}
= \frac{\mathrm{MSLF}}{\mathrm{MSPE}} \;\sim\; F(c-2,\, n-c) .
$$

A large $F^\ast$ says the group means stray too far from the line to blame on
noise: the straight-line shape is wrong.
:::

### Derivation (lack of fit as a general linear test)

**Proof.** This is the general
linear test of @ch08-general-linear-test with a specific pair of models. The
**full model** puts a free mean $\mu_j$ at each of the $c$ distinct predictor
levels: $Y_{jk} = \mu_j + \varepsilon_{jk}$. Its least-squares fitted value at
level $j$ is the group mean $\bar Y_j$, so its error sum of squares is exactly
$\mathrm{SSPE} = \sum_j \sum_k (Y_{jk} - \bar Y_j)^2$, with $n - c$ degrees of
freedom because it estimates $c$ means. The **reduced model** is the straight
line $Y_{jk} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_j + \varepsilon_{jk}$, whose error sum of
squares is the ordinary $\mathrm{SSE}$ with $n - 2$ degrees of freedom.

The general linear test statistic is

$$
F^\ast = \frac{[\mathrm{SSE}(R) - \mathrm{SSE}(F)]/[df_R - df_F]}{\mathrm{SSE}(F)/df_F}
= \frac{[\mathrm{SSE} - \mathrm{SSPE}]/[(n-2)-(n-c)]}{\mathrm{SSPE}/(n-c)}
= \frac{\mathrm{SSLF}/(c-2)}{\mathrm{SSPE}/(n-c)} .
$$

That the difference $\mathrm{SSE} - \mathrm{SSPE}$ equals
$\sum_j n_j(\bar Y_j - \hat Y_j)^2$ follows from expanding
$Y_{jk} - \hat Y_j = (Y_{jk} - \bar Y_j) + (\bar Y_j - \hat Y_j)$, squaring, and
summing: the cross term vanishes because $\sum_k (Y_{jk} - \bar Y_j) = 0$ within
each group. Under the reduced model the two mean squares both estimate $\sigma^2$,
so $F^\ast$ is near 1; when the true means depart from a line, only
$\mathrm{MSLF}$ inflates, since $E\{\mathrm{MSPE}\} = \sigma^2$ always. $\blacksquare$

### R and Python

:::{admonition} Example 9.7: Is the Toluca line the right shape?
:class: note

**Intuition.** Fit the line (reduced) and the group-means model (full), then
compare their error sums of squares with the F test.

**Formula.** $F^\ast = \mathrm{MSLF}/\mathrm{MSPE}$ on $c - 2$ and $n - c$
degrees of freedom.

**Computation.**

:class: dropdown

:::{admonition} Durable skill: Separate signal you can reduce from noise you cannot
:class: tip
The lack-of-fit test works by finding a floor of irreducible error (the spread of
repeats) and asking whether the model's misses sit above that floor. That split,
into error the model could remove and error nothing could, is one of the most
transferable ideas in all of statistics. It is the logic behind measurement
repeatability studies, the irreducible-error term in machine learning, and any
honest accounting of "how good could a model possibly get?" Whenever you can
repeat a condition, do, because repeats are the only direct measurement of the
noise you are up against.
:::

## 9.8 Frequently asked questions

**Q1. Is a high-leverage point a bad thing?** Not by itself. A high leverage value
means an unusual predictor row, which is potential to influence the fit, not proof
of harm. A high-leverage point that sits right on the trend set by the others barely
moves the line. A high leverage value becomes a problem only when it pairs with a
large residual, and that combination is what Cook's distance measures.

**Q2. Should I delete an influential point?** Almost never automatically. An
influential point is a signal to investigate, not garbage to discard. Check it for
a data-entry error first. If it is real, the honest report shows the fit with and
without it and explains the difference, as we did with Libya. Deleting real data
to get a cleaner answer is how analyses go wrong.

**Q3. What is the difference between an outlier and an influential point?** An
outlier has a large residual: the model fits it poorly. An influential point
changes the fit when removed. They can occur separately. Zambia is close to an
outlier at an ordinary leverage value, so it is fit poorly but does not run the model.
Libya is influential without an extreme residual, because its leverage value is so high.

**Q4. My residual plot looks fine but Shapiro-Wilk rejects normality. Who wins?**
Read the sample size. In large samples formal tests reject tiny, harmless
departures from normality; in small samples they miss real ones (the Galapagos
Breusch-Pagan p-value of $0.08$ is an example of the miss). Use the plot to judge
whether the departure is big enough to matter, and remember that the t and F
tests are fairly forgiving of mild non-normality.

**Q5. Why divide by $s_{(i)}$ instead of $s$ for the outlier test?** If case $i$
is truly an outlier, including it inflates the ordinary $s$, which sits in the
denominator of $r_i$ and hides the very case you are testing. Estimating the error
standard deviation from the other cases, $s_{(i)}$, removes that self-masking and
gives an exact $t$ distribution for $t_i$.

**Q6. Does Durbin-Watson apply to any regression?** No. It tests correlation
between *consecutive* errors, so it only means something when the rows have a real
order, usually time. Running it on cross-sectional data like the savings countries
is meaningless, because "consecutive" is just alphabetical order. Chapter 15 uses
it where it belongs.

**Q7. Cook's distance has no p-value. How large is too large?** Cook's distance is
a descriptive scale, not a formal test. Common guidance flags $D_i$ near or above
1, but the most useful reading is relative: look for cases that stand far above
the rest in an index plot. Libya at $0.27$ is below 1 yet clearly separated from
the pack, which is enough to warrant a closer look.

**Q8. If a model fails a diagnostic, is it useless?** No. A diagnostic tells you
which assumption broke and therefore which conclusions to distrust. The Galapagos
fit still describes a real association between geography and species richness; what
it cannot support is a normal-theory prediction interval or a claim about a small
island's exact count. Knowing the limits of a model is what lets you use the part
that holds.

## 9.10 Exam practice

These five questions are written in the style of the course exams. Each asks you
to explain your reasoning in full sentences, not just to report a number. Where a
question shows software output, it was produced on the course machine from the
same `data/` files you used all term; you read the numbers off the printout and
interpret them. Work each one before opening the model answer.

**EP 9.1.** The savings model `sr ~ pop15 + pop75 + dpi + ddpi` ($n = 50$,
$p = 5$) is fit, and the residual scales for Chile and Zambia, the two countries
with the largest raw residuals, are printed below along with the Bonferroni
outlier cutoff.

```r
savings <- read.csv("data/savings.csv")
sfit <- lm(sr ~ pop15 + pop75 + dpi + ddpi, data = savings)
n <- nrow(savings); p <- length(coef(sfit))
i <- which(savings$country %in% c("Chile", "Zambia"))
data.frame(country = savings$country[i],
           hii = round(hatvalues(sfit)[i], 3),
           raw_e = round(residuals(sfit)[i], 3),
           standardized = round(rstandard(sfit)[i], 3),
           stud_deleted = round(rstudent(sfit)[i], 3))
qt(1 - 0.05 / (2 * n), n - p - 1)
```
```text
 country   hii  raw_e standardized stud_deleted
   Chile 0.037 -8.242       -2.209       -2.313
  Zambia 0.064  9.751        2.651        2.854
[1] 3.5258
```

Explain why Zambia's residual grows from $2.564$ on the semistudentized scale (its
raw residual $9.751$ divided by $s = 3.803$) to $2.854$ once it is studentized and
deleted, and say which feature of Zambia in the table drives that increase. Then
state whether the outlier test flags Zambia as a genuine outlier, name the number
you compare it against, and explain in one sentence what the Bonferroni correction
protects you from.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The four scales climb because each divides by a smaller, more honest yardstick.
The semistudentized residual divides only by $s$, treating every residual as if it
had the same variance. But Theorem 9.5 says $\operatorname{Var}\{e_i\} = \sigma^2(1 - h_{ii})$,
so Zambia's residual, sitting at leverage value $h = 0.064$, actually has slightly less
than the full error variance; dividing by $s\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}$ instead of $s$
raises the value to the standardized $2.651$. The studentized deleted residual goes
one step further and estimates the error standard deviation from the other 49
countries as $s_{(i)}$, which removes Zambia's own inflation of $s$ and pushes the
value to $2.854$. The leverage value $h = 0.064$ and the self-masking of $s$ are
the two features driving the climb.

For the outlier test you compare the largest studentized deleted residual against
the Bonferroni cutoff $t(1 - \alpha/(2n);\, n - p - 1) = 3.5258$. Zambia's $2.854$
is well below $3.53$, so it is not a significant outlier: it is fit poorly but not
beyond what 50 draws from a clean model can produce. The Bonferroni correction
divides $\alpha$ by $n$ because you are implicitly running one test per case;
without it, scanning 50 residuals for the largest would flag a clean point roughly
one time in every two datasets, and the correction holds the family-wide
false-alarm rate below $\alpha$.

A weak answer just reports "2.854 < 3.53, not an outlier" without explaining that
the studentizing climb comes from dividing by a smaller variance, and without
saying that the Bonferroni cutoff exists precisely because you selected the most
extreme of many cases.
:::

**EP 9.2.** The same savings fit gives the influence summary below for its three
most talked-about countries.

```text
 country   hii rstudent cooksD dffits
   Japan 0.223    1.603  0.143  0.860
  Zambia 0.064    2.854  0.097  0.748
   Libya 0.531   -1.089  0.268 -1.160
   ( 2p/n = 0.200,   DFFITS cutoff 2*sqrt(p/n) = 0.632 )
```

A student writes: "Zambia has the largest studentized residual of any country, so
Zambia is the single point most distorting the savings fit, and I would refit
without it." Evaluate this claim. State whether it is right, correct the reasoning
using the numbers in the table, and name the country that actually most influences
the fit, with the measure that settles it.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The claim is wrong because it confuses a large residual with influence. A residual
measures how badly a point is fit; influence measures how much the fit would move
if the point left, and Definition 9.7 says influence needs two ingredients at once,
an unusual predictor row (a high leverage value) and a poorly fit response (a large residual).
Zambia has the largest studentized residual, $2.854$, but its leverage value is only
$h = 0.064$, close to the average $p/n = 0.10$ and far below the $2p/n = 0.20$
cutoff. A point sitting in an ordinary part of predictor space can miss its own
response badly and still barely tilt the line, because the crowd around it holds
the line in place. That is why Zambia's Cook's distance is a modest $0.097$.

Libya is the influential country. Its leverage value $h = 0.531$ is the highest in the
data, and although its residual is unremarkable ($t = -1.089$), the product of the
two ingredients is what matters: Cook's distance $D_i = (r_i^2/p)\,h_{ii}/(1 - h_{ii})$
combines them, and Libya's $0.268$ is the largest in the data, nearly triple
Zambia's. Its DFFITS of $-1.160$ also clears the $0.632$ cutoff while Zambia's does
not. Cook's distance is the measure that settles it. The honest next step is not to
delete anyone but to refit without Libya and report how much the answer moves, as
Example 9.4 does with the growth coefficient.

A weak answer simply swaps "Zambia" for "Libya" without explaining that influence
is the product of the leverage value and the residual, and so misses why the biggest residual is
not the biggest problem.
:::

**EP 9.3.** For the Galapagos model
`Species ~ Area + Elevation + Nearest + Scruz + Adjacent` ($n = 30$, $p = 6$), a
grader runs the Breusch-Pagan and Shapiro-Wilk tests and inspects the fitted
values.

```r
gala <- read.csv("data/gala.csv")
gfit <- lm(Species ~ Area + Elevation + Nearest + Scruz + Adjacent, data = gala)
library(lmtest)
bptest(gfit)
shapiro.test(residuals(gfit))
range(fitted(gfit)); sum(fitted(gfit) < 0)
```
```text
	studentized Breusch-Pagan test
data:  gfit
BP = 9.7959, df = 5, p-value = 0.08123

	Shapiro-Wilk normality test
data:  residuals(gfit)
W = 0.91351, p-value = 0.01826

[1] -36.38392 386.40356
[1] 5
```

A student concludes: "The Breusch-Pagan p-value is $0.081$, which is above $0.05$,
so the constant-variance assumption holds and the Galapagos model is fine."
Evaluate this conclusion. Explain what the test does and does not show here, bring
in the other evidence on the printout, and state the general rule about tests
versus plots that this case illustrates.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The conclusion is not defensible. The Breusch-Pagan p-value of $0.081$ means the
test fails to reject constant variance at the $5\%$ level, but failing to reject is
not the same as confirming: it can happen because the assumption holds, or because
the test lacks the power to detect a real problem. With only $n = 30$ islands the
test is underpowered, and the residual-versus-fitted plot (@fig-ch09-gala-hook)
shows an obvious funnel, the spread widening as the fitted count grows. When a
formal test and a clear plot disagree, you trust the plot and treat the test as too
weak to overrule it. So the constant-variance assumption is in fact suspect despite
the p-value.

Even setting variance aside, the model is not "fine." The Shapiro-Wilk p-value of
$0.018$ rejects normality of the errors, echoing the heavy tails and Isabela's
plunge in the quantile-quantile plot. And the fitted range runs down to $-36.4$
species, with five islands assigned a negative predicted count, which is impossible
for a count response. A high $R^2$ coexists with a model that is wrong in several
ways at once.

The general rule: a plot and a test answer the same question with different
strengths. On a small sample a test can miss a departure a plot makes obvious; on a
large sample it can flag a departure too small to matter. Read the picture, and let
the test sharpen rather than replace your judgment.

A weak answer stops at "p > 0.05 so variance is constant" and never notices the
funnel plot, the rejected normality test, or the impossible negative predictions.
:::

**EP 9.4.** Continuing with the Galapagos fit, an analyst is worried that Isabela,
the largest island, is running the whole model. The fit is refit with Isabela
removed and the coefficients compared.

```r
iso <- which(gala$island == "Isabela")
c(h = hatvalues(gfit)[iso], rstudent = rstudent(gfit)[iso],
  cooksD = cooks.distance(gfit)[iso])
gfit2 <- lm(Species ~ Area + Elevation + Nearest + Scruz + Adjacent,
            data = gala[-iso, ])
round(rbind(all = coef(gfit), no_Isabela = coef(gfit2)), 4)
c(R2_all = summary(gfit)$r.squared, R2_no_Isabela = summary(gfit2)$r.squared)
```
```text
       h.16 rstudent.16   cooksD.16
     0.9685     -5.3337     68.0764
           (Intercept)    Area Elevation Nearest   Scruz Adjacent
all             7.0682 -0.0239    0.3195  0.0091 -0.2405  -0.0748
no_Isabela     22.5861  0.2957    0.1404 -0.2552 -0.0901  -0.0650
   R2_all R2_no_Isabela
```

Interpret this output in context. Say what would change in the reported conclusions
about `Area` and `Elevation` if Isabela were dropped, explain why one island can do
this using its three diagnostic numbers, and state what the honest way to report a
result this fragile is.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
Isabela is both an extreme predictor row and a badly fit response, the combination
that produces heavy influence. Its leverage value is $h = 0.969$, almost the maximum
possible value of 1 and far above the $2p/n = 0.40$ cutoff, so the fit is nearly
pinned to Isabela's own species count. Its studentized deleted residual is $-5.33$,
past the Bonferroni cutoff of about $3.56$, so it is also a genuine outlier. Cook's
distance multiplies the two ingredients and comes out at $68.1$, off any reasonable
scale, which is the single-number warning that this one island dominates the fit.

Dropping Isabela changes the story qualitatively, not just by a little. The `Area`
coefficient flips sign, from $-0.0239$ to $+0.2957$: with Isabela in, area appears
to lower species count, and without it, area raises the count, which is the sign you
would expect biologically. The `Elevation` coefficient falls from $0.3195$ to
$0.1404$, less than half its former size. Meanwhile $R^2$ rises from $0.766$ to
$0.871$, because the single hardest island to fit is gone. Any conclusion about how
area or elevation drives species richness rests on one island.

The honest report does not quietly delete Isabela and present the cleaner fit as if
it were the whole story. You first check Isabela for a data error; finding it real,
you report the fit both with and without the island and explain that the sign of the
area effect depends on it, so the reader can judge. Deleting real data to get a
tidier answer is how analyses mislead.

A weak answer notes only that "the coefficients change" and that $R^2$ improves,
without flagging the sign flip on `Area`, without tying the influence to all three
diagnostics, and without insisting that both fits be reported rather than the case
silently dropped.
:::

**EP 9.5.** The Toluca data record labor `hours` against `lotsize` for 25
production runs that used only 11 distinct lot sizes. The lack-of-fit F test
compares the straight-line fit to a model with a free mean at each lot size.

```r
toluca <- read.csv("data/toluca.csv")
reduced <- lm(hours ~ lotsize, data = toluca)
full    <- lm(hours ~ factor(lotsize), data = toluca)
anova(reduced, full)
```
```text
Analysis of Variance Table

Model 1: hours ~ lotsize
Model 2: hours ~ factor(lotsize)
  Res.Df   RSS Df Sum of Sq      F Pr(>F)
1     23 54825
2     14 37581  9     17245 0.7138 0.6893
```

Interpret this output in context: identify which printed number is the pure-error
sum of squares and which is the lack-of-fit sum of squares, state the conclusion of
the test in plain language, and explain what the test is comparing to reach it. Then
answer: what would change about your ability to run this test if every one of the 25
runs had used a different lot size?

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The pure-error sum of squares is the residual sum of squares of the full
group-means model, $\mathrm{SSPE} = 37581$ on $14$ degrees of freedom, because the
free-mean-per-lot-size model fits each group at its own mean and its leftover is
pure noise among repeats. The lack-of-fit sum of squares is the "Sum of Sq" column,
$\mathrm{SSLF} = 17245$ on $9$ degrees of freedom, the extra error the straight line
carries beyond that noise floor, that is the gap between the group means and the
line. Together they split the line's $\mathrm{SSE} = 54825$ into pure error plus
lack of fit.

The test compares two yardsticks of spread: the runs around their own group means
(pure error, which no model can remove) against the group means around the fitted
line (lack of fit, which a wrong shape would inflate). Here $F^\ast = \mathrm{MSLF}/\mathrm{MSPE} = 0.71$
with a p-value of $0.69$, so there is no evidence of lack of fit. In plain language,
the group means hug the straight line no more loosely than pure noise alone would
explain, so the straight-line shape the Toluca engineers relied on is vindicated.

If all 25 runs had used distinct lot sizes there would be no replication, so every
group would contain a single observation, its group mean would equal that
observation, and $\mathrm{SSPE}$ would be zero on $n - c = 25 - 25 = 0$ degrees of
freedom. With no pure-error estimate of $\sigma^2$ the denominator $\mathrm{MSPE}$
is undefined and the test cannot be run at all. Replication is exactly what buys you
a model-free measurement of the noise, and without repeats there is no way to
separate a wrong shape from ordinary scatter.

A weak answer reads off "p = 0.69, no lack of fit" but cannot say which number is
SSPE and which is SSLF, cannot explain the two spreads being compared, and does not
see that the test collapses entirely without replicated predictor values.
:::

:::{admonition} Resumen del capítulo (en español)
:class: dropdown
Este capítulo trata la etapa **CHECK (verificar)** del flujo de trabajo: una vez
ajustado un modelo, hay que comprobar si merece confianza antes de usarlo. Se
apoya en dos casos reales. El modelo de especies de las islas Galápagos (`gala`)
tiene un $R^2$ alto pero falla casi toda prueba: la varianza crece con el valor
ajustado y predice conteos negativos, con la isla **Isabela** como caso extremo.
El modelo de ahorro entre países (`savings`) cumple los supuestos, pero su
ajuste depende de un solo país, **Libia**.

La **influencia** de un punto combina dos ingredientes: un **apalancamiento**
$h_{ii}$ alto, que mide lo inusual de sus predictores, y un residuo grande.
Derivamos las propiedades de $h_{ii}$ a partir de la matriz sombrero: está entre
0 y 1 y suma $p$. Como la varianza del residuo crudo depende de $h_{ii}$, ese
residuo engaña en puntos de alto apalancamiento, así que lo estandarizamos. El
**residuo eliminado studentizado** prueba si un caso es un **valor atípico** con
una corrección de Bonferroni; Isabela resulta un atípico claro.

La **distancia de Cook** resume la influencia combinando el residuo y el
apalancamiento; se deriva del cambio en los coeficientes al eliminar un caso.
En `savings`, Libia desplaza notablemente el coeficiente de crecimiento `ddpi`
al quitarla; en `gala`, Isabela domina por completo. **DFFITS** y **DFBETAS**
afinan el diagnóstico caso por caso.

Los gráficos de residuos, el cuantil-cuantil normal y el escala-ubicación
revelan de un vistazo problemas de forma, normalidad y varianza. Las pruebas
formales (Breusch-Pagan, Brown-Forsythe, Shapiro-Wilk) los confirman, y
**Durbin-Watson** detecta autocorrelación en datos ordenados en el tiempo, tema
que retoma el Capítulo 15. Finalmente, la **prueba F de falta de ajuste** usa el
error puro de observaciones repetidas para juzgar si la forma lineal es
correcta; para Toluca, la recta resulta adecuada.
:::

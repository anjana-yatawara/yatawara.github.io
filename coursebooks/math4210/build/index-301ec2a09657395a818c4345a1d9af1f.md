# 11. Categorical predictors and interactions

:::{div}
:class: lang-toggle
[Leer en espanol](./es.md)
:::

In 2008 a US college pulled together a table of its faculty: for each of 397 professors, the
table recorded rank, academic discipline, years since the PhD, years of service, sex, and the
nine-month academic salary. The table was assembled for a specific reason, to monitor whether
men and women were paid differently. That is a question with real stakes, and it is also a
question that a single number cannot answer well. The average male salary in this dataset is
about \$115,090 and the average female salary is about \$101,002, a raw gap of roughly
\$14,088. Is that gap the whole story?

Almost certainly not, because salary depends on more than one thing. It depends heavily on
rank: full professors out-earn assistant professors by a wide margin, as @fig-ch11-salary-rank
shows. It depends on discipline and on years of service. If the mix of ranks, disciplines, and
experience differs between the two groups, then a raw average difference blends the effect of
sex with the effects of everything else. To say anything careful, we need a model that can hold
those other variables fixed. But rank, discipline, and sex are not numbers. They are
categories, and so far every predictor in this book has been a measured quantity like lot size
or years. This chapter is about putting categories into a regression.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_salary_rank.png
:name: fig-ch11-salary-rank
:alt: A boxplot with jittered points of academic salary in thousands of dollars for three faculty ranks, Assistant, Associate, and Full. Salaries climb from a tight cluster around 80 thousand for Assistant professors, to about 95 thousand for Associate, to a wide spread centered near 127 thousand for Full professors. Red diamonds mark the group means at each rank.
Nine-month academic salary rises steeply with rank, from a mean near 81k for assistant professors to about 127k for full professors. A predictor with three named categories like this one cannot go into a regression as a single number; this chapter shows how to encode it.
```

By the end you will be able to turn a categorical predictor into indicator variables and read
each coefficient. You will combine a category with a continuous predictor to fit parallel or
separate lines, test which fit the data supports, and model curvature with polynomials the right
way. And you will see that the one-way analysis of variance you may remember from intro stats is
just a regression on indicators. The salary data carries us through the categorical part; the
classic `cars` stopping-distance data carries us through the polynomial part.

:::{admonition} This lesson at a glance
:class: important
- **What we are doing:** Putting categorical predictors into regression with indicator coding, combining a category with a continuous predictor for parallel or separate lines, and adding interactions and polynomials.
- **Why we are doing it:** Salary depends on rank, discipline, and sex, which are categories, not numbers, and a raw male-minus-female gap blends sex with rank and experience, so we need a model that can hold the others fixed.
- **Main objective:** Encode a $k$-level factor with $k-1$ indicators, read each coefficient as a difference of group means or of slopes, and see that one-way analysis of variance is regression on indicators.
- **What changed from the last chapters:** Every predictor so far was a measured quantity; from here predictors can be categories, curves, and their combinations, still inside the least-squares framework of @ch07-ls-matrix and tested with the general linear test of @ch08-general-linear-test.
:::

:::{admonition} Learning objectives
:class: tip
By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- **Interpret** each coefficient of an indicator-coded model as a difference of group means, and explain what the intercept means.
- **Fit** and **interpret** an ANCOVA-style model with one categorical and one continuous predictor as parallel lines.
- **Interpret** an interaction coefficient as a difference of slopes, and **diagnose** the common wrong reading of the main-effect coefficient that comes with it.
- **Test** whether two or more groups share a slope using the general linear test of @ch08-general-linear-test.

- **Show** that one-way analysis of variance is regression on indicator variables, and connect its $F$ test to the regression $F$ test.

## 11.1 From categories to numbers: indicator coding

### Intuition

A regression equation multiplies each predictor by a number, so a predictor has to *be* a
number. "Rank" is not: its values are the words Assistant, Associate, and Full. You might be
tempted to code them 1, 2, 3 and feed that in. Resist. Coding rank as 1, 2, 3 forces a specific
and probably false claim on the data. It says the salary step from Assistant to Associate equals
the step from Associate to Full, because both are a move of one unit on your invented scale. The
categories have an order, but nothing says the gaps are equal, and a nominal predictor like
discipline or sex has no order at all.

The honest way to carry a category into a regression is with **indicator variables** (Definition
11.1), also called dummy variables: 0-or-1 columns, each answering a yes-or-no question ("is this professor
a full professor?"). Indicators let each category have its own level, free of any assumption
about spacing. The only subtlety is that you do not use one indicator per category. You use one
fewer, and let the left-out category set the baseline. @fig-ch11-dummy-means shows the idea for
the three ranks: one group becomes the baseline, and the coefficients measure how far each other
group sits above it.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_dummy_means.png
:name: fig-ch11-dummy-means
:alt: Three group means for salary by rank plotted as dots, Assistant at 80.8 thousand, Associate at 93.9, and Full at 126.8. A dashed horizontal line sits at the Assistant mean and is labeled b-zero equals 80.8. Red arrows point up from that baseline to the Associate dot, labeled b-one equals plus 13.1, and to the Full dot, labeled b-two equals plus 46.0.
Reference-cell coding for rank. The intercept is the reference group's mean (Assistant, 80.8k), and each coefficient is the vertical gap from that baseline up to another group's mean: +13.1k to Associate, +46.0k to Full.
```

:::{admonition} Definition 11.1: Indicator (dummy) variable
:class: note definition
An **indicator variable** (or **dummy variable**) is a column that equals $1$ when a case belongs
to a specified category and $0$ otherwise. It turns a yes-or-no category membership into a number a
regression can multiply by a coefficient.
:::

### Formula

Suppose a categorical predictor (a **factor**, Definition 11.2) has $k$ levels. Pick one level as the
**reference** (or baseline). For each of the other $k-1$ levels, define an indicator variable
that is 1 when a case is in that level and 0 otherwise. For the three ranks, taking Assistant
as the reference, define

$$
X_1 = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if Associate} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}, \qquad
X_2 = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if Full} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}.
$$

- $X_1$ is the Associate indicator: 1 for associate professors, 0 for everyone else.
- $X_2$ is the Full indicator: 1 for full professors, 0 for everyone else.
- There is no indicator for Assistant. An assistant professor has $X_1 = X_2 = 0$; the two
  zeros *are* the code for the reference level.

@fig-ch11-encoding shows this coding at work on the first few professors in the data: the
single column of rank words becomes two 0-or-1 columns, and the assistant professor's row is
just a pair of zeros.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_encoding.png
:name: fig-ch11-encoding
:alt: A table with a left column labeled rank in words, listing Full, Full, Assistant, Full, Full, Associate for six professors, and an arrow labeled encode pointing to two indicator columns on the right, the Associate indicator X-one and the Full indicator X-two. Each Full row shows 0 and 1, each Associate row shows 1 and 0, and the Assistant row shows 0 and 0, marked with a red note reading Assistant equals 0 0, both zeros are its code.
Reference-cell coding in action. One column of category words becomes $k-1$ indicator columns of zeros and ones. A full professor is coded (0, 1) and an associate (1, 0); the assistant professor gets no column of its own, and its pair of zeros is the code for the reference level.
```

:::{admonition} Definition 11.2: Factor
:class: note definition
A **factor** is a categorical predictor: a predictor whose values are named categories (its
**levels**) rather than measured quantities. A factor with $k$ levels enters a regression through
$k-1$ indicator variables, never as a single number.
:::

The model with these two indicators is

$$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{i1} + \beta_2 X_{i2} + \varepsilon_i .
$$

In words: the mean salary is a baseline $\beta_0$, plus an extra $\beta_1$ if the professor is
an associate, plus an extra $\beta_2$ if the professor is full. Read the mean response one
level at a time by plugging in the indicators:

$$
E\{Y \mid \text{Assistant}\} = \beta_0, \qquad
E\{Y \mid \text{Associate}\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1, \qquad
E\{Y \mid \text{Full}\} = \beta_0 + \beta_2 .
$$

So $\beta_0$ is the mean of the reference group, $\beta_1$ is the Associate-minus-Assistant
difference in means, and $\beta_2$ is the Full-minus-Assistant difference. The coefficients are
contrasts against the baseline, nothing more.

This is **reference-cell coding** (Definition 11.3; R and Python call it treatment coding). It is
the default in both languages, and the one this book uses throughout. Chapter 13 will code the
predictors of a logistic regression exactly the same way (@ch11-dummy-coding is the pattern it
reuses).

:::{admonition} Definition 11.3: Reference-cell coding
:class: note definition
**Reference-cell coding** (also called treatment coding) represents a $k$-level factor by choosing
one level as the reference and defining $k-1$ indicators for the other levels, while keeping the
intercept. The intercept is then the reference group's mean, and each coefficient is a group's mean
minus the reference mean.
:::

### Derivation (why $k-1$ indicators, not $k$)

:::{admonition} Theorem 11.4: A $k$-level factor needs $k-1$ indicators
:class: important theorem
Consider a factor with $k$ levels. If the design matrix holds the intercept column of ones together
with all $k$ level indicators $X_1,\dots,X_k$, then

$$
X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_k = \mathbf{1},
$$

so the columns are linearly dependent, $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is singular, and the least-squares
coefficients are not unique. Dropping one indicator (reference-cell coding) restores full column
rank and makes the coefficients identifiable.
:::

The reason is easier to see than to say. @fig-ch11-rank-deficient lays out the design matrix with
the intercept column and all three rank indicators. In every row exactly one indicator is a 1, so
adding the three indicator columns together rebuilds the intercept column of ones. One column is
then a copy of the others combined, which is the linear dependence that sinks the fit.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_rank_deficient.png
:name: fig-ch11-rank-deficient
:alt: A table with an intercept column of all ones on the left, and three indicator columns on the right, one for Assistant, Associate, and Full. In each of the six rows exactly one of the three indicators is 1 and the other two are 0. A red bracket under the three indicator columns is labeled add these three columns arrow a column of all ones, with an equals sign pointing back to the intercept column. A caption above reads X-Asst plus X-Assoc plus X-Full equals one, redundant with the intercept.
Keeping all $k$ indicators alongside the intercept is redundant. Because every professor holds exactly one rank, the three indicator columns add up row by row to the intercept column of ones. That linear dependence makes $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ singular, so we drop one indicator and let its level become the baseline.
```

**Proof.** Suppose you stubbornly
include all $k$ indicators, $X_1, \dots, X_k$, one per level, alongside the intercept column of
ones. Every case belongs to exactly one level, so exactly one of its indicators is 1 and the
rest are 0. Adding the indicator columns across all levels therefore reconstructs the all-ones
column:

$$
X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_k = \mathbf{1} .
$$

That is a linear dependence among the columns of the design matrix $\mathbf{X}$. From
@ch07-ls-matrix, the least-squares estimate $\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}$
needs $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ to be invertible, which requires the columns of $\mathbf{X}$ to be
linearly independent (full column rank). With the intercept plus all $k$ indicators, they are
not: the coefficients are not uniquely determined, because you could add any constant to
$\beta_0$ and subtract it from every level's effect and get the identical fitted values. Drop
one indicator (equivalently, drop the intercept) and the dependence is gone. Reference-cell
coding drops the reference level's indicator, keeps the intercept, and gives a full-rank design
with $k-1$ indicators. $\blacksquare$

This "one fewer than the number of levels" fact has a name you will meet again: a factor with
$k$ levels contributes $k-1$ degrees of freedom to the model.

:::{admonition} Example 11.1: Salary by rank with reference-cell coding
:class: note
**Question.** Fit salary on rank alone. What does each coefficient mean, and do the coefficients

**Intuition.** With Assistant as the reference, the intercept should be the assistant-professor
mean and each coefficient should be a difference of group means.

**Formula.** $\beta_0 = E\{Y \mid \text{Assistant}\}$, $\beta_1 = E\{Y\mid\text{Associate}\} - E\{Y\mid\text{Assistant}\}$,
$\beta_2 = E\{Y\mid\text{Full}\} - E\{Y\mid\text{Assistant}\}$.

**Computation.**

```r
salaries$rank <- factor(salaries$rank,
                        levels = c("AsstProf", "AssocProf", "Prof"))
```

In Python, statsmodels reads the same file. We make rank an ordered category so Assistant is the
reference, then fit with the `C()` categorical marker.

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from statsmodels.formula.api import ols

salaries = pd.read_csv("data/salaries.csv")
salaries["rank"] = pd.Categorical(
    salaries["rank"], categories=["AsstProf", "AssocProf", "Prof"]
)
fit_rank = ols("salary ~ C(rank)", data=salaries).fit()
print(fit_rank.params)
print(salaries.groupby("rank", observed=True)["salary"].mean())
```

**Interpretation.** The intercept, \$80,776, is exactly the assistant-professor mean. The
coefficient on the Associate indicator, \$13,100, is exactly the associate mean minus the

mean minus the assistant mean ($126{,}772 - 80{,}776$). The regression has not invented
anything: with a single factor it simply reports each group's mean, re-expressed as a baseline

the reference, so reading the coefficient table is just reading group means.
:::

:::{admonition} A note on other codings
:class: note
Reference-cell coding is not the only choice. **Effects coding** (sometimes called sum-to-zero
coding) uses $-1/0/+1$ columns so that the intercept becomes the grand mean and each coefficient
measures a level's departure from that grand mean. **Cell-means coding** drops the intercept and
uses all $k$ indicators, so each coefficient is a group mean outright. All three give the
identical fitted values, residuals, and $F$ tests; they differ only in what the individual
coefficients mean. This book uses reference-cell coding because "difference from a baseline" is
usually the most natural thing to report, but if you ever see an intercept described as a grand
mean, someone has switched codings on you.
:::

::::{admonition} Try it 11.1
:class: important
A colleague codes discipline as a single numeric column, `A = 0` and `B = 1`, and fits
`salary ~ discipline_num`. A second colleague codes three ranks as `1, 2, 3` in one numeric
column. Which fit is harmless and which one imposes a false assumption? Explain.

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
The two-level discipline coding is harmless. A single 0/1 column *is* the reference-cell
indicator for a two-level factor: the intercept is the mean of discipline A and the slope is the
B-minus-A difference. With only two levels there is nothing to misspace.

The three-rank coding `1, 2, 3` is the dangerous one. Using a single numeric column forces the
model to treat the Assistant-to-Associate step and the Associate-to-Full step as equal (each is
"+1 unit," multiplied by the same slope). The data flatly reject that: the first step is about
13k and the second is about 33k. Ranks need two indicators, not one numeric code, so each step
can be its own size.
:::
::::

:::{admonition} Durable skill: Recognize when a number is really a label
:class: tip
Zip codes, jersey numbers, Likert responses, product SKUs, and rank codes all look like numbers
and arrive in numeric columns, but arithmetic on them is meaningless: zip code 93311 is not
"more" than 90210. Before you put any column into a model, ask whether its numbers measure a
quantity or just name a category. If they name a category, encode it as a factor and let the
software build indicators. This habit prevents a whole class of silent modeling errors, and it
transfers to every dataset you will ever touch, in any field.
:::

## 11.2 One categorical and one continuous predictor

### Intuition

A category by itself just sorts cases into groups. The interesting models mix a category with a
measured predictor. Salary, for instance, depends both on discipline (a category, theoretical A
or applied B) and on years of service (a number). We now have two knobs, and there are three
increasingly flexible ways to turn them, drawn schematically in @fig-ch11-three-models.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_three_models.png
:name: fig-ch11-three-models
:alt: Three side-by-side panels each showing a response Y against a continuous predictor X for two groups A and B, drawn as a solid line and a dashed line. Left panel, common line: the two lines lie exactly on top of each other. Middle panel, parallel lines: the two lines have the same slope but the dashed group-B line is shifted up. Right panel, separate slopes: the two lines start close together but the dashed line rises more steeply and they fan apart.
The three models for one continuous and one categorical predictor. Common line ignores the group; parallel lines give each group its own intercept but a shared slope; separate slopes give each group its own slope as well, which is what an interaction term does.
```

The simplest model ignores the category and fits one line to everybody. The next model, the
subject of this section, gives each discipline its own intercept but makes them share a single
slope: two **parallel lines**. Reading it is easy: experience is worth the same number of
dollars per year in both disciplines, and one discipline simply sits a fixed amount above the
other at every experience level. This parallel-lines model has a traditional name,
**analysis of covariance** or ANCOVA (Definition 11.5), from the days when the continuous predictor
was called a covariate and the category was the treatment. The third model, separate slopes, is the next
section.

### Formula

Let $X$ be years of service and let $D$ be the discipline-B indicator ($D = 1$ for applied,
$D = 0$ for theoretical). The parallel-lines model is

$$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 D_i + \varepsilon_i .
$$

In words: salary is a baseline, plus a per-year effect of service that both disciplines share,
plus a fixed bump for being in discipline B.

- $\beta_1$ is the slope on years of service, shared by both disciplines.
- $\beta_2$ is the vertical gap between the two discipline lines, the same at every $X$.

Split it by discipline to see the two lines:

$$
\text{Theoretical } (D=0): \quad E\{Y\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X, \\
\text{Applied } (D=1): \quad E\{Y\} = (\beta_0 + \beta_2) + \beta_1 X .
$$

In words: both lines have slope $\beta_1$; the applied line is shifted up by $\beta_2$. The two
lines never cross, because they are parallel. The reading of $\beta_2$ is a genuine
"holding years of service fixed" comparison: at any given experience, applied faculty are paid
$\beta_2$ more on average, exactly the kind of adjusted comparison the chapter opened by asking
for.

:::{admonition} Definition 11.5: Analysis of covariance (ANCOVA)
:class: note definition
An **analysis of covariance (ANCOVA)** model has one categorical and one continuous predictor and
no interaction,

$$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 D_i + \varepsilon_i .
$$

It gives each category its own intercept but a single shared slope, so the fitted lines are
parallel and $\beta_2$ is the constant vertical gap between them.
:::

## 11.3 Interactions: letting the slopes differ

### Intuition

The parallel model makes a strong assumption: a year of service is worth the same in both
disciplines. Maybe it is not. Maybe applied faculty, whose skills track a hot outside job
market, gain more per year than theoretical faculty. If so, the two lines should have different
slopes, fanning apart as service grows rather than staying a fixed distance apart. Letting the
slopes differ is what an **interaction** (Definition 11.6) does. An interaction between a category
and a continuous predictor says the effect of the continuous predictor depends on the category.

You build it by adding one more column: the product of the continuous predictor and the
indicator. That product is zero for the reference group (so its line is unchanged) and equals
the continuous predictor for the other group (so that group gets an extra slope term).

### Formula

With $X$ = years of service and $D$ = discipline-B indicator, the **interaction model** is

$$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 D_i + \beta_3 (X_i D_i) + \varepsilon_i .
$$

In words: take the parallel-lines model and add one product term, which lets discipline B have
its own slope instead of sharing the baseline slope.

- $\beta_3$ is the interaction coefficient, the coefficient on the product $X D$.
- The product term $X_i D_i$ is $0$ when $D_i = 0$ and equals $X_i$ when $D_i = 1$.

Split by discipline again:

$$
\text{Theoretical } (D=0): \quad E\{Y\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X, \\
\text{Applied } (D=1): \quad E\{Y\} = (\beta_0 + \beta_2) + (\beta_1 + \beta_3) X .
$$

In words: the theoretical line has intercept $\beta_0$ and slope $\beta_1$; the applied line has
intercept $\beta_0 + \beta_2$ and slope $\beta_1 + \beta_3$. So $\beta_2$ is the gap between the
two intercepts (the difference at $X = 0$), and $\beta_3$ is the gap between the two slopes (the
extra dollars per year that applied faculty gain over theoretical faculty). If $\beta_3 = 0$ the
slopes are equal and we are back to parallel lines, which is why testing $\beta_3 = 0$ is the
test for "do the slopes differ."

:::{admonition} Definition 11.6: Interaction
:class: note definition
An **interaction** is a product term added to a model so that the effect of one predictor depends on
the value of another. For a continuous predictor $X$ and an indicator $D$, the interaction term
$X D$ gives each category its own slope: the model

$$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 D_i + \beta_3 X_i D_i + \varepsilon_i
$$

has slope $\beta_1$ when $D = 0$ and slope $\beta_1 + \beta_3$ when $D = 1$. In a model containing an
interaction, a **main effect** coefficient is the effect only where the interacting variable equals
zero.
:::

### The wrong reading, first

Here is where careful reading matters, because the interaction model sets a trap. Look at the
fitted coefficients (Example 11.3 below computes them): the discipline coefficient $\beta_2$
comes out to about \$857 with a large standard error and a p-value of 0.86, nowhere near
significant. A hurried reader concludes: "discipline does not matter for salary." That reading
is wrong, and the mistake is worth dwelling on.

In the interaction model, $\beta_2$ is *not* "the effect of discipline." It is only the gap
between the disciplines at $X = 0$, that is, for a professor with zero years of service. At zero
service the two lines nearly meet, so of course that particular gap is small and uncertain. But
the lines are not parallel; the applied line climbs faster. Out where the actual faculty live,
at 20 or 30 years of service, the disciplines are far apart. @fig-ch11-interaction-anatomy makes
the point: $\beta_2$ measures the gap at the far-left edge of the plot, which says nothing about
the gap in the body of the data.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_interaction_anatomy.png
:name: fig-ch11-interaction-anatomy
:alt: Two fitted salary lines against years of service, blue for theoretical A and dashed orange for applied B. At X equals zero on the far left the two lines nearly touch, and a small red double arrow labels the gap there as b-two equals 0.9k, not significant. At 40 years of service a tall green double arrow shows a much larger gap of 28.7k between the lines, computed as 0.9 plus 0.695 times 40.
The trap in reading an interaction model. The discipline coefficient b-two is only the vertical gap at X = 0 (red arrow, tiny and not significant). Because the slopes differ, the gap out in the data is large (green arrow, 28.7k at 40 years). A small, non-significant b-two does not mean the groups are alike.
```

The correct statement is: the interaction coefficient $\beta_3$ is significant, so the slopes
differ, and the discipline gap grows with service. The gap at a specific service level $X$ is
$\beta_2 + \beta_3 X$, not $\beta_2$ alone. Whenever a model contains an interaction, the
coefficient on a main effect is a conditional statement about the point where the other
predictor is zero, never a blanket "effect."

will save you from wrong conclusions in fields from medicine to marketing, wherever "the effect
of A depends on B" is the actual finding.

## 11.4 Polynomial regression and centering

### Intuition

Categories are one way a straight line can fail to fit; curvature is another. Stopping distance
does not grow in a straight line with speed. Physics says braking distance rises with the square
of speed, because kinetic energy does, and the classic `cars` data (50 cars from the 1920s)
shows exactly the upward bend, drawn in @fig-ch11-cars-fits. A straight line undershoots at the
high speeds where the curve turns up. These are the same `cars` data whose residual funnel drove
the square-root transformation in @ch10-box-cox; there we reshaped the response, and here we
leave it alone and let the mean function bend instead.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_cars_fits.png
:name: fig-ch11-cars-fits
:alt: Scatterplot of stopping distance in feet against speed in miles per hour for fifty cars, with two fitted curves. A blue straight line rises steadily, and a red quadratic curve bends gently upward, sitting slightly higher than the line at the highest speeds and slightly higher at the very lowest speeds. The scatter of points widens as speed increases.
Stopping distance against speed with a straight line (blue) and a quadratic curve (red). The quadratic bends upward to match the physics that distance grows faster than linearly with speed, though for these data the improvement over the line is modest.
```

We can bend a regression line by adding the square of the predictor as a second term. A
**polynomial regression** (Definition 11.7) keeps the model linear in its coefficients (so all the
least-squares machinery still applies) while making the fitted curve a parabola, or a cubic, or
higher. The one practical wrinkle is that a predictor and its square are usually strongly related
to each other, which makes the raw coefficients unstable and hard to read. The fix is **centering**
(Definition 11.8): subtract the mean of the predictor before squaring.

### Formula

The **second-order (quadratic) polynomial model** in a predictor $X$ is

$$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 X_i^2 + \varepsilon_i .
$$

In words: the straight-line model with one extra column, the square of the predictor, which lets
the fitted curve bend instead of staying straight.

- $\beta_2$ controls the curvature: positive bends the parabola upward, negative bends it down.
- The model is still a linear model, because it is linear in $\beta_0, \beta_1, \beta_2$; the
  predictor $X^2$ is just another column.

:::{admonition} Definition 11.7: Polynomial regression
:class: note definition
A **polynomial regression** adds powers of a predictor ($X^2, X^3, \dots$) as extra columns so the
fitted curve can bend, while the model stays linear in its coefficients. The second-order
(quadratic) model is

$$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \beta_2 X_i^2 + \varepsilon_i ,
$$

with $\beta_2$ controlling the curvature.
:::

The **centered** version replaces $X$ by $x = X - \bar X$:

$$
Y_i = \beta_0^{\ast} + \beta_1^{\ast} x_i + \beta_2^{\ast} x_i^2 + \varepsilon_i,
\qquad x_i = X_i - \bar X .
$$

In words: measure the predictor as a deviation from its average before squaring. Centering does
not change the shape of the fitted curve or any fitted value. It only shifts what the linear and
intercept terms mean, and it sharply reduces the correlation between the first-order and
second-order columns. After centering, $\beta_0^{\ast}$ is the fitted mean at the average speed
and $\beta_1^{\ast}$ is the slope of the curve at the average speed, both readable numbers.

:::{admonition} Definition 11.8: Centering
:class: note definition
**Centering** a predictor means replacing $X$ by the deviation $x = X - \bar X$ before forming its
powers. Centering leaves every fitted value unchanged, but it sharply reduces the correlation
between the linear and squared columns, which makes the coefficients stable and interpretable.
:::

### Why centering helps

The raw predictor `speed` runs from 4 to 25, so `speed` and `speed^2` both increase together
across that whole range; their correlation in the `cars` data is 0.98. Two columns that move
almost in lockstep carry nearly the same information, and the regression struggles to divide
credit between them: the individual coefficients get large standard errors and can even flip
sign. Subtracting the mean recenters the predictor on zero, so its square becomes small in the
middle and large at both ends, a U-shape that no longer tracks the linear term.
@fig-ch11-centering shows the correlation collapsing from 0.98 to about $-0.10$ after centering.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_centering.png
:name: fig-ch11-centering
:alt: Two side-by-side scatterplots. Left, raw speed on the horizontal axis against speed squared on the vertical axis, the points falling almost perfectly along an upward curve, labeled r equals 0.98. Right, centered speed against centered speed squared, the points forming a U shape scattered around a nearly flat trend, labeled r equals minus 0.10.
Centering breaks the near-perfect correlation between a predictor and its square. On the raw scale speed and speed-squared move together (r = 0.98); after subtracting the mean, the centered predictor and its square are nearly uncorrelated (r = -0.10), which stabilizes the polynomial's coefficients.
```

### The hierarchy principle and choosing the degree

Two rules keep polynomial models sane.

First, the **hierarchy principle** (Definition 11.9): if a model includes a higher-order term, keep
all the lower-order terms below it, even if they look non-significant. A model with $X^2$ but no $X$
forces the parabola's vertex to sit at $X = 0$. That is an arbitrary constraint the data never
asked for, and it is not even preserved if you shift the predictor's origin. Keep $X$ whenever
you keep $X^2$. The same logic says keep both main effects whenever you keep their interaction,
which is why the interaction models in section 11.3 always carried both `yrs.service` and
`discipline`.

:::{admonition} Definition 11.9: Hierarchy principle
:class: note definition
The **hierarchy principle** says that if a model includes a higher-order term, it should keep all
lower-order terms beneath it: keep $X$ whenever $X^2$ is present, and keep both main effects
whenever their interaction is present.
:::

Second, **do not chase degree**. Each extra power bends the curve more and will always fit the
sample a little better, but high-degree polynomials wiggle wildly between and beyond the data
points. @fig-ch11-poly-degree fits degrees 1, 2, and 5 to the `cars` data: the quadratic tracks
the gentle bend, while the degree-5 curve starts chasing individual points and swings at the
ends, the classic face of overfitting. Test whether the added curvature is real rather than
reading individual high-order coefficients.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_poly_degree.png

Fitting polynomials of degree 1, 2, and 5 to the same fifty points. The quadratic captures the real upward bend; the degree-5 curve overfits, wiggling between points and swinging at the ends where data is sparse. More flexibility is not more truth.
```

50 cars the upward bend is suggestive but not decisive, and a straight line is defensible. The
honest report is that the curvature is in the expected direction but not established at
conventional significance, exactly the kind of judgment call this course asks you to make and
state rather than hide. Second, both the raw and centered models predict the identical stopping
distance of 60.72 feet at 20 mph, confirming that centering is a change of parametrization, not a
change of model.

### A brief look at piecewise regression

Polynomials bend a single smooth curve across the whole range. Sometimes a relationship instead
changes slope at a particular point: a dose that helps up to a threshold and hurts after, a tax
rate that kicks in at an income level. For these you can fit **piecewise linear regression**
(Definition 11.10), two or more straight segments joined at chosen points called **knots**. The
trick is one extra column: for a knot at $c$, add the "hinge" predictor $(X - c)_+$, which equals
$X - c$ when $X > c$ and 0 otherwise. Its coefficient is the *change* in slope at the knot, and
because the hinge is continuous the two segments meet without a jump. @fig-ch11-spline shows a
two-segment fit with a knot at $X = 5$. This is the simplest member of the **spline** family; the
course does not pursue splines further, but the idea, add hinge columns to let the slope change at
knots, is a natural extension of everything in this chapter.

:::{admonition} Definition 11.10: Piecewise linear regression
:class: note definition
A **piecewise linear regression** joins two or more straight segments at chosen predictor values
called **knots**. A knot at $c$ enters through the hinge column $(X - c)_+ = \max(X - c,\ 0)$, whose
coefficient is the change in slope at the knot; continuity across the knot is automatic. It is the
simplest member of the spline family.
:::

## 11.5 Analysis of variance as regression

### Intuition

If you took intro statistics you likely met **one-way analysis of variance** (ANOVA, Definition
11.11), the $F$ test for whether several group means are equal, and you may have met it as a
separate technique with its own formulas. It is not separate. One-way ANOVA is exactly the regression of the
response on the group factor, coded with indicators, which is the model of section 11.1. Seeing
this collapses two things you learned as one and lets every tool of regression, diagnostics,
intervals, the general linear test, work on group-comparison problems.

The key fact is that when the only predictor is a factor, the fitted value for every case is
just its own group's sample mean. So the regression "curve" is a set of flat levels, one per
group, as @fig-ch11-anova-reg shows for the three ranks. Nothing else could minimize the squared
error within each group, because the mean is the least-squares constant.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch11_anova_reg.png
:name: fig-ch11-anova-reg
:alt: Salary in thousands for the three faculty ranks, shown as faint jittered points in three vertical clusters, with a thick red horizontal bar at each cluster marking the group mean, at about 81, 94, and 127 thousand. A dashed horizontal line marks the grand mean near 114 thousand. The fitted value for every point is its own group's mean.
One-way ANOVA is regression on rank indicators. The fitted value for each professor is simply that rank's group mean (red bars), so the model is a step function across the groups. The regression F test for the rank factor is identical to the one-way ANOVA F test.
```

### Formula

The one-way ANOVA model is usually written

$$
Y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_i + \varepsilon_{ij},
$$

for observation $j$ in group $i$, where $\mu$ is a baseline and $\tau_i$ is group $i$'s effect.
With reference-cell coding this is precisely $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \cdots + \beta_{k-1} X_{k-1} + \varepsilon$
from section 11.1: $\mu$ is the reference group's mean $\beta_0$, and each $\tau_i$ is a
coefficient $\beta$, the difference of a group's mean from the reference. The ANOVA null
hypothesis that all group means are equal,

$$
H_0: \tau_1 = \tau_2 = \cdots = \tau_k = 0,
$$

is the regression hypothesis that all the indicator coefficients are zero,
$H_0: \beta_1 = \cdots = \beta_{k-1} = 0$: the factor explains nothing. That is tested by the
model's overall $F$ statistic.

:::{admonition} Definition 11.11: One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA)
:class: note definition
**One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA)** is the $F$ test of whether several group means are equal.
Its model $Y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_i + \varepsilon_{ij}$ is exactly the regression of the response on
the group factor coded with indicators, and its null hypothesis $H_0:\tau_1 = \cdots = \tau_k = 0$
is the regression hypothesis $H_0:\beta_1 = \cdots = \beta_{k-1} = 0$.
:::

:::{admonition} Theorem 11.12: One-way ANOVA is regression on indicators
:class: important theorem
Let a single $k$-level factor be the only predictor, coded with reference-cell indicators. Then the
least-squares fitted value for every case is its own group's sample mean; the regression sum of
squares equals the ANOVA between-groups sum of squares; the error sum of squares equals the
within-groups sum of squares; and the model's overall

$$
F = \frac{\mathrm{MSR}}{\mathrm{MSE}}
$$

equals the one-way ANOVA $F$ statistic on $k-1$ and $n-k$ degrees of freedom.
:::

## 11.6 Chapter summary

You can now bring categories and curves into a regression. You encode a $k$-level factor with
$k-1$ reference-cell indicators, and you can say why one indicator must be dropped: including all
$k$ plus the intercept makes the design rank-deficient (Theorem 11.4). You read each indicator
coefficient as a difference of group means and the intercept as the reference-group mean. You
combine a category with a continuous predictor three ways: a common line, parallel lines (the
ANCOVA model), and separate slopes (an interaction), and you test between the parallel and separate
models with the general linear test of @ch08-general-linear-test. You know the trap that a
main-effect coefficient in an interaction model is only the effect at the other predictor's zero.
You fit a polynomial, center it so its coefficients are stable and interpretable, apply the
hierarchy principle, and resist over-fitting the degree. And you see one-way ANOVA as nothing but
regression on indicators, with the same $F$ test (Theorem 11.12).

**Key results at a glance.**

| Result | Statement or formula | Valid when |
|---|---|---|
| Reference-cell model (Def 11.3) | $Y = \beta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\beta_j X_j + \varepsilon$ | one $k$-level factor, one level chosen as reference |
| Indicator coefficient | $\beta_j = E\{Y\mid\text{level }j\} - E\{Y\mid\text{ref}\}$ | reference-cell coding |
| $k-1$ indicators (Theorem 11.4) | $X_1+\cdots+X_k=\mathbf{1}$ forces rank deficiency; drop one | intercept kept in the model |
| Parallel lines / ANCOVA (Def 11.5) | $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \beta_2 D + \varepsilon$ | one continuous, one categorical, equal slopes |
| Separate slopes / interaction (Def 11.6) | $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \beta_2 D + \beta_3 X D + \varepsilon$ | slopes allowed to differ by group |
| Between-group gap (interaction) | $\text{gap}(X) = \beta_2 + \beta_3 X$ | equals $\beta_2$ only at $X = 0$ |
| Test of equal slopes | general linear test, full vs reduced ($t^2 = F$) | nested parallel vs interaction models |
| Quadratic model (Def 11.7) | $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \beta_2 X^2 + \varepsilon$ | curvature; still linear in coefficients |
| Centering (Def 11.8) | $x = X - \bar X$; curvature $\beta_2$ unchanged | to cut linear-square correlation, stabilize coefficients |
| Hierarchy principle (Def 11.9) | keep $X$ with $X^2$; keep main effects with interaction | any higher-order term present |
| One-way ANOVA = regression (Theorem 11.12) | $F = \mathrm{MSR}/\mathrm{MSE}$ equals the ANOVA $F$ | a single factor is the only predictor |

## 11.7 Frequently asked questions

**Q1. Which category should I make the reference?** Any of them; the fit is identical, only the
coefficients' meaning changes. Pick the reference that makes the comparisons you care about
easiest to read: a control group, the largest group, or a natural baseline like "assistant
professor." If a coefficient's sign feels backwards, you may just want to relevel so the other
category is the reference.

**Q2. Do I ever include an indicator for every level?** Only if you drop the intercept. The
intercept column plus all $k$ indicators are linearly dependent, so software will either drop one
automatically or (with the intercept removed) switch to cell-means coding, where each coefficient
is a group mean. Never include the intercept and all $k$ indicators; the model is not
identifiable.

**Q3. My interaction is significant but one main effect is not. Do I drop the main effect?** No.
The hierarchy principle says keep both main effects whenever you keep their interaction. A
non-significant main effect in an interaction model usually just means the groups are similar at
the point where the other predictor is zero, which is rarely an interesting place and often
outside the data. Dropping it distorts the model.

**Q4. Why not just fit separate regressions to each group instead of one interaction model?**
Fitting each group separately gives the same fitted lines as the full interaction model, so for
prediction it is fine. But the single interaction model lets you *test* whether the slopes differ
(one coefficient, one p-value) and pools the groups to estimate a common error variance, which
separate fits cannot do. When the question is "are these groups different," one model with an
interaction is the cleaner tool.

**Q5. Does centering change my predictions or my $R^2$?** No. Centering is a change of
parametrization: it shifts the meaning of the intercept and linear coefficient but leaves every
fitted value, residual, $R^2$, and $F$ statistic untouched. Example 11.5 shows the raw and
centered quadratics predicting the identical 60.72 feet at 20 mph. Center for interpretation and
numerical stability, not to change the fit.

**Q6. How high a polynomial degree should I go?** Rarely past two or three, and only with a test
to back it up. Each degree fits the sample better but generalizes worse, wiggling between points
(@fig-ch11-poly-degree). Use the general linear test to check whether the added term reduces error
significantly, prefer the lowest degree that captures the visible shape, and never extrapolate a
high-degree polynomial beyond the data, where it can shoot off to absurd values.

**Q7. The adjusted sex coefficient is not significant. Does that prove pay is fair?** No. It says
that in this sample and this model, once rank, discipline, and service are held fixed, the
remaining male-minus-female difference is not distinguishable from zero, with a wide interval
driven partly by there being only 39 women. It does not establish equity, and it depends on the
choice of adjustment variables; if rank itself is affected by sex, adjusting for it can hide part
of the difference. Report the numbers and the assumptions, and let readers judge.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The claim misreads the main-effect coefficient, the classic interaction trap. Because the model
contains the product `yrs.since.phd:sex`, the `sexMale` coefficient is not the overall male-minus-

with zero years since the PhD. So the honest reading of each coefficient is: the intercept

male-minus-female gap at zero years since the PhD; and the interaction $-\$728$ is how much less
per year the male salary rises, so the male slope is $1{,}645 - 728 = \$917$ per year.

Because the male slope is smaller, the gap does not stay at \$20,210, it shrinks with experience.
The gap at $X$ years since the PhD is $20{,}209.61 - 727.98\,X$. At a realistic mid-career value of,
say, 20 years, the estimated gap is $20{,}209.61 - 727.98(20) \approx \$5{,}650$, far below the
\$20,000 the colleague quoted, and at about 28 years it closes entirely. On top of that, the
interaction is not significant ($p = 0.12$), so the data do not firmly establish that the slopes
differ at all; the safest statement is that men start ahead but the estimated gap narrows with
seniority. A weak answer reports \$20,210 as "the effect of sex" and never notices that the
coefficient is conditional on zero years of experience.

**EP 11.2 (explain why).** In Example 11.5 the raw quadratic `dist ~ speed + I(speed^2)` gives the
`speed` coefficient a standard error of about 2.03 (so $t = 0.45$), while the centered quadratic
gives the `speed_c` coefficient a standard error of about 0.41 (so $t = 9.69$). The curvature
coefficient, the fitted curve, and $R^2$ are identical in the two fits. Explain why centering
shrinks the standard error of the linear coefficient so dramatically, and explain why this is not
evidence that the centered model "fits better."

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The two fits are the same parabola written in two coordinate systems, so they have identical fitted
values, residuals, SSE, and $R^2$; centering changes the parametrization, not the model. What
changes is the correlation between the two predictor columns. On the raw scale `speed` runs from 4
to 25, so `speed` and `speed^2` both climb together and correlate about 0.98. When two columns are
nearly collinear the least-squares fit cannot tell how much credit each one deserves, because many
different coefficient pairs give almost the same fitted values; this near-singularity inflates the
diagonal of $(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$, and since $s\{b_j\} = s\sqrt{(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}_{jj}}$
the standard error of the linear coefficient blows up. Subtracting the mean before squaring makes
the linear column and the squared column nearly uncorrelated (their correlation falls to about
$-0.10$), so each coefficient is pinned down on its own and its standard error collapses.

This is a gain in interpretability and numerical stability, not in fit. The centered `speed_c`
coefficient is now clearly readable as the slope of the curve at the mean speed, with a small
standard error, but the model predicts exactly the same stopping distances as before. A weak answer
claims centering "improves the fit" or raises $R^2$; it does neither, and saying so misses that
collinearity hurts the standard errors while leaving the fitted curve untouched.
:::

**EP 11.3 (what would change if).** Example 11.1 fit `salary ~ rank` with Assistant as the reference
level and reported an intercept of \$80,776, a `rankAssocProf` coefficient of \$13,100, and a
`rankProf` coefficient of \$45,996. A colleague refits the identical model but sets Full professor
(`Prof`) as the reference instead. Describe what would change in the coefficient table and what
would stay exactly the same, and explain why. Give the numerical values the new table would show.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
Changing the reference level only relabels the comparisons; it does not change the model or its
fit. The fitted group means, the residuals, $R^2$, and the overall $F$ statistic are all identical,
because the three fitted salaries are still each group's own sample mean (Assistant \$80,776,
Associate \$93,876, Full \$126,772) no matter which level is chosen as the baseline. What changes is
what the intercept and the two coefficients measure. With Full as the reference, the intercept
becomes the full-professor mean, and each coefficient becomes a group's mean minus the
full-professor mean, so both coefficients turn negative. The refit produces

```text
               Estimate Std. Error t value Pr(>|t|)
(Intercept)   126772.11    1449.07   87.48        0
rankAssocProf -32895.67    3290.47  -10.00        0
rankAsstProf  -45996.12    3230.54  -14.24        0
```

The intercept \$126,772 is the full-professor mean; `rankAssocProf` $= -\$32,896$ says associates
earn that much less than full professors, and `rankAsstProf` $= -\$45,996$ says assistants earn
that much less. That last number is exactly the negative of the old `rankProf` coefficient
(\$45,996), because the Full-minus-Assistant gap and the Assistant-minus-Full gap are the same
distance with opposite sign. A weak answer says the fit itself changes or that one reference is more
correct than another; the choice of reference is only a choice of which comparison is easiest to
read.
:::

**EP 11.4 (interpret output in context, then evaluate a claim).** For the `cars` data a student fits
the linear model and the quadratic model and compares them.

```r
fit_lin  <- lm(dist ~ speed, data = cars)
fit_quad <- lm(dist ~ speed + I(speed^2), data = cars)
round(coef(summary(fit_quad)), 4)
anova(fit_lin, fit_quad)
```
```text
            Estimate Std. Error t value Pr(>|t|)
(Intercept)   2.4701    14.8172  0.1667   0.8683
speed         0.9133     2.0342  0.4490   0.6555
I(speed^2)    0.1000     0.0660  1.5153   0.1364

Analysis of Variance Table

Model 1: dist ~ speed
Model 2: dist ~ speed + I(speed^2)
  Res.Df   RSS Df Sum of Sq     F Pr(>F)
1     48 11354
2     47 10825  1    528.81 2.296 0.1364
```

The student concludes: "The squared-speed coefficient is positive, which matches the physics that
braking distance grows faster than linearly, so we should report the quadratic model." Evaluate this
conclusion, using the numbers in the output.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The student is right about the direction and wrong to treat it as settled. The squared-speed
coefficient is $+0.100$, and a positive curvature does match the physics that stopping distance
rises with the square of speed. But a coefficient having the expected sign is not the same as the
curvature being established. The general linear test comparing the quadratic to the straight line
gives $F = 2.30$ on 1 and 47 degrees of freedom with $p = 0.1364$, well above 0.05, so the drop in
error sum of squares (from 11,354 to 10,825) is within what sampling noise could produce. For these
50 cars the upward bend is suggestive but not significant, and a straight line is defensible.

The honest report is exactly that: the curvature is in the physically expected direction but not
established at conventional significance, so either model is reasonable and the simpler straight line
is a fair default. Note also that the individual $t$ value on `speed` in the raw quadratic (0.45,
$p = 0.66$) should not be read as "speed does not matter"; that large p-value is an artifact of the
0.98 correlation between `speed` and `speed^2`, not evidence against the linear term. A weak answer
either declares the quadratic proven from the coefficient sign alone, ignoring the $p = 0.14$ test,
or misreads the raw `speed` p-value as a real finding.
:::

**EP 11.5 (what would change if).** Example 11.4 estimated the male-minus-female salary difference,
adjusting for rank, discipline, and years of service, and found `sexMale` $= \$4,771$ with
$p = 0.22$. Suppose you drop `rank` from the adjustment and refit `salary ~ discipline + yrs.service + sex`.

```r
fit_norank <- lm(salary ~ discipline + yrs.service + sex, data = salaries)
round(coef(summary(fit_norank)), 2)
```
```text
            Estimate Std. Error t value Pr(>|t|)
(Intercept) 84361.06    4941.37   17.07     0.00
disciplineB 13033.85    2840.35    4.59     0.00
yrs.service   832.15     110.22    7.55     0.00
sexMale      8423.34    4744.54    1.78     0.08
```

Explain why the sex coefficient changed when rank left the model, and explain why deciding whether
to include rank is a question about the world rather than a question the regression can settle.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
Dropping rank nearly doubled the estimated sex gap, from \$4,771 to \$8,423 (and moved its p-value
from 0.22 toward significance at 0.08). The reason is that rank is related to both salary and sex.
Rank is the strongest single driver of salary, and the sexes are not evenly spread across ranks:
proportionally fewer women hold the full-professor rank. When rank is in the model, it absorbs the
part of the raw male-minus-female difference that runs through rank, leaving a small residual sex
coefficient. When rank is removed, that rank-carried difference has nowhere else to go, so the sex
coefficient swells to pick it up. Neither number is a mistake; they answer different questions,
"among faculty of the same rank" versus "ignoring rank."

Which question is the right one is not something the arithmetic can decide, because it depends on how
rank came to be. If promotion practices themselves differ by sex, then rank sits on the causal
pathway between sex and salary, and controlling for it would remove part of the very difference an
equity analysis is trying to measure. If rank is instead a fair reflection of seniority and merit,
adjusting for it is exactly right. The regression will happily produce either coefficient cleanly;
choosing which variables belong in the adjustment set is a substantive judgment about promotion and
careers, not about the model. A weak answer just reports that the number went up, or treats one of
the two coefficients as the single correct estimate of discrimination without engaging the
causal-pathway question.
:::

:::{admonition} Resumen del capítulo (en español)
:class: dropdown
Este capítulo enseña a incluir **predictores categóricos (categorical predictors)** y curvas en
una regresión, usando los salarios de 397 profesores (rango, disciplina, años de servicio, sexo y
salario) y los datos clásicos de frenado (`cars`).

Una categoría no es un número, así que se codifica con **variables indicadoras (indicator
variables)**, columnas de ceros y unos. Un factor con $k$ niveles usa $k-1$ indicadores; incluir
los $k$ más el intercepto deja la matriz de diseño sin rango completo. En la **codificación de
celda de referencia (reference-cell coding)**, el intercepto es la media del grupo de referencia y
cada coeficiente es la diferencia de medias respecto a ese grupo. Para el rango: la media de
profesor asistente es \$80,776, y los coeficientes \$13,100 y \$45,996 son las diferencias hacia
asociado y titular.

Con un predictor categórico y uno continuo hay tres modelos: una recta común, **rectas paralelas
(parallel lines)** (modelo ANCOVA, un intercepto por grupo y pendiente compartida), y **pendientes
separadas (separate slopes)** mediante una **interacción (interaction)**. En el modelo con
interacción, el coeficiente de la categoría mide solo la diferencia en $X = 0$; leerlo como "el
efecto de la categoría" es un error común. La **prueba lineal general (general linear test)**
decide entre ambos modelos: aquí $F = 10.37$, $p = 0.0014$, las pendientes difieren.

La **regresión polinómica (polynomial regression)** añade el cuadrado del predictor para curvar la
recta. **Centrar (centering)** el predictor no cambia la curvatura ni las predicciones, pero reduce
la correlación entre el término lineal y el cuadrático (de 0.98 a $-0.10$ en `cars`) y aclara los
coeficientes. El **principio de jerarquía (hierarchy principle)** exige conservar los términos de
orden inferior. Finalmente, el **análisis de varianza (analysis of variance)** de una vía es la
regresión sobre indicadores: su $F = 128.22$ coincide con el $F$ global de la regresión. Un solo
modelo contiene la prueba $t$ de dos muestras, el ANOVA y el ANCOVA.
:::

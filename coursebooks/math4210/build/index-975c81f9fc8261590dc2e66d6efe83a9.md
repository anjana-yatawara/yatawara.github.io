# 5. Randomization and bootstrap inference for regression

:::{div}
:class: lang-toggle
[Español](./es.md)
:::

A football coach wants to know what makes a punter kick far. He measures thirteen college
punters on a set of physical tests: right and left leg strength (in pounds, on a leg-press
machine), hamstring flexibility, an overall leg-strength score, and hang time. For each punter
he also records the average distance of ten punts, in feet. The question is simple to ask and
hard to answer with so few kickers: does a stronger leg really buy more distance, or could the
pattern he sees be a fluke of thirteen athletes?

@fig-ch05-punting-scatter plots punt distance against right-leg strength. The trend slopes up,
and the fitted line climbs about nine tenths of a foot for every extra pound of strength. That
looks like a real effect. But thirteen points is not many. The inference tools from Chapter 3,
the $t$ test and its confidence interval for the slope, all rest on a chain of assumptions.
Either the errors are normal, or the sample is large enough for the normal approximation to
save us. With thirteen punters, neither is guaranteed, and there is no way to check normality
from thirteen residuals with any confidence.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch05_punting_scatter.png
:name: fig-ch05-punting-scatter
:alt: Scatterplot of punt distance in feet on the vertical axis against right-leg strength in pounds on the horizontal axis for thirteen punters, with an upward-sloping least-squares line. Distance rises from about 105 feet at the weakest legs near 110 pounds to about 190 feet at the strongest near 180 pounds, and the thirteen points scatter around the line.
The thirteen punters, with the least-squares line. Distance climbs with right-leg strength at about 0.9 feet per pound, but thirteen points make it fair to ask whether the slope is real or an accident of a small sample.
```

This chapter answers the question a different way. Instead of assuming a shape for the errors,
it uses the computer to shuffle and resample the data thousands of times and watches what
happens. Two ideas do the work. A **permutation test** (Definition 5.1) breaks the link between
predictor and response by shuffling, then asks how often pure chance produces a slope as steep as
the one you saw. The **bootstrap** (Definition 5.4) resamples the data with replacement to see how
much the slope would bounce around if you could collect the study again. Both replace a fragile assumption with a
computation you can always run. By the end you will be able to build each one from scratch, read
its output, and say when it agrees with the classical $t$ and $F$ tests and when it does not.

:::{admonition} This lesson at a glance
:class: important
- **What we are doing:** Testing a regression slope and putting a confidence interval on it by shuffling and resampling the small punting dataset on the computer, instead of assuming a shape for the errors.
- **Why we are doing it:** With only thirteen punters the normal-theory $t$ and $F$ machinery of Chapter 3 may not hold, and normality cannot be checked from thirteen residuals.
- **Main objective:** Build a permutation test and a bootstrap confidence interval for the slope from scratch, and say when they agree with the classical $t$ test and when they do not.
- **What changed from the last chapters:** Chapter 3's inference leaned on the normal-error assumption (@ch02-mle); this chapter drops it and replaces it with computation, comparing its permutation p-value against Chapter 3's $t$-test p-value on the same slope.
:::

:class: tip
By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- **State** the no-association null hypothesis and describe the exact logic of a permutation test for the slope.
- **Implement** the permutation test for the slope from scratch in R and Python, with no special packages, and compute a permutation p-value.
- **Justify** the permutation p-value and the plus-one correction, and compare it against the classical $t$-test p-value on the same data.
- **Distinguish** case resampling from residual resampling for the bootstrap, and code each one.

- **Decide** when computational and classical inference agree, and what to report when they disagree.

## 5.1 When normal-theory inference is on thin ice

### Intuition

Every test from Chapter 3 came with fine print. Its formulas give exact answers only when the
errors follow a normal bell curve, or when the sample is large enough to stand in for that. With
thirteen punters we have neither, and this section shows that we cannot even check the fine print.
Here is that fine print written out in symbols.

Chapter 3 built a confidence interval for the slope as $b_1 \pm t^{\ast} \, s\{b_1\}$ and tested
$H_0: \beta_1 = 0$ with the ratio $t^{\ast} = b_1 / s\{b_1\}$, comparing it to a $t$ distribution
with $n - 2$ degrees of freedom. That machinery is exact only when the errors
$\varepsilon_i$ are normal. When they are not normal, the $t$ ratio is still approximately
$t$-distributed if the sample is large, because a version of the central limit theorem smooths
the estimate toward normality. The catch is the word large. With thirteen punters there is no
large-sample rescue, and there is no way to confirm the errors are normal.

You might think you could just check: fit the line, plot the thirteen residuals on a normal
quantile plot, and see whether they fall on a straight line. The problem is that thirteen points
are too few to tell normal from non-normal. @fig-ch05-small-sample-qq makes this concrete. The
top-left panel is the real punting residuals; the other five are genuinely normal samples of the
same size, drawn by the computer. Every one of them bends and wiggles away from the straight
line. If known-normal data looks this crooked at $n = 13$, then a crooked plot from real data
tells you almost nothing. The normality assumption is simply uncheckable here.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch05_small_sample_qq.png
:name: fig-ch05-small-sample-qq
:alt: Six normal quantile-quantile plots arranged in a two-by-three grid, each with thirteen points and a dashed reference line. The top-left is labeled real punting residuals; the other five are labeled normal samples. All six show points that wander noticeably off the straight reference line, with bends and gaps, even the ones that are truly normal.
At n = 13 even data that is genuinely normal (the five blue panels) produces a bent, wandering quantile plot. So the crooked plot of the real residuals (top left) is no evidence against normality: thirteen points cannot tell the two apart. This is why we want inference that does not lean on a normality check.
```

The way out is to stop assuming a distribution and start generating one. If we can build the
distribution of the slope under some clearly stated chance mechanism, using only the data in
hand, then we can measure how surprising the observed slope is without ever writing down a normal
density. That is what the rest of the chapter does.

::::{admonition} Try it 5.1

residuals, sees a slight bend, and declares "the normality assumption is violated, so the
$t$-test p-value is worthless." What is wrong with both halves of that statement?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
First, a slight bend in a quantile plot of only 15 residuals is expected even when the errors are
perfectly normal, as @fig-ch05-small-sample-qq shows, so a mild bend is not evidence of a
violation. The classmate is reading noise as signal. Second, even if the errors were genuinely
non-normal, the $t$-test p-value is not automatically "worthless"; it may still be a decent
approximation, and the honest response is to check it against a method that does not need
normality, such as a permutation test, rather than to throw it out by decree. The point of this
chapter is to replace the argument about normality with a computation.
:::
::::

## 5.2 The permutation test for the slope

### Intuition

Here is the question a hypothesis test asks, in plain words: if leg strength had nothing to do
with punt distance, how often would random chance hand us a slope as steep as $0.902$? To answer
it we need to see slopes produced by "nothing going on." We can manufacture them. Keep the
thirteen strength values exactly where they are, take the thirteen distances, and shuffle them so
that each distance is reassigned to a random punter. Shuffling destroys any real link between
strength and distance while keeping both columns of numbers intact. Fit a line to the shuffled
data and record its slope. That slope is a sample of "what a slope looks like when there is no
association."

@fig-ch05-shuffle-idea shows one shuffle. On the left, the real data slopes up. On the right, the
same distances reattached to random strengths give a nearly flat line. Do the shuffle thousands
of times and you get thousands of no-association slopes: their histogram is the null distribution
of the slope, built entirely from your own data with no formula and no normality assumption.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch05_shuffle_idea.png
:name: fig-ch05-shuffle-idea
:alt: Two side-by-side scatterplots of punt distance against right-leg strength. The left panel, labeled real data, shows an upward-sloping line with slope 0.90. The right panel, labeled one reshuffle, shows the same distances reassigned to random strengths, with a nearly flat fitted line of slope near zero.
Shuffling the distances against the fixed strengths breaks the link between them, so a reshuffled fit has a slope near zero. Repeating the shuffle thousands of times shows what slopes chance alone can produce.
```

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 5.1: Permutation test
:class: note definition
A **permutation test** for a regression slope builds the null distribution of the slope by
repeatedly reshuffling the response values $Y_i$ against the fixed predictor values $X_i$ (a
sample without replacement), which breaks any real association, and then compares the observed
slope to the reshuffled ones. It tests $H_0$: $X$ and $Y$ are unrelated ($\beta_1 = 0$) against
the alternative that they are related.
:::

The permutation test for the slope works with these pieces. Let the data be pairs
$(X_i, Y_i)$ for $i = 1, \dots, n$, and let the observed least-squares slope be

$$
b_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar X)(Y_i - \bar Y)}{\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar X)^2}
= \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} .
$$

In words: the slope is the cross-product of $X$ and $Y$ deviations divided by the spread of $X$,
exactly as in @ch02-least-squares. The null hypothesis is

$$
H_0 : X \text{ and } Y \text{ are unrelated} \quad(\text{in particular } \beta_1 = 0),
$$

and the alternative is that they are related ($\beta_1 \neq 0$). A **permutation** is a
reshuffling $\pi$ of the response values that pairs $Y_{\pi(i)}$ with $X_i$. For each permutation
compute the reshuffled slope $b_1^{\pi} = \sum (X_i - \bar X)(Y_{\pi(i)} - \bar Y) / S_{xx}$. The
two-sided **permutation p-value** is the fraction of permutations whose slope is at least as far
from zero as the observed one:

$$
p_{\text{perm}} = \frac{\#\{\pi : |b_1^{\pi}| \ge |b_1|\} + 1}{(\text{number of permutations}) + 1} .
$$

:::{admonition} Definition 5.2: Permutation p-value
:class: note definition
The two-sided **permutation p-value** is the fraction of permutations whose reshuffled slope is at
least as far from zero as the observed slope, with a plus one added to both the count and the
total for the observed data itself:
$$
p_{\text{perm}} = \frac{M + 1}{B + 1}, \qquad M = \#\{\pi : |b_1^{\pi}| \ge |b_1|\},
$$
where $B$ is the number of permutations drawn. The plus one keeps $p_{\text{perm}} \ge 1/(B+1)$,
so it can never be exactly zero.
:::

In words: the p-value is how often a reshuffle beats or ties the real slope in size, plus one for
the real data itself, divided by the number of tries plus one. There are $n!$ possible
permutations, far too many to list ($13!$ is over six billion), so we draw $B$ of them at random
and use those. The extra $+1$ on top and bottom counts the observed arrangement as one of the
possibilities, which we explain and justify next.

### Derivation (why the permutation test is valid)

:::{admonition} Theorem 5.3: Validity of the permutation p-value
:class: important theorem
Under the no-association null $H_0$, in which the response values are attached to the predictor
values without regard to them, the observed slope is one draw from the permutation distribution
that places probability $1/n!$ on each of the $n!$ equally likely pairings. The permutation
p-value $p_{\text{perm}} = (M+1)/(B+1)$ is then a valid p-value: it never rejects a true null more
often than its stated level, and its smallest attainable value is the floor $1/(B+1)$.
:::

**Proof.** Suppose $H_0$ holds in the strong
sense that the response values were attached to the predictor values without any regard to them.
Then the particular pairing we observed is just one of the $n!$ equally likely ways to match the
fixed collection of $Y$ values to the fixed $X$ values. Any statistic computed on a random pairing,
here the slope $b_1^{\pi}$, therefore has a completely known distribution under $H_0$: it places
probability $1/n!$ on each of the $n!$ arrangements. This is the **permutation distribution**, and
it needs no assumption about the shape of the errors. Normality never appears.

Our observed slope $b_1$ is the value from one particular arrangement, so under $H_0$ it is one
draw from that permutation distribution. The exact two-sided p-value is the null probability of a
slope at least as extreme in size,

$$
p_{\text{exact}} = \frac{\#\{\pi : |b_1^{\pi}| \ge |b_1|\}}{n!} ,
$$

which counts arrangements, including the observed one (it satisfies $|b_1^{\pi}| \ge |b_1|$ with
equality). When $n!$ is too large to enumerate, we estimate $p_{\text{exact}}$ by drawing $B$
random permutations. Writing $M$ for the number of those $B$ draws with $|b_1^{\pi}| \ge |b_1|$,
the estimator

$$
p_{\text{perm}} = \frac{M + 1}{B + 1}
$$

adds the observed arrangement to both the count and the total. This $+1$ is not a fudge. It keeps
the test honest: the observed data is itself a valid arrangement under $H_0$, so it belongs in the
reference set, and including it guarantees $p_{\text{perm}}$ can never be exactly zero. A test that
could report $p = 0$ would reject $H_0$ with certainty on finite evidence, which is never
justified. With the $+1$, the smallest possible p-value is $1/(B+1)$, and one can show the
resulting test never rejects a true null more often than its stated level. $\blacksquare$

One tidy consequence: because $S_{xx}$ and the $X_i$ do not change when we shuffle $Y$, the
reshuffled slope $b_1^{\pi}$ is just $S_{xy}^{\pi}/S_{xx}$, a fixed positive constant times the
reshuffled cross-product $S_{xy}^{\pi}$. So permuting the slope is the same test as permuting the
sample correlation, or permuting $S_{xy}$ itself. Whatever monotone version of "association" you
prefer, the permutation test gives the same p-value.

### R

The permutation test is a short loop. Read the data, write a one-line slope function, compute the
observed slope, then shuffle and refit $B$ times. No package beyond base R is needed.

```r
punting <- read.csv("data/punting.csv")
dim(punting)
fit <- lm(Distance ~ RStr, data = punting)
round(summary(fit)$coefficients, 5)
```
```text
[1] 13  7
            Estimate Std. Error t value Pr(>|t|)
(Intercept) 15.00896   31.35922 0.47861  0.64159
RStr         0.90204    0.21003 4.29474  0.00127
```

The classical fit gives a slope of $0.902$ feet per pound with a $t$-test p-value of $0.00127$.
Hold on to that number; the permutation test will produce its own p-value to compare against it.
Now the from-scratch pieces.

```r
slope <- function(x, y) {
  sum((x - mean(x)) * (y - mean(y))) / sum((x - mean(x))^2)
}
x <- punting$RStr
y <- punting$Distance
b1_obs <- slope(x, y)
b1_obs
```
```text
[1] 0.9020383
```

```r
set.seed(4210)
B <- 5000
perm_slopes <- numeric(B)
for (i in 1:B) {
  y_shuffled <- sample(y)              # shuffle distances, keep strengths fixed
  perm_slopes[i] <- slope(x, y_shuffled)
}
count_extreme <- sum(abs(perm_slopes) >= abs(b1_obs))
p_perm <- (count_extreme + 1) / (B + 1)
c(count_extreme = count_extreme, p_permutation = p_perm)
```
```text
count_extreme p_permutation 
    4.0000000     0.0009998 
```

Out of $5000$ reshuffles, only $4$ produced a slope as steep as the real one, so the permutation
p-value is $(4+1)/(5000+1) \approx 0.001$.

### Python

The same loop in Python with NumPy. `rng.permutation` does the shuffling; everything else is a
handful of array operations.

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

punting = pd.read_csv("data/punting.csv")
print(punting.shape)
fit = smf.ols("Distance ~ RStr", data=punting).fit()
print(fit.summary().tables[1])
```
```text
(13, 7)
==============================================================================
                 coef    std err          t      P>|t|      [0.025      0.975]
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Intercept     15.0090     31.359      0.479      0.642     -54.012      84.030
RStr           0.9020      0.210      4.295      0.001       0.440       1.364
==============================================================================
```

```python
def slope(x, y):
    x = np.asarray(x, float)
    y = np.asarray(y, float)
    return np.sum((x - x.mean()) * (y - y.mean())) / np.sum((x - x.mean()) ** 2)

x = punting["RStr"].to_numpy()
y = punting["Distance"].to_numpy()
b1_obs = slope(x, y)
print(b1_obs)
```
```text
0.9020382716049381
```

```python
rng = np.random.default_rng(4210)
B = 5000
perm_slopes = np.empty(B)
for i in range(B):
    y_shuffled = rng.permutation(y)       # shuffle distances, keep strengths fixed
    perm_slopes[i] = slope(x, y_shuffled)
count_extreme = int(np.sum(np.abs(perm_slopes) >= abs(b1_obs)))
p_perm = (count_extreme + 1) / (B + 1)
print("count_extreme =", count_extreme, " p_permutation =", p_perm)
```
```text
count_extreme = 5  p_permutation = 0.0011997600479904018
```

R and Python use different random number generators, so they draw different shuffles: R found $4$
extreme slopes and Python found $5$, giving permutation p-values of about $0.0010$ and $0.0012$.
The two disagree only in the third decimal place, which is Monte Carlo noise, and both point to
the same clear conclusion.

:::{admonition} Example 5.1: The punting slope by permutation
:class: note
**Question.** Is the upward slope of punt distance on right-leg strength stronger than chance
would produce, without assuming normal errors?

**Intuition.** Shuffle the distances against the fixed strengths $5000$ times, collect the
no-association slopes, and see where the real slope $0.902$ falls among them.

**Formula.** $p_{\text{perm}} = (M + 1)/(B + 1)$, where $M$ counts reshuffles with
$|b_1^{\pi}| \ge |b_1|$.

**Computation.** The R and Python loops above give $M = 4$ (R) and $M = 5$ (Python) out of
$B = 5000$, so $p_{\text{perm}} \approx 0.001$. @fig-ch05-perm-null-punting shows the null
distribution with the observed slope marked far out in the tail.

:::{admonition} Durable skill: Replace an untestable assumption with a computation
:class: tip
The permutation test trades an assumption you cannot check (normal errors from thirteen points)
for a mechanism you can state exactly (shuffle the labels) and run on a computer. This move,
turning "I hope the assumptions hold" into "I will simulate the null and look," is one of the most
portable ideas in modern statistics. Whenever a formula-based test rests on a premise you cannot
verify, ask whether you can instead generate the null distribution directly by resampling or
shuffling. To practice, take any two-group comparison you meet and build its permutation test in
ten lines before reaching for a named procedure.
:::

::::{admonition} Try it 5.2
:class: important
In the permutation loop, why do we shuffle only the response `y` and leave `x` untouched? Would
shuffling `x` instead, or shuffling both, change the test?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
The null hypothesis is that the pairing between $X$ and $Y$ carries no information, so we destroy
the pairing by randomly rematching the two columns. It does not matter which column you shuffle:
shuffling $Y$ against fixed $X$, shuffling $X$ against fixed $Y$, or shuffling both, all produce
the same set of random pairings and the same permutation distribution of the slope, because the
slope depends only on how the $X$ and $Y$ values are matched up. We shuffle $Y$ by convention.
What you must not do is shuffle the two columns together (a paired shuffle), because that keeps
every original pair intact and tests nothing.
:::
::::

## 5.3 Permutation on Toluca, and how it compares with the t test

The punting example ended in a near-tie between the permutation and $t$-test p-values. To see the
two methods on a case where the signal is overwhelming, return to the Toluca Company data from
Chapters 2 and 3: the labor hours to produce a part against the lot size, with the least-squares
slope $b_1 = 3.5702$ hours per unit (@ch02-least-squares). Chapter 3 tested that slope with a $t$
test and found a p-value of about $4.45 \times 10^{-10}$ (@ch03-f-test, where the $t$ and $F$
tests coincide for a single slope). We now run the permutation test on the same slope and compare.

```r
toluca <- read.csv("data/toluca.csv")
xt <- toluca$lotsize
yt <- toluca$hours
b1_toluca <- slope(xt, yt)
p_t <- summary(lm(hours ~ lotsize, data = toluca))$coefficients["lotsize", "Pr(>|t|)"]

set.seed(4210)
perm_toluca <- numeric(B)
for (i in 1:B) perm_toluca[i] <- slope(xt, sample(yt))
c(b1_toluca      = b1_toluca,
  perm_count     = sum(abs(perm_toluca) >= abs(b1_toluca)),
  max_perm_slope = max(abs(perm_toluca)),
  t_test_p_value = p_t)
```
```text
     b1_toluca     perm_count max_perm_slope t_test_p_value 
  3.570202e+00   0.000000e+00   2.767172e+00   4.448828e-10 
```

```python
toluca = pd.read_csv("data/toluca.csv")
xt = toluca["lotsize"].to_numpy()
yt = toluca["hours"].to_numpy()
b1_toluca = slope(xt, yt)
p_t = smf.ols("hours ~ lotsize", data=toluca).fit().pvalues["lotsize"]

rng = np.random.default_rng(4210)
perm_toluca = np.array([slope(xt, rng.permutation(yt)) for _ in range(B)])
print("b1_toluca      =", b1_toluca)
print("perm_count     =", int(np.sum(np.abs(perm_toluca) >= abs(b1_toluca))))
print("max_perm_slope =", np.max(np.abs(perm_toluca)))
print("t_test_p_value =", p_t)
```
```text
b1_toluca      = 3.57020202020202
perm_count     = 0
max_perm_slope = 2.7454545454545456
t_test_p_value = 4.4488275871889375e-10
```

:::{admonition} Example 5.2: The Toluca slope by permutation
:class: note
**Question.** How does the permutation test judge the Toluca slope, and can it match the $t$
test's tiny p-value?

**Intuition.** Shuffle the hours against the fixed lot sizes and see whether any reshuffle comes
near the real slope $3.5702$.

**Formula.** $p_{\text{perm}} = (M + 1)/(B + 1)$, as before.

**Computation.** In $5000$ reshuffles the largest slope that chance produced was about $2.77$ in
size (R) or $2.75$ (Python), nowhere near the observed $3.5702$. So $M = 0$ and

$$
p_{\text{perm}} = \frac{0 + 1}{5000 + 1} = \frac{1}{5001} \approx 0.0002 .
$$

@fig-ch05-perm-null-toluca shows the observed slope sitting off the right edge of the whole
permutation histogram.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch05_perm_null_toluca.png
:name: fig-ch05-perm-null-toluca
:alt: Histogram of 5000 reshuffled Toluca slopes centered at zero and spanning roughly minus 3 to plus 3. A green arrow points to the observed slope 3.57, which lies to the right of every bar in the histogram, with the annotation that no reshuffle reaches it.
The Toluca permutation null distribution. The real slope 3.57 is larger than every one of 5000 reshuffled slopes, so the permutation p-value is smaller than 1 in 5001, roughly 0.0002.
```

**Interpretation.** Both methods say the same thing: the Toluca slope is real beyond any
reasonable doubt. But notice a limit of the permutation test. With $5000$ reshuffles, the smallest
p-value it can report is $1/5001 \approx 0.0002$. It cannot produce the $t$ test's
$4.45 \times 10^{-10}$, not because the two disagree, but because a Monte Carlo permutation test
has a floor set by how many reshuffles you run. When the evidence is this strong, the permutation
test says "smaller than I can measure," and the $t$ test, if you trust its assumptions, puts a
number on just how small.
:::

The floor $1/(B+1)$ raises a fair question: how many reshuffles is enough? Because
$p_{\text{perm}}$ is a Monte Carlo estimate, it wobbles from run to run when $B$ is small and
settles as $B$ grows. @fig-ch05-pval-stability tracks the running punting p-value against the
number of reshuffles used, for three different random seeds. Past a few thousand reshuffles the
three lines have nearly stopped moving, which is why this chapter uses $B = 5000$. If you needed
to resolve a p-value near a sharp cutoff, you would run more.

:class: important
You run a permutation test with $B = 999$ reshuffles and none of them beats your observed
statistic. What p-value do you report, and what would you have to do to report something smaller?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
With $M = 0$ out of $B = 999$, the permutation p-value is $(0 + 1)/(999 + 1) = 1/1000 = 0.001$.
You cannot honestly report anything smaller from this run, because $1/(B+1)$ is the floor. To
resolve a smaller p-value you must run more reshuffles (raise $B$), which lowers the floor, or
switch to a method like the $t$ test that puts an analytic number on the far tail, accepting its
assumptions in exchange.
:::
::::

## 5.4 The bootstrap for regression

### Intuition

A permutation test answers "is there an effect?" It does not tell you how big the effect is or how
precise your estimate of it is. For that we want the sampling distribution of $b_1$: the spread of
slopes you would see if you could repeat the whole study many times. We cannot repeat the study.
The **bootstrap** fakes the repetition using the one sample we have, by resampling it with
replacement. The idea, due to Bradley Efron, is that the sample is our best picture of the
population, so drawing new samples from the sample imitates drawing new samples from the
population.

:::{admonition} Definition 5.4: Bootstrap
:class: note definition
The **bootstrap** estimates the sampling variability of a statistic by resampling the observed
data with replacement many times, recomputing the statistic on each resample, and treating the
spread of those values as an estimate of how much the statistic would vary across repeated
studies. It rests on the principle that the sample is the best available stand-in for the
population.
:::

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
A permutation test and a bootstrap answer different questions. Permutation shuffling breaks the
link between predictor and response to ask whether any effect exists at all, and it returns a
p-value. Bootstrap resampling imitates repeated studies to ask how precise the effect estimate is,
and it returns a standard error or confidence interval. Reach for the first to detect an effect,
the second to measure one.
:::

There are two natural ways to resample a regression, and they answer slightly different questions.
@fig-ch05-bootstrap-idea shows both. **Case resampling** draws whole rows $(X_i, Y_i)$ with
replacement, so a given punter may appear twice or not at all, and the set of strength values
changes from resample to resample. This treats the punters as a random sample from a population of
punters. **Residual resampling** keeps the strengths and the fitted line fixed, then builds a new
response by adding reshuffled-with-replacement residuals back onto the fitted values. This treats
the strength values as fixed by design and puts all the randomness in the errors, matching the
regression model of Chapter 2 more literally.

:::{admonition} Definition 5.5: Case resampling and residual resampling
:class: note definition
**Case resampling** draws whole rows $(X_i, Y_i)$ with replacement, so the set of predictor values
varies from resample to resample; it fits data in which the subjects were randomly sampled.
**Residual resampling** holds the predictors $X_i$ and the fitted line $\hat Y_i = b_0 + b_1 X_i$
fixed and rebuilds the response as $Y_i^{\ast} = \hat Y_i + e_{s(i)}^{\ast}$, where each
$e_{s(i)}^{\ast}$ is a residual drawn with replacement from $e_1, \dots, e_n$; it fits data in
which the predictor values are fixed by design.
:::

```{figure} figures/fig_ch05_bootstrap_idea.png
:name: fig-ch05-bootstrap-idea
:alt: Two side-by-side schematic scatterplots on six points. The left panel, case resampling, shows some points drawn as larger markers (selected more than once) and one hollow marker (not selected), with the fitted line dashed. The right panel, residual resampling, shows the fitted line fixed with points on it and arrows moving each point up or down by a reshuffled residual to a new square marker.
The two bootstraps for regression. Case resampling (left) redraws whole rows, so which x-values appear changes each time. Residual resampling (right) keeps the x-values and the fitted line fixed and reattaches reshuffled residuals, putting all the randomness in the errors.
```

### Formula

Both bootstraps repeat a resample-and-refit loop $B$ times and collect the slopes. The
**case-resampling** algorithm is:

$$
\text{for } b = 1, \dots, B: \quad
\text{draw } (X_i^{\ast}, Y_i^{\ast}) \text{ by sampling } n \text{ rows with replacement, then } b_1^{\ast b} = \frac{S_{xy}^{\ast}}{S_{xx}^{\ast}} .
$$

In words: rebuild a dataset by drawing $n$ rows at random with repeats allowed, and refit. The
**residual-resampling** algorithm fixes $X_i$ and the observed fit $\hat Y_i = b_0 + b_1 X_i$ with
residuals $e_i = Y_i - \hat Y_i$:

$$
\text{for } b = 1, \dots, B: \quad
Y_i^{\ast} = \hat Y_i + e_{s(i)}^{\ast}, \quad
b_1^{\ast b} = \frac{\sum (X_i - \bar X)(Y_i^{\ast} - \bar Y^{\ast})}{S_{xx}} ,
$$

where $e_{s(i)}^{\ast}$ is a residual drawn at random with replacement from $e_1, \dots, e_n$. In
words: keep the line and the strengths, then jiggle each fitted point up or down by a residual
borrowed from somewhere in the data. The **bootstrap standard error** of the slope is the standard
deviation of the collected slopes,

$$
s_{\text{boot}}\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{B-1}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\left(b_1^{\ast b} - \bar b_1^{\ast}\right)^2} ,
$$

the ordinary spread of the $B$ resampled slopes. It estimates how much $b_1$ would vary across
repeated studies.

:::{admonition} Definition 5.6: Bootstrap standard error
:class: note definition
The **bootstrap standard error** of a statistic is the standard deviation of its values across the
$B$ bootstrap resamples, $s_{\text{boot}}\{b_1\} = \operatorname{sd}(b_1^{\ast 1}, \dots, b_1^{\ast B})$.
It estimates the true sampling standard deviation of the statistic without any distributional
formula.
:::

## 5.5 Bootstrap confidence intervals

### Intuition

A standard error is a single number for the spread. A confidence interval is a range that should
contain the true slope $\beta_1$ with, say, $95\%$ confidence. The bootstrap distribution of the
slope already contains the information; we just have to read an interval off it. Two simple recipes
do this without assuming the distribution is normal (both are collected in Definition 5.7). The
**percentile interval** takes the middle $95\%$ of the bootstrap slopes directly. The **basic
interval** (also called the reflection interval) corrects for a subtle bias by flipping the
percentiles around the observed slope. When
the bootstrap distribution is symmetric they nearly coincide; when it is skewed they part ways, and
the basic interval is usually the better behaved of the two.

### Formula

Let $q_{\alpha/2}$ and $q_{1-\alpha/2}$ be the lower and upper percentiles of the $B$ bootstrap
slopes, for a $100(1-\alpha)\%$ interval (so $\alpha = 0.05$ for $95\%$). The two intervals are

$$
\text{percentile:} \quad \big(q_{\alpha/2}, \; q_{1-\alpha/2}\big), \qquad\qquad
\text{basic:} \quad \big(2 b_1 - q_{1-\alpha/2}, \; 2 b_1 - q_{\alpha/2}\big) .
$$

:::{admonition} Definition 5.7: Percentile and basic bootstrap intervals
:class: note definition
Let $q_{\alpha/2}$ and $q_{1-\alpha/2}$ be the lower and upper percentiles of the $B$ bootstrap
slopes. The $100(1-\alpha)\%$ **percentile interval** is $(q_{\alpha/2},\, q_{1-\alpha/2})$, read
straight off the bootstrap distribution. The **basic (reflection) interval** is
$(2 b_1 - q_{1-\alpha/2},\, 2 b_1 - q_{\alpha/2})$, the percentiles reflected through twice the
observed slope. The two coincide when the bootstrap distribution is symmetric about $b_1$.
:::

In words: the percentile interval is just the $2.5$th and $97.5$th percentiles of the resampled
slopes; the basic interval reflects those two percentiles through twice the observed slope. Notice
the basic interval swaps the roles of the two percentiles, because reflecting turns the upper tail
into the lower endpoint.

That swap is easier to see than to say. @fig-ch05-reflection draws it. Think of the observed slope
$b_1$ as a mirror standing in the middle. The percentile interval sits above; to get the basic
interval you reflect each endpoint through the mirror, so the point that was far to the right lands
far to the left and vice versa. The arithmetic $2b_1 - q$ is exactly "reflect $q$ across $b_1$": it
measures how far $q$ sits from $b_1$ and steps the same distance to the other side.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch05_reflection.png
:name: fig-ch05-reflection
:alt: A schematic right-skewed bootstrap distribution of the slope with a vertical green line at the observed slope b1 labeled the mirror. Above the distribution an orange percentile interval runs from the lower percentile q point 025 to the upper percentile q point 975. Below it a violet basic interval runs from 2b1 minus q point 975 to 2b1 minus q point 025. Two curved gray arrows show the right percentile reflecting through b1 to become the left basic endpoint and the left percentile reflecting to become the right basic endpoint.
The basic interval is the percentile interval mirrored through the observed slope b1. Reflecting swaps the tails: the far-right percentile becomes the left endpoint and the far-left percentile becomes the right endpoint. On a skewed distribution like this one the two intervals sit in different places; on a symmetric one they land on top of each other.
```

### Derivation (the basic bootstrap interval)

:::{admonition} Theorem 5.8: Basic bootstrap interval
:class: important theorem
Suppose the bootstrap principle holds, so that $b_1^{\ast} - b_1$ has approximately the same
distribution as $b_1 - \beta_1$. If $q_{\alpha/2}$ and $q_{1-\alpha/2}$ are the percentiles of the
bootstrap slopes $b_1^{\ast}$, then
$$
P\big(2 b_1 - q_{1-\alpha/2} \le \beta_1 \le 2 b_1 - q_{\alpha/2}\big) \approx 1 - \alpha,
$$
so the basic interval $(2 b_1 - q_{1-\alpha/2},\, 2 b_1 - q_{\alpha/2})$ has approximate coverage
$1 - \alpha$. It equals the percentile interval exactly when the bootstrap distribution is
symmetric about $b_1$.
:::

**Proof.** The bootstrap principle says the variation of the
resampled slope around the observed slope imitates the variation of the observed slope around the
true slope. Write this as: the distribution of $b_1^{\ast} - b_1$ approximates the distribution of
$b_1 - \beta_1$. Let $q_{\alpha/2}$ and $q_{1-\alpha/2}$ be the percentiles of $b_1^{\ast}$, so by
definition

$$
P\big(q_{\alpha/2} \le b_1^{\ast} \le q_{1-\alpha/2}\big) = 1 - \alpha .
$$

Subtract $b_1$ throughout to center on the observed slope:

$$
P\big(q_{\alpha/2} - b_1 \le b_1^{\ast} - b_1 \le q_{1-\alpha/2} - b_1\big) = 1 - \alpha .
$$

Now use the bootstrap principle to replace $b_1^{\ast} - b_1$ with $b_1 - \beta_1$, which has
approximately the same distribution:

$$
P\big(q_{\alpha/2} - b_1 \le b_1 - \beta_1 \le q_{1-\alpha/2} - b_1\big) \approx 1 - \alpha .
$$

Solve the inequalities for $\beta_1$. Subtracting $b_1$ and negating (which flips the inequality
directions and swaps the endpoints) gives

$$
P\big(2 b_1 - q_{1-\alpha/2} \le \beta_1 \le 2 b_1 - q_{\alpha/2}\big) \approx 1 - \alpha .
$$

That bracket is the basic interval. The percentile interval $(q_{\alpha/2}, q_{1-\alpha/2})$ is
what you get if you skip the pivoting step and read the bootstrap percentiles as if they were
percentiles of $\beta_1$ directly. The two agree exactly when the bootstrap distribution is
symmetric about $b_1$, because then reflecting the percentiles through $2 b_1$ lands them back on
themselves. $\blacksquare$

## 5.6 When computational and classical inference agree

You have now met every tool in the chapter. Before comparing their answers, it helps to see them
on one page. @fig-ch05-method-map is a map: it starts from your fitted slope and asks what you want
to know, then walks you to the right method. The first fork is the big one. If you want to know
whether an effect exists at all, you run a permutation test and report a p-value. If you want to
know how precise your estimate is, you run a bootstrap and report a standard error or confidence
interval. The bootstrap then forks again by how the data were collected: case resampling for
randomly sampled subjects, residual resampling for a design with fixed predictor values.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch05_method_map.png
:name: fig-ch05-method-map
:alt: A decision flowchart. The top box reads you have a fitted slope b1, what do you want to know. It branches left along an arrow labeled detect to a green box is there any effect at all, you want a p-value, which leads down to a permutation test box shuffle Y against fixed X refit collect slopes, then to report p equals M plus 1 over B plus 1. It branches right along an arrow labeled measure to an orange box how precise is the effect, you want an SE or CI, which leads to a bootstrap box resample with replacement refit take the spread, which forks to two boxes, subjects sampled at random giving case resampling redraw whole rows, and predictors fixed by design giving residual resampling fix line reattach residuals, both leading to report SE and a percentile or basic CI.
The chapter on one page. The first question, detect an effect or measure its precision, chooses between the permutation test and the bootstrap; the bootstrap then splits by how the data arose.
```

You have now seen three verdicts on the punting slope (permutation, bootstrap, classical) that all
pointed the same way, and a Toluca slope where they agreed up to the permutation test's resolution
floor. That is the common case: when the classical assumptions roughly hold, the computational and
classical answers match, and the agreement is itself evidence that the classical p-value can be
trusted. The methods earn their keep when the assumptions are shaky, because then the resampling
answer does not depend on those assumptions.

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
When computational and classical inference agree, the agreement is itself evidence that the
classical assumptions roughly hold and its p-value can be trusted. When they disagree by a lot,
that gap is a diagnostic: it warns that an assumption is failing, and the assumption-free
resampling answer is the safer one to report while you investigate.
:::

:::{admonition} Example 5.5: Bootstrap and classical standard errors on Toluca
:class: note
**Question.** On the clean $n = 25$ Toluca data, does the case bootstrap reproduce the classical
standard error of the slope?

**Intuition.** Case-resample the Toluca rows $5000$ times, take the spread of slopes, and compare
to the formula standard error $0.347$ from @ch02-gauss-markov-slr.

**Formula.** Compare $s_{\text{boot}}\{b_1\}$ with $s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}}$, where
$\mathrm{MSE}$ is the mean squared error (the residual variance estimate) from the fit.

**Computation.** The case bootstrap gives a standard error of about $0.377$ (R) or $0.366$
(Python), against the classical $0.347$.

**Interpretation.** The two standard errors are close, within about ten percent, which confirms
that with a healthy sample the bootstrap and the classical formula measure the same variability.
The bootstrap is a touch larger because case resampling adds the variation from the changing lot
sizes and because $n = 25$ is still modest. The lesson is that the bootstrap is not a rival that
overturns classical inference when the assumptions hold; it agrees with it, and that agreement is
what lets you trust the classical result. The bootstrap matters most when you cannot check the
assumptions, exactly the punting situation.
:::

A wrong reading to avoid: a student sees the bootstrap standard error $0.377$ next to the classical
$0.347$ and concludes "the bootstrap is wrong, because it does not match the formula." That gets it
backward. Neither number is the truth; both estimate the same unknown standard deviation of $b_1$,
and they differ only by sampling noise and by the small extra variability case resampling includes
on purpose. The right reading is that they agree well enough to reinforce each other. You would
only worry if they disagreed by a lot, which would be a signal that the classical assumptions are
failing and the bootstrap is telling you something the formula cannot.

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
A large gap means the classical formula is missing a source of variability. Likely culprits are
strong non-constant error variance, a few high-influence points whose repeated appearance in case
resamples swings the slope, or a badly non-normal error distribution in a small sample. In that
situation the classical standard error, which assumes constant variance and leans on normality, is
probably too small, and the case-bootstrap standard error, which makes neither assumption, is the
more trustworthy of the two. The disagreement is a diagnostic: it tells you to investigate the
assumptions before reporting the classical interval. Chapters 9 and 10 build the tools to find and
fix the underlying problem.
:::
::::

## 5.7 Chapter summary

You can now do inference for a regression slope without leaning on the normal error assumption.
When a small sample puts the $t$ and $F$ tests on thin ice and thirteen residuals cannot confirm
normality, you replace the untestable assumption with a computation. A permutation test shuffles
the response against the fixed predictor to build the no-association null of the slope and reports
$p_{\text{perm}} = (M+1)/(B+1)$; on the punting data it gave $\approx 0.001$, matching the
classical $0.00127$. A bootstrap resamples the data with replacement to measure precision: case
resampling and residual resampling gave slope standard errors of about $0.249$ and $0.198$, and
percentile and basic intervals near $(0.42, 1.39)$, all consistent with the classical
$(0.440, 1.364)$. The recurring lesson is that when computational and classical answers agree the
agreement builds trust, and when they diverge sharply that divergence is a warning about
assumptions.

**Key results at a glance.**

| Result | Statement or formula | Valid when |
|---|---|---|
| Permutation test (Definition 5.1) | Shuffle $Y$ against fixed $X$, refit, collect slopes | No-association null; any error distribution |
| Permutation p-value (Definition 5.2) | $p_{\text{perm}} = (M+1)/(B+1)$ | $M$ of $B$ reshuffles at least as extreme |
| Validity of the permutation p-value (Theorem 5.3) | $P(p_{\text{perm}} \le \alpha) \le \alpha$; floor $1/(B+1)$ | Under the no-association null |
| Bootstrap (Definition 5.4) | Resample with replacement, recompute, take the spread | Sample stands in for the population |
| Case vs residual resampling (Definition 5.5) | Rows with replacement, or $\hat Y_i + e^{\ast}_{s(i)}$ | Random subjects, or fixed-design predictor |
| Bootstrap standard error (Definition 5.6) | $s_{\text{boot}}\{b_1\} = \operatorname{sd}(b_1^{\ast 1}, \dots, b_1^{\ast B})$ | $B$ resamples of the fitted model |
| Percentile / basic intervals (Definition 5.7) | $(q_{\alpha/2}, q_{1-\alpha/2})$ / $(2b_1 - q_{1-\alpha/2}, 2b_1 - q_{\alpha/2})$ | Read off the bootstrap distribution |
| Basic interval by pivoting (Theorem 5.8) | Reflect percentiles through $2b_1$; equals percentile if symmetric | Bootstrap principle holds |

**plus-one correction**, **bootstrap**, **case resampling**, **residual resampling**, **bootstrap

## 5.8 Frequently asked questions

**Q1. Is a permutation test the same as a bootstrap?** No, though both resample. A permutation test
shuffles without replacement to break the link between $X$ and $Y$, building the distribution of a
statistic under the null hypothesis of no association; it answers "is there an effect?" The
bootstrap resamples with replacement to imitate repeated sampling from the population, building the
distribution of the estimate itself; it answers "how precise is the effect?" Use a permutation test
for a p-value, a bootstrap for a standard error or confidence interval.

**Q2. Why shuffle without replacement for the permutation test but with replacement for the
bootstrap?** The permutation test needs every reshuffle to be a genuine rearrangement of the same
values, so that it represents one of the equally likely pairings under the null; sampling without
replacement (a shuffle) does exactly that. The bootstrap needs each resample to look like a fresh
draw from the population, and real repeated samples can contain repeats and omissions, so it samples
with replacement.

**Q3. How many reshuffles or resamples do I need?** Enough that the answer stops moving. For a
p-value you are comparing to $0.05$, a few thousand reshuffles is usually plenty; near a sharp
cutoff, use more, because the Monte Carlo floor is $1/(B+1)$ and the noise shrinks like
$1/\sqrt{B}$. For a bootstrap standard error, $1000$ to $2000$ resamples often suffice; for the tail
percentiles of a confidence interval, use $5000$ or more, since the extreme quantiles are noisier
than the center.

**Q4. My permutation p-value is $0$. Do I report $p = 0$?** Never report exactly zero. If no
reshuffle out of $B$ beat your statistic, the plus-one correction gives $p_{\text{perm}} =
1/(B+1)$, and you report "less than" that: for $B = 5000$, report $p < 0.0002$. Reporting $p = 0$
would claim infinite certainty from finite data.

**Q5. Which bootstrap should I use, case or residual?** Match the resampling to how the data arose.
If the predictor values were fixed by design (a planned experiment with set doses, or the Toluca
lot sizes chosen by schedulers), residual resampling fits the model literally. If the rows are a
random sample from a population and the predictor is just another measured variable (the punters),
case resampling is more honest. It is also the safer default when you are unsure, because it
captures variability in the predictor and does not assume constant error variance.

**Q6. The bootstrap and the $t$ test gave slightly different intervals. Which is right?** Neither is
"right" in an absolute sense; both estimate the same true uncertainty. When they are close, as in
the punting and Toluca examples, trust either and note the agreement. When they differ a lot, the
disagreement is information: it usually means a classical assumption (normality, constant variance,
no influential points) is failing, and the assumption-free bootstrap is the safer report while you
investigate.

**Q7. Do these methods work for multiple regression?** Yes. The bootstrap generalizes directly:
case-resample whole rows, or residual-resample around the fitted surface, and collect whichever
coefficient you care about. Permutation is subtler with several predictors, because shuffling one
predictor while holding others fixed requires care about what null you are testing, but the core
idea carries over. This chapter keeps to simple regression so the logic stays visible; the
resampling code changes little when you add predictors.

**EP 5.1 (explain why).** The chapter calls the classical $t$ test for the punting slope "on thin
ice" with $n = 13$, yet it runs the permutation test on those same thirteen punters without
apology. Explain, in full sentences, exactly which assumption the $t$ test needs that the
permutation test does not, and why having only thirteen punters makes that difference matter here
instead of being a technicality.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The classical $t$ test treats the ratio $t^{\ast} = b_1 / s\{b_1\}$ as exactly $t$-distributed on
$n - 2$ degrees of freedom, and that distribution is exact only when the errors are normal. When
the errors are not normal, the $t$ result holds only as an approximation, and only when the sample
is large enough for a central-limit effect to smooth the slope estimate toward normality. With
thirteen punters there is no large-sample rescue, and thirteen residuals cannot tell a normal
distribution from a non-normal one (@fig-ch05-small-sample-qq), so the single premise the $t$ test
leans on is precisely the one that cannot be checked. That is what "thin ice" means: not that the
test is known to be wrong, but that its safety net is unverifiable.

The permutation test needs no distributional assumption at all. Under the no-association null the
thirteen distances are attached to the thirteen strengths without regard to them, so the observed
slope is literally one of the $13!$ equally likely pairings, and reshuffling generates that
reference distribution exactly from the data in hand. Normality never enters the argument, so the
small sample that undermines the $t$ test does not undermine the permutation test. The only price
the permutation test pays for small $n$ is a coarser reference set (fewer genuinely distinct
pairings), not a broken assumption.

A weak answer just says "permutation tests do not assume normality" without explaining that the
$t$ test's normality requirement is exactly the uncheckable one at $n = 13$, and without noting
that the permutation reference distribution comes from the equally likely pairings under the null.
:::

**EP 5.3 (a student claims something, evaluate it).** A student runs the punting permutation test
(`Distance ~ RStr`, $B = 5000$) twice, once with `set.seed(4210)` and once with `set.seed(2026)`,
and gets:

```text
seed 4210:  M = 4 extreme reshuffles,  p_perm = 0.00100
seed 2026:  M = 8 extreme reshuffles,  p_perm = 0.00180
```

The student concludes: "The permutation test is unreliable, because it gives a different answer
every time I run it. I should just report the classical $t$ test's p-value of $0.00127$, which is
exact and reproducible." Evaluate the student's reasoning. Say what is right and what is wrong in
each half of the claim.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The student is half right and half wrong. It is true that a Monte Carlo permutation p-value wobbles
from run to run, because it estimates the true permutation p-value from a finite random sample of
$B$ reshuffles. The values $0.00100$ and $0.00180$ are two such estimates, and the gap between them
is ordinary Monte Carlo noise, which shrinks like $1/\sqrt{B}$ and would nearly disappear if the
student ran far more reshuffles or enumerated all $13!$ pairings. But "different every time" is not
the same as "unreliable." Both runs place the p-value near $0.001$, both are far below any usual
cutoff, and both lead to exactly the same decision, so the conclusion is not in doubt. The
reproducibility worry is also already solved by fixing the seed, which the student did.

The second half of the claim is where the reasoning fails. The classical $0.00127$ is "exact" only
if its normality assumption holds, and the whole reason the chapter uses the punting data is that
thirteen residuals cannot confirm normality. So that number is not more trustworthy than the
permutation p-value, only more precise-looking. The honest report is the permutation p-value (about
$0.001$) together with the note that it agrees with the $t$ test, or a permutation run with a larger
$B$ if a sharper figure is wanted.

A weak answer says only "it is random, so that is fine" without naming Monte Carlo error, the
$1/\sqrt{B}$ rate, or the fact that both runs give the same decision, and it fails to challenge the
false claim that the $t$ test's p-value is exact here.
:::

**EP 5.4 (a student claims something, evaluate it).** The chapter's `RStr` example found the
case-bootstrap standard error ($0.249$) larger than the residual-bootstrap one ($0.198$), and
explained that case resampling "adds one more source of variability." A student turns this into a
rule: "Case resampling always gives a larger standard error than residual resampling, because it
lets the predictor values vary too." To test the rule they refit punt distance on overall leg
strength (`OStr`) with $B = 5000$ and get:

```text
Distance ~ OStr
classical formula standard error   = 0.0991
case-resampling bootstrap SE       = 0.0889
residual-resampling bootstrap SE   = 0.0910
```

Evaluate the student's rule in light of this output.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The rule is false as stated, and this output is a clean counterexample. Here the case-bootstrap
standard error ($0.089$) is actually smaller than the residual-bootstrap one ($0.091$), the reverse
of the `RStr` pattern. The chapter's explanation described a tendency, not a guarantee. Case
resampling and residual resampling estimate the same underlying sampling variability of the slope by
two different mechanisms, and which one comes out larger on any given dataset depends on the
particular residuals and high-leverage points that happen to get resampled, plus ordinary Monte
Carlo noise. With only thirteen points these accidents easily swamp the "extra source of variation"
argument.

The sound reading is that the three standard errors ($0.089$, $0.091$, and $0.099$) all agree to
within about ten percent and tell the same story about a slope of $0.43$, so the choice between the
two bootstraps should be made on the data-collection story (case resampling when the punters are a
random sample from a population, residual resampling when the strengths are treated as fixed by
design), not on which one happens to yield the bigger number.

A weak answer simply declares the rule right or wrong without using the printed numbers to show
that case is smaller than residual here, or without explaining that the "extra variation" effect is
only a tendency that small-sample noise can reverse.

**EP 5.5 (what would change if).** For the punting slope `Distance ~ RStr` (observed
$b_1 = 0.902$), answer each part in full sentences.

*(a)* The chapter uses $B = 5000$ reshuffles and reports $p_{\text{perm}} \approx 0.001$. What
would change about the number you could honestly report if you reran the permutation test with only
$B = 500$ reshuffles and again found no reshuffle beating the observed slope?

*(b)* The chapter case-resamples the punters because they are treated as a sample from a larger
population of punters. What would change about which bootstrap is the honest choice if instead these
thirteen were the entire group you cared about, say the complete roster of one team, and you only
wanted to describe the line within that fixed group?

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
*(a)* With $B = 500$ and $M = 0$, the permutation p-value would be $(0 + 1)/(500 + 1) = 1/501
\approx 0.002$, so the smallest number you could honestly report roughly doubles from the $B = 5000$
floor of $1/5001 \approx 0.0002$. Fewer reshuffles raise the resolution floor $1/(B + 1)$, so you
lose the ability to resolve very small p-values and your estimate is noisier from run to run, even
though the qualitative conclusion (strong evidence, p well below $0.05$) does not change. To report
anything smaller you would have to run more reshuffles.

*(b)* If the thirteen punters are the whole group and you only want the line within that fixed set,
the case for case resampling weakens. Case resampling imitates the randomness of drawing subjects
from a population, and there is no such randomness left once you already have everyone. The
variability worth modeling is then the scatter of the distances around the line, which is exactly
what residual resampling captures by holding the strengths fixed and reattaching resampled
residuals, so residual resampling (or the classical fixed-design view) becomes the more honest
choice. In the extreme where the fit is treated as a pure description of one fixed finite group,
inference about a wider population is arguably not even the right question to ask.

A weak answer to (a) misses that it is the floor $1/(B + 1)$ that changes (about $0.002$ versus
$0.0002$) rather than the decision; a weak answer to (b) just repeats "use case resampling" without
recognizing that removing the sampling-of-subjects randomness removes the very reason to prefer it.
:::

:::{admonition} Resumen del capítulo (en español)
:class: dropdown
Este capítulo presenta la **inferencia por remuestreo** para la pendiente de una regresión, útil
cuando la muestra es pequeña y no se puede confiar en el supuesto de errores normales. El ejemplo
motivador son los datos de **punting**: trece pateadores, con la distancia del punt frente a la
fuerza de la pierna derecha. La prueba $t$ del Capítulo 3 descansa en supuestos que no se pueden
verificar, porque trece residuos no distinguen lo normal de lo no normal.

La **prueba de permutación** responde si existe una asociación. Se mezcla la respuesta contra el
predictor fijo muchas veces, rompiendo cualquier vínculo, y se calcula la pendiente en cada mezcla.
El **valor p de permutación** es $p = (M+1)/(B+1)$, donde $M$ cuenta las mezclas con pendiente tan
extrema como la observada. Para los punters, con $B = 5000$, resultó $p \approx 0.001$, cercano al
$0.00127$ de la prueba $t$. Para Toluca, ninguna mezcla alcanzó la pendiente real $3.5702$, así que
$p < 1/5001$. La corrección de "más uno" evita reportar un valor p de exactamente cero.

El **bootstrap** mide la precisión de la estimación. El **remuestreo de casos** toma filas completas
con reemplazo; el **remuestreo de residuos** mantiene fijos los predictores y la recta ajustada, y
reasigna residuos con reemplazo. La desviación estándar de las pendientes remuestreadas es el
**error estándar bootstrap**. De ahí se leen dos intervalos: el **de percentiles** y el **básico**,
que refleja los percentiles a través de $2 b_1$. Para los punters, ambos dieron cerca de
$(0.42, 1.39)$, algo más anchos que el intervalo $t$ clásico $(0.440, 1.364)$.

La lección final: cuando los supuestos clásicos se cumplen, los métodos computacionales y clásicos
concuerdan, lo que justifica confiar en la prueba $t$. Cuando difieren mucho, es señal de que algún
supuesto falla, y el bootstrap, que no lo necesita, es el reporte más seguro.
:::

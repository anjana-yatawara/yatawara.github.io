# 3. Inference for simple linear regression

:::{admonition} Espanol
:class: tip
Esta leccion esta disponible en espanol: [Leer en espanol](./es.md)
:::

In @ch02-least-squares we fit a line to the Toluca Company's 25 production runs and got a slope
of $b_1 = 3.5702$ hours per unit of lot size. The engineers were pleased, and then they asked the
questions any careful person asks about a number: how sure are you? A different 25 runs would have
given a slightly different slope. Could the true slope really be zero, meaning lot size does not
affect hours at all and we are fooling ourselves? And the practical one, the reason they collected
the data: if we schedule a new run of 100 units next week, how many hours should we budget, and how
far off might that be?

Chapter 2 gave point estimates: single best guesses for the intercept, slope, and error variance.
A point estimate with no measure of its uncertainty is half an answer. This chapter supplies the
other half. We attach margins of error to the slope and intercept, test whether the slope is really
different from zero, and, most importantly for planning, build two very different intervals around a
prediction. One says where the *average* run of a given size lands. The other says where a *single
new* run lands. @fig-ch03-ci-vs-pi shows both drawn around the Toluca line, and the gap between them
is the single most useful idea in the chapter.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_ci_vs_pi.png
:name: fig-ch03-ci-vs-pi
:alt: The Toluca scatterplot with the fitted line and two shaded bands. A narrow inner blue band hugs the line closely; a much wider outer yellow band surrounds it. Both are narrowest near the middle lot sizes and flare out toward the ends. Nearly all 25 data points fall inside the outer band, and the points scatter well outside the inner band.
The inner (blue) band is a 95% confidence band for the mean hours at each lot size; the outer (yellow) band is a 95% prediction band for a single new run. The prediction band must cover one run's own random scatter, so it is far wider. Confusing the two is the most common and most expensive mistake in applied regression.
```

Everything here rests on one added assumption that Chapter 2 introduced but did not yet use for
inference: the errors are normally distributed (@ch02-mle). With that assumption the sampling
distributions sketched in @ch02-gauss-markov-slr become exact $t$ and $F$ distributions, and honest
intervals and tests follow.

:::{admonition} This lesson at a glance
:class: important
- **What we are doing:** Attaching uncertainty to the Toluca fit: confidence intervals and tests for the slope and intercept, the ANOVA table and $F$ test, and two different intervals around a prediction.
- **Why we are doing it:** A point estimate with no margin of error is half an answer; the engineers need to know whether the true slope could be zero and how many hours to budget for a new run.
- **Main objective:** Build and interpret $t$ intervals and tests and the ANOVA $F$ test, and distinguish a confidence interval for a mean response from a prediction interval for a new observation.
- **What changed from the last chapters:** Chapter 2 gave point estimates assuming only that the errors have mean zero, constant variance, and are uncorrelated; this chapter adds the normal-errors assumption (@ch02-mle), which turns the sampling distributions into exact $t$ and $F$. The Toluca fit ($b_1 = 3.5702$) is unchanged, now carrying inference.

## 3.1 The sampling distributions of $b_0$ and $b_1$

### Intuition

We already know from @ch02-gauss-markov-slr that the slope estimate $b_1$ is unbiased, with variance
$\sigma^2/S_{xx}$, where $S_{xx} = \sum_i (X_i - \bar{X})^2$ is the spread of the predictor values about
their mean $\bar{X}$. That tells us where $b_1$ centers and how much it spreads, but not the *shape* of
its distribution. To build an interval we need the shape. The trick is that $b_1$ is a weighted sum
of the responses, and a weighted sum of independent normal random variables is itself normal. So the
moment we assume normal errors, $b_1$ is exactly normal, and we can standardize it the way intro
statistics standardizes a sample mean.

There is one wrinkle. Standardizing needs the true error standard deviation $\sigma$, which we do not
know. We replace it with the estimate $s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}}$. Swapping a known constant for a noisy
estimate adds a little extra uncertainty, and that extra uncertainty is exactly what turns the normal
distribution into a $t$ distribution. @fig-ch03-t-sampling confirms this: standardize 5000 simulated
slopes by their own estimated standard errors, and the pile matches a $t$ curve, with slightly heavier
tails than the normal.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_t_sampling.png
:name: fig-ch03-t-sampling
:alt: A histogram of 5000 standardized slope estimates, bell-shaped and centered at zero. A red t-distribution curve with 23 degrees of freedom traces the histogram closely, while a dashed orange standard-normal curve sits slightly too low in the tails and slightly too high in the middle.
Five thousand slope estimates, each standardized by its own estimated standard error. The pile follows a t distribution with 23 degrees of freedom (red), whose tails are a touch heavier than the standard normal (dashed). Using s in place of sigma is what makes the reference distribution t rather than normal.
```

### Formula

Under the normal error model $Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \varepsilon_i$ with
$\varepsilon_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0, \sigma^2)$, the estimators are exactly normal:

$$
b_1 \sim N\!\left(\beta_1, \ \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{xx}}\right), \qquad
b_0 \sim N\!\left(\beta_0, \ \sigma^2\Big(\tfrac{1}{n} + \tfrac{\bar{X}^2}{S_{xx}}\Big)\right).
$$

In words: each estimator is centered on the parameter it estimates, with the variance we derived in
Chapter 2, and now with a normal shape. To standardize either one we need the spread of the estimate.
The **estimated standard error** of the slope (Definition 3.1) supplies it.

:::{admonition} Definition 3.1: Estimated standard error
:class: note definition
The **estimated standard error** of an estimator is the estimated standard deviation of its sampling
distribution. For the slope and intercept,

$$
s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}}, \qquad
s\{b_0\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}\Big(\tfrac{1}{n} + \tfrac{\bar{X}^2}{S_{xx}}\Big)},
$$

with $s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}}$ standing in for the unknown error standard deviation $\sigma$.
:::

The estimated standard error $s\{b_1\}$ is our best guess at how much $b_1$ bounces from one sample to
the next. Standardizing $b_1$ with it gives a $t$ statistic, the result stated next.

:::{admonition} Theorem 3.2: Sampling distributions of $b_0$ and $b_1$
:class: important theorem
Under the SLR model with normal errors $\varepsilon_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0, \sigma^2)$, the
estimators are exactly normal, $b_1 \sim N(\beta_1, \sigma^2/S_{xx})$ and
$b_0 \sim N\!\big(\beta_0, \sigma^2(1/n + \bar{X}^2/S_{xx})\big)$, and standardizing by the estimated
standard error gives

$$
\frac{b_1 - \beta_1}{s\{b_1\}} \sim t_{n-2}, \qquad
\frac{b_0 - \beta_0}{s\{b_0\}} \sim t_{n-2}.
$$
:::

In words: subtract the true value and divide by the estimated standard error, and the result follows a
$t$ distribution with $n-2$ degrees of freedom. The degrees of freedom are $n-2$ because that is the
divisor in $\mathrm{MSE}$, the two lost degrees paying for $b_0$ and $b_1$ (@ch02-sigma). This single
$t$ result powers every interval and test in the rest of the chapter.

That last sentence is worth a picture. Almost everything ahead is one idea, the $t$ statistic above,
pointed at a different estimate each time. @fig-ch03-t-roadmap is the map of the chapter on one page:
keep it in mind and each new section becomes "the same move, new target."


### Derivation

**Derivation (the $t$ statistic for the slope).** We assemble the result from three facts.

*Normality.* From @ch02-gauss-markov-slr, $b_1 = \sum_i k_i Y_i$ with $k_i = (X_i - \bar{X})/S_{xx}$.
Under the normal error model the $Y_i$ are independent normal variables, and any linear combination of
independent normals is normal. With the mean and variance already derived, $b_1 \sim N(\beta_1, \sigma^2/S_{xx})$.
Dividing by the true standard deviation gives a standard normal:

$$
Z = \frac{b_1 - \beta_1}{\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}} \sim N(0,1).
$$

*A chi-square, independent of the slope.* It can be shown that

$$
\frac{\mathrm{SSE}}{\sigma^2} = \frac{(n-2)\,\mathrm{MSE}}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-2},
$$

and that this quantity is statistically independent of $b_1$ (indeed of the whole vector $(b_0, b_1)$).
The clean proof uses the hat matrix and a theorem on quadratic forms in normal vectors; we give it in
full in @ch07-hat-matrix, once we have the matrix tools. For now we take these two facts on faith, and
note that the simulation in @fig-ch03-t-sampling is a direct check that the end result is right.

*Combine into a $t$.* A standard normal divided by the square root of an independent chi-square over
its degrees of freedom is, by definition, a $t$ random variable:

$$
\frac{b_1 - \beta_1}{s\{b_1\}}
= \frac{(b_1 - \beta_1)\big/\big(\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}\big)}{\sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/\sigma^2}}
= \frac{Z}{\sqrt{\chi^2_{n-2}/(n-2)}} \sim t_{n-2}.
$$

The middle step just multiplies top and bottom out: the numerator is $Z$, and $s\{b_1\}/(\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}})
= \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/\sigma^2} = \sqrt{\chi^2_{n-2}/(n-2)}$. The identical argument with the weights
$m_i$ from @ch02-gauss-markov-slr gives the $t$ statistic for $b_0$. $\blacksquare$

The setup code below refits the Toluca model and recovers the standard errors we will use throughout.

```r
toluca <- read.csv("data/toluca.csv")
fit <- lm(hours ~ lotsize, data = toluca)
n <- nrow(toluca)
x <- toluca$lotsize
y <- toluca$hours
Sxx <- sum((x - mean(x))^2)
MSE <- sum(residuals(fit)^2) / (n - 2)
round(c(n = n, Sxx = Sxx, MSE = MSE, s = sqrt(MSE),
        se_b1 = sqrt(MSE / Sxx)), 4)
```
```text
         n        Sxx        MSE          s      se_b1 
   25.0000 19800.0000  2383.7156    48.8233     0.3470 
```

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

toluca = pd.read_csv("data/toluca.csv")
fit = smf.ols("hours ~ lotsize", data=toluca).fit()
n = len(toluca)
x = toluca["lotsize"].to_numpy()
y = toluca["hours"].to_numpy()
Sxx = np.sum((x - x.mean()) ** 2)
MSE = np.sum(fit.resid ** 2) / (n - 2)
print(round(MSE, 4), round(np.sqrt(MSE), 4), round(np.sqrt(MSE / Sxx), 4))
```
```text
2383.7156 48.8233 0.347
```

The estimated standard error of the slope is $s\{b_1\} = 0.347$ hours per unit, the same number
Chapter 2 computed from the variance formula. It is the yardstick for everything that follows.

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
If we knew $\sigma$, the standardized slope would be exactly standard normal. We do not: we estimate it
with $s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}}$. That single substitution injects extra uncertainty and is the whole
reason inference here uses a $t$ distribution with $n-2$ degrees of freedom rather than the normal. As
$n$ grows and $s$ steadies, the $t$ shrinks back toward the normal.
:::

::::{admonition} Try it 3.1
:class: important
The standardized slope follows a $t_{23}$ distribution, not a standard normal. For a 95% interval, the
normal multiplier is $1.96$. Look up the $t_{23}$ multiplier (in R, `qt(0.975, 23)`). Is it larger or
smaller than $1.96$, and why does that direction make sense?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
The multiplier is $t_{0.975, 23} = 2.069$, larger than $1.96$. It has to be larger: replacing the known
$\sigma$ with the estimate $s$ injects extra uncertainty, so the $t$ distribution has heavier tails than
the normal, and a wider multiplier is needed to still capture 95% of the distribution. As $n$ grows, the
estimate $s$ becomes reliable and $t_{n-2}$ approaches the normal, so the multiplier shrinks toward $1.96$.
:::
::::

(ch03-slope-inference)=
## 3.2 Inference for the slope

### Intuition

The slope is usually the number a study is about: does lot size affect hours, and by how much? Two
questions come in a pair. A **confidence interval** (Definition 3.3) answers "how much?" by giving a
range of plausible values for $\beta_1$. A **hypothesis test** answers "is there any effect at all?" by
asking whether zero is a plausible value. Both are built from the one $t$ statistic of @ch03-sampling, so they always agree:
a 95% interval excludes zero exactly when the two-sided test rejects at the 5% level.

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 3.3: Confidence interval for the slope
:class: note definition
A **$100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval** for the slope $\beta_1$ is

$$
b_1 \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}\; s\{b_1\}, \qquad s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}},
$$

the estimate reaching out $t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}$ estimated standard errors on each side. It covers the
true slope in $100(1-\alpha)\%$ of repeated samples.
:::

In words: take the estimate and reach out a multiplier's worth of standard errors on each side. To
**test** $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$ against $H_a: \beta_1 \ne 0$, form the test statistic

$$
t^{\ast} = \frac{b_1 - 0}{s\{b_1\}},
$$

and reject $H_0$ when $|t^{\ast}| > t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}$, or equivalently when the two-sided p-value
$2\,P(t_{n-2} > |t^{\ast}|)$ is below $\alpha$. In words: if the estimate sits many standard errors away
from zero, zero is not credible. Nothing here is special to zero; to test $H_0: \beta_1 = c$ for any
value $c$, put $c$ in place of the $0$.

:::{admonition} Example 3.1: Is lot size related to hours, and by how much?
:class: note
**Question.** Test whether the Toluca slope differs from zero at the 5% level, and give a 95% confidence
interval for it.

**Intuition.** The estimate $3.57$ is about ten standard errors of $0.347$ away from zero, so zero will
not survive. The interval reaches out $t_{0.975, 23} = 2.069$ standard errors each way.

**Formula.** $t^{\ast} = b_1/s\{b_1\}$ and $b_1 \pm t_{0.975, 23}\, s\{b_1\}$.

**Computation.**


**Interpretation.** The test statistic is $t^{\ast} = 10.29$, far beyond the critical value $2.069$,
with a two-sided p-value of $4.45 \times 10^{-10}$. We reject $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$: lot size is strongly
related to work hours. The 95% confidence interval is $(2.85, 4.29)$ hours per unit. So a plausible
range for the true effect of one more unit per lot is roughly 2.9 to 4.3 hours, and the whole interval
sits well above zero. That tiny p-value is the number Chapter 5 will compare against a permutation test
that assumes nothing about normality (@ch05-permutation-slope).
:::

:::{admonition} Durable skill: Report an estimate with its uncertainty
:class: tip
An estimate alone ($b_1 = 3.57$) invites false confidence; an estimate with an interval ($3.57$, 95% CI
$2.85$ to $4.29$) tells the reader how firmly the data pin it down. Whenever you report a number that
came from data, report a range with it, and say what the range means. This habit separates an analyst
from a calculator, and it is what a decision-maker actually needs. To practice, never write a slope,
mean, or proportion in a report without the interval beside it.
:::

::::{admonition} Try it 3.2
:class: important
A colleague tests $H_0: \beta_1 = 3$ (a supplier's claimed rate of 3 hours per unit) against a two-sided
alternative. Using $b_1 = 3.5702$ and $s\{b_1\} = 0.3470$, compute $t^{\ast}$ and decide at the 5% level.

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
$t^{\ast} = (3.5702 - 3)/0.3470 = 1.643$. Compared with $t_{0.975, 23} = 2.069$, we have $|t^{\ast}| < 2.069$,
so we do **not** reject: a true rate of 3 hours per unit is consistent with the data. Equivalently, $3$
lies inside the 95% interval $(2.85, 4.29)$. Notice the same data reject $\beta_1 = 0$ overwhelmingly but
cannot rule out $\beta_1 = 3$; "significant" always means "significantly different from a specific value,"
almost always zero.
:::

(ch03-intercept-inference)=
## 3.3 Inference for the intercept

The intercept is where the fitted line crosses the vertical axis: the model's predicted hours for a lot of
zero units. It is the same kind of estimate as the slope, so it gets the exact same machinery. Follow the
roadmap in @fig-ch03-t-roadmap one box over, and only the standard error changes.

The intercept gets the same treatment, with its own standard error
$s\{b_0\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}\,(1/n + \bar{X}^2/S_{xx})}$. The confidence interval is
$b_0 \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}\, s\{b_0\}$, and the test of $H_0: \beta_0 = 0$ uses $t^{\ast} = b_0/s\{b_0\}$.

The reason the intercept comes second is that it is usually less interesting and often not interpretable.
For Toluca, $\beta_0$ is the mean hours at a lot size of zero, an extrapolation far outside the data
(@ch02-least-squares), so a test of $\beta_0 = 0$ answers a question nobody asked. The interval is still
worth computing, if only to see how loosely the intercept is pinned down.

```r
confint(fit, "(Intercept)", level = 0.95)
```
```text
               2.5 %  97.5 %
(Intercept) 8.213711 116.518
```

```python
print(fit.conf_int(alpha=0.05).loc["Intercept"])
```
```text
0      8.213711
1    116.518006
Name: Intercept, dtype: float64
```

The 95% interval for the intercept runs from about $8$ to $117$ hours, a span of over 100 hours. That
enormous width is the price of extrapolating to $X = 0$, which sits 70 units below the smallest observed
lot. Compare it with the slope's tight interval: the slope is anchored by data on both sides, the intercept
is not.

::::{admonition} Try it 3.3
:class: important
The intercept's p-value in the summary table is $0.026$, below $0.05$. A student concludes "the setup time
for a lot of zero units is significantly greater than zero, so there is real fixed cost." What is wrong with
this reading?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
Two things. First, $X = 0$ is far outside the data (the smallest lot is 20 units), so $b_0$ is an
extrapolation and does not estimate a real "setup time." Second, statistical significance says the estimate
is far from zero relative to its standard error; it does not certify that the quantity is physically
meaningful. A significant intercept for extrapolated data is a fact about the fitted line's height, not
evidence about hours to make nothing.
:::

(ch03-mean-response)=
## 3.4 Confidence interval for a mean response

### Intuition

Now we move from the coefficients to a prediction. Ask: across all runs of lot size $X_h = 65$, what is
the *average* number of hours? That average is the point on the true line, $E\{Y_h\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_h$,
and our estimate of it is the fitted value $\hat{Y}_h = b_0 + b_1 X_h$. Because $b_0$ and $b_1$ wobble from
sample to sample, so does $\hat{Y}_h$, and we want an interval for the true mean.

The key feature is that the interval's width depends on where $X_h$ sits. Near the center $\bar{X}$ the
fitted line is pinned down tightly, so the interval is narrow. Far from $\bar{X}$ a small tilt in the slope
swings the line up or down a lot, so the interval widens. This is the pinch we saw in the bundle of fitted
lines in @fig-ch02-sampling-lines, now made into a formula.

### Formula

The estimated variance of the fitted mean at $X_h$ is

$$
s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \mathrm{MSE}\left(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{S_{xx}}\right),
$$

and the confidence interval for the mean response follows.

:::{admonition} Definition 3.4: Confidence interval for a mean response
:class: note definition
A **$100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for the mean response** $E\{Y_h\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_h$ at a
given predictor value $X_h$ is

$$
\hat{Y}_h \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}\; s\{\hat{Y}_h\}, \qquad
s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \mathrm{MSE}\left(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{S_{xx}}\right).
$$

It is narrowest at $X_h = \bar{X}$ and widens as $X_h$ moves away.
:::

In words: the further $X_h$ is from the average predictor value $\bar{X}$, the larger the second term
inside the parentheses, and the wider the interval. At $X_h = \bar{X}$ the second term vanishes and the
interval is at its narrowest, width set only by $1/n$.

### Derivation

:::{admonition} Theorem 3.5: Variance of the fitted mean
:class: important theorem
Under the SLR model, the fitted value $\hat{Y}_h = b_0 + b_1 X_h$ at a fixed $X_h$ has variance

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \sigma^2\!\left(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{S_{xx}}\right),
$$

which the estimate $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ obtains by replacing $\sigma^2$ with $\mathrm{MSE}$.
:::

**Derivation (variance of the fitted mean).** Write the fitted value in its pivoted form, using
$b_0 = \bar{Y} - b_1 \bar{X}$:

$$
\hat{Y}_h = b_0 + b_1 X_h = \bar{Y} + b_1 (X_h - \bar{X}).
$$

The two pieces $\bar{Y}$ and $b_1$ are uncorrelated. To see it, recall $b_1 = \sum k_i Y_i$ and
$\bar{Y} = \sum \tfrac{1}{n} Y_i$, so, using independence of the $Y_i$ and $\sum k_i = 0$,

$$
\operatorname{Cov}\{\bar{Y}, b_1\} = \sum_i \frac{1}{n}\, k_i \operatorname{Var}\{Y_i\}
= \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \sum_i k_i = 0.
$$

Because the two pieces are uncorrelated, their variances add:

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \operatorname{Var}\{\bar{Y}\} + (X_h - \bar{X})^2 \operatorname{Var}\{b_1\}
= \frac{\sigma^2}{n} + (X_h - \bar{X})^2 \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{xx}}
= \sigma^2\!\left(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{S_{xx}}\right).
$$

Replacing $\sigma^2$ by $\mathrm{MSE}$ gives $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$. The $t$ interval then follows from the same
standardize-and-divide argument as in @ch03-sampling, since $(\hat{Y}_h - E\{Y_h\})/s\{\hat{Y}_h\} \sim t_{n-2}$. $\blacksquare$

@fig-ch03-band-width plots $s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ against $X_h$: a curve that dips to its minimum at $\bar{X} = 70$
and rises symmetrically on both sides. That curve is what gives the confidence band in @fig-ch03-ci-vs-pi its
gentle bow-tie shape.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_band_width.png
:name: fig-ch03-band-width
:alt: Two curves of estimated standard error against lot size. The lower blue curve, for the mean response, is U-shaped, dipping to about 10 at the mean lot size 70 and rising toward the ends. The upper yellow curve, for a new observation, is nearly flat near 49 to 51 across the whole range, always far above the blue curve.
The mean-response standard error (blue) is smallest at the mean lot size and grows toward the extremes. The prediction standard error (yellow) stays near 49 hours everywhere, because it is dominated by a single run's own scatter, which does not shrink no matter where you predict.
```

:::{admonition} Example 3.2: Mean hours for lots of 65 units

## 3.5 Prediction interval for a new observation

### Intuition

Here is the distinction that trips up more practitioners than any other. The confidence interval of
@ch03-mean-response is about the *average* run of size $X_h$. But the engineers do not schedule an average
run; they schedule one specific run next week, and they want to know where *its* hours will land. A single
run scatters around the mean by its own random error $\varepsilon$, and no amount of data removes that
scatter. So predicting a single new observation carries two sources of uncertainty: our uncertainty about
where the line is (the same $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ as before), plus the new run's own irreducible variance
$\sigma^2$. The interval that accounts for both is the **prediction interval** (Definition 3.6), and it is
always wider than the confidence interval.

:::{admonition} A common wrong reading, corrected
:class: warning
A manager sees the mean-response interval $(274, 315)$ for lot size 65 and budgets a specific new 65-unit run
at "somewhere between 274 and 315 hours, 95% sure." That is wrong, and it will blow the budget. That interval
is for the *average* of many runs; a single run scatters far more. The right interval for one new run at
$X_h = 65$ is the prediction interval $(191, 397)$, more than twice as wide. Use the confidence interval to
describe the process on average; use the prediction interval to plan for one case.
:::

The whole choice comes down to one question, and @fig-ch03-which-interval lays it out. Ask what you are
bounding: the average outcome over many cases at $X_h$, or the outcome of one specific new case. The left
answer is a confidence interval; the right answer is a prediction interval. Decide the question first, and
the formula follows.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_which_interval.png
:name: fig-ch03-which-interval
:alt: A decision flowchart. A top box reads you have a fitted line and a predictor value X-h, what do you want to bound. Two arrows split downward. The left branch reads the average outcome over many cases at X-h, leading to a confidence interval for the mean, Y-hat-h plus or minus t times s of Y-hat-h, and then to a note that it is narrower and shrinks toward zero as n grows. The right branch reads one specific new case at X-h, leading to a prediction interval for a new case, Y-hat-h plus or minus t times s of pred, and then to a note that it is wider, keeps the case's own scatter sigma squared, and never shrinks to zero.
Pick the interval by naming the question, not by taste. Averaging over many cases at X_h calls for the confidence interval (left, narrow); planning for one new case calls for the prediction interval (right, wide). The only difference in the formula is the extra sigma-squared the new case brings.
```

### Formula

For a new observation $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ at $X_h$, the estimated variance of the prediction error is
$s^2\{\text{pred}\} = \mathrm{MSE}(1 + 1/n + (X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx})$, and the prediction interval follows.

:::{admonition} Definition 3.6: Prediction interval for a new observation
:class: note definition
A **$100(1-\alpha)\%$ prediction interval** for a single new observation $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ at $X_h$ is

$$
\hat{Y}_h \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}\; s\{\text{pred}\}, \qquad
s^2\{\text{pred}\} = \mathrm{MSE}\left(1 + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{S_{xx}}\right).
$$

The leading $1$ inside the parentheses is the new run's own scatter, absent from the mean-response
interval, so the prediction interval is always wider.
:::

In words: this is the mean-response variance with an extra $\mathrm{MSE}$ (the leading $1$) added for the new
run's own scatter. That extra term is usually the dominant one, which is why the prediction interval is much
wider and why, unlike the confidence interval, it never shrinks to zero even with infinite data.

### Derivation

:::{admonition} Theorem 3.7: Variance of a prediction
:class: important theorem
Under the SLR model, the prediction error $Y_{h(\text{new})} - \hat{Y}_h$ for a new observation at $X_h$,
whose error $\varepsilon_{\text{new}}$ is independent of the fitting data, has variance

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{Y_{h(\text{new})} - \hat{Y}_h\}
= \sigma^2\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{S_{xx}}\right),
$$

which the estimate $s^2\{\text{pred}\}$ obtains by replacing $\sigma^2$ with $\mathrm{MSE}$.
:::

**Derivation (variance of a prediction).** The quantity we bound is the prediction error
$Y_{h(\text{new})} - \hat{Y}_h$, the gap between the actual new run and our forecast. The new run is
$Y_{h(\text{new})} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_h + \varepsilon_{\text{new}}$, and its error $\varepsilon_{\text{new}}$
is independent of the past data that produced $\hat{Y}_h$. Two independent pieces, so their variances add:

$$
\operatorname{Var}\{Y_{h(\text{new})} - \hat{Y}_h\}
= \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}\{\varepsilon_{\text{new}}\}}_{\sigma^2}
+ \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}\{\hat{Y}_h\}}_{\sigma^2(1/n + (X_h - \bar X)^2/S_{xx})}
= \sigma^2\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{S_{xx}}\right).
$$

Replacing $\sigma^2$ by $\mathrm{MSE}$ gives $s^2\{\text{pred}\}$, and standardizing the prediction error by
$s\{\text{pred}\}$ again yields a $t_{n-2}$ distribution. The $t$ interval follows. $\blacksquare$

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
A confidence interval and a prediction interval answer two different questions, not one question at two
confidence levels. The confidence interval bounds the *average* response at $X_h$; the prediction
interval bounds a *single new* response, so it carries the new case's own error $\sigma^2$ on top. That
extra term never vanishes, even with infinite data, which is why the prediction interval stays wide.
Decide which question you are asking before you compute anything.
:::

## 3.6 The analysis of variance approach

### Intuition

There is a second, equivalent way to see the same inference, and it scales up beautifully to the multiple
regression of later chapters. The idea is bookkeeping for variation. The response values $Y_i$ vary; some of
that variation the line explains, and some it leaves as residual scatter. If we can split the total variation
cleanly into an "explained" part and a "leftover" part, we can compare them, and a big explained part relative
to leftover is evidence that the slope is real.

@fig-ch03-anova-decomp shows the split for a single run: the total deviation from the mean, $Y_i - \bar{Y}$,
breaks into the part the line accounts for, $\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}$, plus the residual, $Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$.
Remarkably, when you square these and sum over all runs, the cross term vanishes and the totals split just as
cleanly.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_anova_decomp.png
:name: fig-ch03-anova-decomp
:alt: The Toluca scatterplot with the fitted line and a horizontal dashed line at the mean of Y. One run at the largest lot size is highlighted in red. Three double-headed arrows next to it show the total deviation from the mean split into an explained part, from the mean line up to the fitted line, and a residual part, from the fitted line to the point.
For one highlighted run, the total deviation from the mean (violet) equals the part the line explains (orange, mean up to the fitted line) plus the residual (red, fitted line to the point). Squaring and summing over all runs gives SSTO = SSR + SSE, because the cross term is zero.
```

### Formula

Define the three sums of squares:

$$
\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \bar{Y})^2}_{\mathrm{SSTO}} \;=\;
\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2}_{\mathrm{SSR}} \;+\;
\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2}_{\mathrm{SSE}} .
$$

In words: the total variation of $Y$ about its mean (SSTO) equals the variation the regression explains (SSR,
the spread of the fitted values about the mean) plus the variation it does not (SSE, the error sum of
squares from @ch02-sigma). Each sum has degrees of freedom: $n-1$ for SSTO, $1$ for SSR (one predictor), and
$n-2$ for SSE. The degrees of freedom add the same way, $n-1 = 1 + (n-2)$. Dividing each sum of squares by its
degrees of freedom gives a **mean square** (Definition 3.8).

:::{admonition} Definition 3.8: Mean square
:class: note definition
A **mean square** is a sum of squares divided by its degrees of freedom. The regression and error mean
squares are

$$
\mathrm{MSR} = \frac{\mathrm{SSR}}{1}, \qquad \mathrm{MSE} = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}}{n-2}.
$$
:::

In words: a mean square is a sum of squares averaged over its degrees of freedom, so it is a variance-like
number. MSR and MSE are the two we will put in a ratio to test the slope.

### Derivation

:::{admonition} Theorem 3.9: The ANOVA decomposition
:class: important theorem
Under the SLR model, the total variation of the response splits exactly into an explained part and a
residual part,

$$
\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \bar{Y})^2}_{\mathrm{SSTO}} =
\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2}_{\mathrm{SSR}} +
\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2}_{\mathrm{SSE}},
$$

with degrees of freedom adding the same way, $n-1 = 1 + (n-2)$.
:::

**Derivation ($\mathrm{SSTO} = \mathrm{SSR} + \mathrm{SSE}$).** Split each total deviation by routing it
through the fitted value:

$$
Y_i - \bar{Y} = (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}) + (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i).
$$

Square both sides and sum over $i$:

$$
\sum (Y_i - \bar{Y})^2 = \sum (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 + \sum (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2 + 2\sum (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i).
$$

The cross term is zero. Write $Y_i - \hat{Y}_i = e_i$ and expand:

$$
\sum (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}) e_i = \sum \hat{Y}_i e_i - \bar{Y} \sum e_i.
$$

Both pieces vanish by the residual identities of @ch02-residuals: $\sum \hat{Y}_i e_i = 0$ (fitted values
orthogonal to residuals) and $\sum e_i = 0$. So the cross term is zero and $\mathrm{SSTO} = \mathrm{SSR} + \mathrm{SSE}$.
The residual identities we proved in Chapter 2 are doing the work again. $\blacksquare$

:::{admonition} Theorem 3.10: Expected mean squares
:class: important theorem
Under the SLR model,

$$
E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \sigma^2, \qquad E\{\mathrm{MSR}\} = \sigma^2 + \beta_1^2 S_{xx}.
$$

MSR exceeds $\sigma^2$ in expectation exactly when $\beta_1 \ne 0$, which is what makes the ratio
$\mathrm{MSR}/\mathrm{MSE}$ a test for the slope.
:::

**Derivation (expected mean squares).** From @ch02-sigma we already have $E\{\mathrm{MSE}\} = \sigma^2$. A
similar expectation calculation, using $\mathrm{SSR} = b_1^2 S_{xx}$ and $E\{b_1^2\} = \operatorname{Var}\{b_1\} + \beta_1^2 = \sigma^2/S_{xx} + \beta_1^2$, gives

$$
E\{\mathrm{MSR}\} = E\{b_1^2 S_{xx}\} = \sigma^2 + \beta_1^2 S_{xx}.
$$

In words: MSE always estimates $\sigma^2$, while MSR estimates $\sigma^2$ *plus* a term that is positive
exactly when $\beta_1 \ne 0$. So if the slope is zero, MSR and MSE estimate the same thing and their ratio
should be near 1; if the slope is nonzero, MSR is inflated and the ratio is large. That ratio is the $F$ test. $\blacksquare$

The ANOVA table collects these quantities in a standard layout. This is the anchor later chapters extend to
extra sums of squares (@ch08-extra-ss).

## 3.7 The F test and its equivalence to the t test

### Intuition

The ANOVA table hands us a test almost for free. Under $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$, both MSR and MSE estimate the same
$\sigma^2$, so their ratio $F^{\ast} = \mathrm{MSR}/\mathrm{MSE}$ hovers near 1. Under $H_a: \beta_1 \ne 0$,
MSR is inflated by $\beta_1^2 S_{xx}$ and the ratio grows. A large $F^{\ast}$ is evidence against the null.

But we already tested $\beta_1 = 0$ with a $t$ test in @ch03-slope-inference and got $t^{\ast} = 10.29$. Do
the two tests agree? They do, exactly and always: in simple linear regression the $F$ statistic is the square
of the $t$ statistic. This is worth proving, because it shows the ANOVA $F$ test is not a new test, just the
$t$ test wearing different clothes.

### Formula

The **$F$ test** of $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$ uses

$$
F^{\ast} = \frac{\mathrm{MSR}}{\mathrm{MSE}} \sim F_{1,\,n-2} \text{ under } H_0,
$$

and rejects $H_0$ when $F^{\ast} > F_{1-\alpha;\,1,\,n-2}$. The claimed identity is

$$
F^{\ast} = (t^{\ast})^2,
$$

where $t^{\ast} = b_1/s\{b_1\}$ is the slope $t$ statistic. In words: the $F$ statistic and the squared $t$
statistic are the same number, and the tests give identical p-values.

### Derivation

:::{admonition} Theorem 3.11: The $F$ test and its equivalence to the $t$ test
:class: important theorem
Under the SLR model, the ANOVA $F$ statistic tests $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$ with

$$
F^{\ast} = \frac{\mathrm{MSR}}{\mathrm{MSE}} \sim F_{1,\,n-2} \text{ under } H_0,
$$

and in simple linear regression it equals the square of the slope $t$ statistic,

$$
F^{\ast} = (t^{\ast})^2, \qquad t^{\ast} = b_1/s\{b_1\},
$$

so the two tests give identical p-values, with $F_{1-\alpha;\,1,\,n-2} = (t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2})^2$.
:::

**Derivation ($F^{\ast} = (t^{\ast})^2$).** Start from the regression sum of squares. Since
$\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y} = b_1 (X_i - \bar{X})$,

$$
\mathrm{SSR} = \sum (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 = b_1^2 \sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2 = b_1^2 S_{xx}.
$$

With one regression degree of freedom, $\mathrm{MSR} = \mathrm{SSR} = b_1^2 S_{xx}$. Therefore

$$
F^{\ast} = \frac{\mathrm{MSR}}{\mathrm{MSE}} = \frac{b_1^2 S_{xx}}{\mathrm{MSE}}.
$$

Now write the $t$ statistic and square it, using $s^2\{b_1\} = \mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}$:

$$
(t^{\ast})^2 = \left(\frac{b_1}{s\{b_1\}}\right)^2 = \frac{b_1^2}{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}} = \frac{b_1^2 S_{xx}}{\mathrm{MSE}} = F^{\ast}.
$$

The two expressions are literally the same. Distributionally this matches a standard fact: the square of a
$t_{n-2}$ random variable is an $F_{1,\,n-2}$ random variable, so squaring the slope $t$ statistic lands on the
ANOVA $F$ statistic, and the critical values match too, $F_{1-\alpha;\,1,n-2} = (t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2})^2$. $\blacksquare$

:::{admonition} Key idea
:class: tip keyidea
The ANOVA table is variance bookkeeping: split the total variation into what the line explains and what
it leaves behind, then compare the two. In simple regression the resulting $F$ test is not a new test,
just the slope $t$ test squared. Its real payoff comes in Chapter 8, where the same bookkeeping tests
several slopes at once, something a single $t$ cannot do.
:::

@fig-ch03-f-tsq confirms the distributional half: square 200,000 draws from a $t_{23}$ and the histogram lands
on the $F_{1,23}$ density, with the 5% critical value $4.28$ equal to $2.069^2$.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_f_tsq.png
:name: fig-ch03-f-tsq
:alt: A histogram of squared t values with 23 degrees of freedom, piled up near zero and decaying to the right. A red F-distribution density with 1 and 23 degrees of freedom traces the histogram exactly. A dotted vertical line marks the 5% critical value at 4.28, labeled as 2.069 squared.
Squaring a t with 23 degrees of freedom produces exactly an F with 1 and 23 degrees of freedom. The 5% critical value 4.28 is the square of the t critical value 2.069, so the F test and the two-sided t test reject in identical situations.
```

:::{admonition} Example 3.5: Confirming F equals t squared for Toluca
:class: note
**Question.** Verify numerically that the Toluca $F$ statistic equals the square of the slope $t$ statistic.

**Intuition.** Compute $F^{\ast} = \mathrm{SSR}/\mathrm{MSE}$ and $t^{\ast} = b_1/s\{b_1\}$ and square the
second.

**Formula.** $F^{\ast} = b_1^2 S_{xx}/\mathrm{MSE}$ and $(t^{\ast})^2 = (b_1/s\{b_1\})^2$.

**Computation.**

```r
b1 <- coef(fit)[["lotsize"]]
```

## 3.8 R-squared and its interpretation

### Intuition

The ANOVA split invites a natural summary: what fraction of the total variation did the line explain? That
fraction is $R^2$, the **coefficient of determination** (Definition 3.12). It runs from 0 (the line
explains nothing beyond the mean) to 1 (the line passes through every point). It is the single most
reported and most misread number in regression, so we will state plainly what it does and does not mean.

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 3.12: Coefficient of determination
:class: note definition
The **coefficient of determination** $R^2$ is the proportion of the total variation in $Y$ that the
regression explains,

$$
R^2 = \frac{\mathrm{SSR}}{\mathrm{SSTO}} = 1 - \frac{\mathrm{SSE}}{\mathrm{SSTO}}.
$$

In simple linear regression it equals $r^2$, the square of the Pearson correlation between $X$ and $Y$.
:::

In words: $R^2$ is the proportion of the total variation in $Y$ that the regression on $X$ accounts for. In
simple linear regression it also equals $r^2$, the square of the Pearson correlation between $X$ and $Y$, a
link Chapter 4 develops (@ch04-r-and-slope).

@fig-ch03-r2 shows the two ingredients: the total scatter of hours about their mean (SSTO, left) shrinks to the
much smaller residual scatter about the line (SSE, right). $R^2$ is how much of the vertical spread the line
removed.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_r2.png
:name: fig-ch03-r2
:alt: Two side-by-side scatterplots of the Toluca data. Left: points with a horizontal line at the mean of Y and long vertical red segments from each point to the mean, labeled total variation SSTO = 307,203. Right: the same points with the fitted line and much shorter orange vertical segments from each point to the line, labeled leftover SSE = 54,825.
Left: the hours vary widely about their own mean (SSTO). Right: once lot size is in the model, the leftover scatter about the line (SSE) is much smaller. R-squared = 1 minus SSE/SSTO = 0.82 is the fraction of the vertical spread the line removed.
```

:::{admonition} Example 3.6: R-squared for Toluca
:class: note
**Question.** What fraction of the variation in work hours does lot size explain?

**Computation.**

```r

round(c(SSR = SSR, SSE = sum(residuals(fit)^2), SSTO = SSTO,
        R2 = SSR / SSTO, r = sqrt(SSR / SSTO)), 4)
```
```text
        SSR         SSE        SSTO          R2           r 
252377.5808  54825.4592 307203.0400      0.8215      0.9064 
```

```python

print(round(SSR, 4), round(SSTO, 4), round(SSR / SSTO, 4),

```
```text

```

**Interpretation.** $R^2 = 0.82$: lot size accounts for about 82% of the variation in work hours across these
runs. The remaining 18% is the residual scatter that other factors (crew, tooling, luck) produce. The
correlation is $r = 0.91$.
:::

:::{admonition} What R-squared does not tell you
:class: warning
Three wrong readings to avoid. First, $R^2 = 0.82$ does **not** mean the model is correct or the line is
straight; a curved relationship can still post a high $R^2$ (this is one lesson of the Anscombe quartet in
Chapter 4). Second, it does **not** mean predictions are accurate to 82%; prediction accuracy is set by $s$
and the prediction interval, not by $R^2$. Third, a high $R^2$ does **not** establish that $X$ causes $Y$.
$R^2$ measures how much of $Y$'s spread this line removed, nothing more. Always read it beside a residual plot
and the standard error $s$.
:::

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
$R^2 = 0.82$ means lot size explains 82% of the *variation* in hours, not that predictions are within 82% of
the truth. Prediction accuracy is described by $s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}} = 48.8$ hours, the typical size of a
residual, and more completely by a prediction interval, which for a 100-unit run spans about $\pm 105$ hours
(@ch03-prediction-interval). A model can have a high $R^2$ and still predict a single case loosely.
:::

## 3.9 A confidence band for the whole line

### Intuition

The confidence interval of @ch03-mean-response is honest about *one* lot size you name in advance. But often
you want to draw a band around the entire fitted line and claim, with 95% confidence, that the true line lies
inside it everywhere at once. If you simply string together the pointwise intervals, the simultaneous
confidence is less than 95%, because you are making many claims and some will fail by chance. The
**Working-Hotelling** band (Definition 3.13) fixes this by using a slightly larger multiplier, so the
whole-line claim holds at the stated level.

### Formula

:::{admonition} Definition 3.13: Working-Hotelling confidence band
:class: note definition
The **Working-Hotelling $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence band** for the regression line is, at every $X_h$,

$$
\hat{Y}_h \pm W\, s\{\hat{Y}_h\}, \qquad W^2 = 2\, F_{1-\alpha;\,2,\,n-2}.
$$

Because $W > t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}$, the band covers the entire true line simultaneously at the stated
level, at the cost of being a little wider than a pointwise interval everywhere.
:::

In words: the band has the same center and the same standard error $s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ as the pointwise interval,
but the multiplier $W$ replaces $t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-2}$. Because $W > t$, the band is a little wider everywhere,
and that extra width buys simultaneous coverage of the true line at all $X_h$ at once. The multiplier uses
$F$ with $2$ numerator degrees of freedom because the line has two parameters, $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$, that
can both vary.

```r
W <- sqrt(2 * qf(0.95, 2, n - 2))
round(c(W = W, t = qt(0.975, n - 2)), 4)
```
```text
     W      t 
2.6162 2.0687 
```

```python
W = np.sqrt(2 * stats.f.ppf(0.95, 2, n - 2))
print(round(W, 4), round(stats.t.ppf(0.975, n - 2), 4))
```
```text
2.6162 2.0687
```

For Toluca the whole-line multiplier is $W = 2.62$ against the pointwise $t = 2.07$, so the simultaneous band
is about 26% wider than a single-point interval. @fig-ch03-working-hotelling draws both.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_working_hotelling.png
:name: fig-ch03-working-hotelling
:alt: The Toluca scatterplot with the fitted line, a pair of dashed blue curves for the pointwise 95% confidence interval, and a wider shaded orange band for the Working-Hotelling simultaneous band. Both are narrowest at the mean lot size and flare toward the ends, with the orange band always outside the dashed curves.
The pointwise confidence interval (dashed) is honest about a single chosen lot size. The Working-Hotelling band (shaded) is wider by the factor W/t = 2.62/2.07 and is honest about the entire line at once. Use the wider band when you will read the fitted line at several places.
```

::::{admonition} Try it 3.7
:class: important
You plan to read the fitted Toluca line at five different lot sizes and report all five mean-response estimates
together. Should you use the pointwise $t$ multiplier or the Working-Hotelling $W$? Why?

:::{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
Use $W$. Reading the line at five places and claiming all five intervals hold is a simultaneous statement, and
stringing pointwise 95% intervals together gives less than 95% joint confidence. The Working-Hotelling band
guarantees 95% coverage of the true line at all lot sizes at once, including your five, at the cost of being a
little wider. (Bonferroni, an alternative you will meet later, is another way to control the joint error rate.)
:::

## 3.12 Frequently asked questions

**Q1. What is the one-sentence difference between a confidence interval and a prediction interval?** A
confidence interval is for the *mean* response at $X_h$, averaged over all cases at that predictor value; a
prediction interval is for a *single new* case, and is wider because it must also cover that case's own random
scatter $\sigma^2$.

**Q2. Why does the interval widen as $X_h$ moves away from $\bar{X}$?** Because the variance of the fitted mean
carries a $(X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx}$ term. Away from the center, a small change in the estimated slope pivots
the line a large vertical distance, so the fitted value there is less certain. Every fitted line passes through
$(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$, so the uncertainty is smallest there.

**Q3. Why use $t$ and not the normal distribution?** Because we estimate $\sigma$ with $s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}}$
rather than knowing it. That extra estimation makes the reference distribution $t_{n-2}$, with heavier tails
than the normal. For large $n$ the two nearly coincide, but for $n = 25$ the difference matters.

**Q4. The $F$ test and the $t$ test gave the same p-value. Do I need both?** In simple linear regression, no:
$F^{\ast} = (t^{\ast})^2$, so they are one test. Report the $t$ test for a single slope because it keeps the
direction (sign) of the effect. The $F$ test earns its keep in multiple regression, where it can test several
coefficients simultaneously.

**Q5. My $R^2$ is high. Does that mean my model is good?** Not by itself. A high $R^2$ can accompany a curved
relationship, non-constant variance, or influential outliers, all of which a residual plot would reveal. $R^2$
measures variation explained, not correctness, not prediction accuracy, and not causation. Read it alongside
$s$ and diagnostic plots (Chapter 9).

**Q6. Can I use these intervals to predict hours for a lot of 500 units?** No. That is extrapolation far beyond
the data range (20 to 120 units). The formulas will happily produce an interval, but its stated confidence
assumes the straight-line model holds out there, which the data cannot support. Trust intervals only inside, or
just barely outside, the observed range of $X$.

**Q7. What exactly does "95% confident" mean for the slope interval?** It means the *procedure* traps the true
slope in 95% of repeated samples, as @fig-ch03-ci-logic shows with 100 simulated intervals. For your one
interval $(2.85, 4.29)$, the true slope is either in it or not; the 95% describes the long-run reliability of
the method, not a probability about this particular interval.

```{figure} figures/fig_ch03_ci_logic.png

:alt: One hundred horizontal confidence-interval bars stacked vertically, one per simulated sample, each a 95% interval for the slope. A dashed vertical line marks the true slope 3.57. Almost all bars cross the line; a small number that miss it are drawn in red.
```

**EP 3.1 (interpret output in context).** A production scheduler has one specific new run of 80 units
on next week's calendar. Software fits the Toluca model and returns two intervals at a lot size of 80.

Interpret each interval in one sentence, with units and in the context of this problem. Then the
scheduler proposes to budget next week's single run at "between 327 and 369 hours, 95% sure," using the
first interval. Explain in full sentences whether that is the right interval to use, which one the
scheduler should use instead, and why the two intervals differ by so much.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The first interval, $(326.5, 369.4)$ hours, is a 95% confidence interval for the *mean* hours across all
runs of 80 units: we are 95% confident that the average time for 80-unit runs, taken over many such runs,
lies between about 327 and 369 hours. The second interval, $(244.7, 451.2)$ hours, is a 95% prediction
interval for a *single* new 80-unit run: we are 95% confident that one specific new run of that size will
take between about 245 and 451 hours. The scheduler's plan is wrong, and it will understate the risk. The
confidence interval describes where the long-run average lands, but next week's job is one run, not an
average, and a single run carries its own random scatter of about $s = 48.8$ hours on top of our
uncertainty about the line. The scheduler should budget with the prediction interval, $(245, 451)$ hours,
which is roughly $206$ hours wide against the confidence interval's $43$ hours. The two differ because the
prediction interval adds the new run's own error variance $\sigma^2$ (the leading $1$ in
$s^2\{\text{pred}\} = \mathrm{MSE}(1 + 1/n + (X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx})$), a term that never goes away no
matter how much data we collect.

*A weak answer* labels the two intervals correctly but does not explain that a single run needs the
prediction interval *because* it carries the extra $\sigma^2$ scatter, and so misses why the confidence
interval would blow the budget.

**EP 3.2 (a student claims X, evaluate).** A classmate writes: "Under the normal-error model the slope
estimate $b_1$ is exactly normal, so a 95% confidence interval for the slope should use the standard
normal multiplier $1.96$." The chapter instead uses $t_{0.975,\,23} = 2.069$. Explain in full sentences
what is right and what is wrong in the classmate's reasoning, what specific step forces the switch from
$1.96$ to $2.069$, and what happens to the gap between the two multipliers as the sample size grows.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The classmate is right that $b_1$ is exactly normal under the normal-error model: it is a linear
combination of independent normal responses, so $b_1 \sim N(\beta_1, \sigma^2/S_{xx})$. If we knew the
error standard deviation $\sigma$, then $(b_1 - \beta_1)/(\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}})$ would be exactly standard
normal and $1.96$ would be the correct multiplier. The flaw is that we do not know $\sigma$. We estimate
it by $s = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}}$ and divide by the *estimated* standard error $s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}}$
instead. Replacing the fixed constant $\sigma$ with the noisy estimate $s$ injects extra uncertainty, and
that is exactly what turns the reference distribution from a normal into a $t$ with $n - 2 = 23$ degrees
of freedom, whose heavier tails require the larger multiplier $2.069$ to still capture 95% of the
distribution. As $n$ grows, $\mathrm{MSE}$ estimates $\sigma^2$ more and more reliably, the $t$
distribution approaches the normal, and the multiplier shrinks back toward $1.96$, so the gap closes.

*A weak answer* just states "we use $t$ because $n$ is small" without naming the substitution of $s$ for
$\sigma$ as the reason, and so cannot explain why the gap vanishes as $n$ grows.
:::

First, verify from the numbers that the $F$ statistic equals the square of the TV slope's $t$ statistic,
and explain in one sentence why that identity must hold here. Then a manager reads $F = 312$ and
$R^2 = 0.61$ and concludes, "the model is so significant that it will predict any one market's sales to
within a unit or two." Explain in full sentences what the large $F$ actually establishes, what
$R^2 = 0.61$ means, and which number in the output governs how far a single new market can fall from the
line.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The slope's $t$ statistic is $17.66763$, and its square is $17.66763^2 = 312.14$, which matches the $F$
value in the ANOVA table to the digits shown. This identity must hold because in simple linear regression
$F^{\ast} = (t^{\ast})^2$: the $F$ test and the two-sided slope $t$ test are the same test, so the $F$
statistic carries no information the $t$ statistic did not. The large $F$, like the tiny slope p-value,
establishes only that the slope is far from zero relative to its standard error, that is, that TV budget
is genuinely related to sales; it says nothing about how tightly the points hug the line. The value
$R^2 = 0.61$ means that TV budget accounts for about 61% of the variation in sales across markets, leaving
39% to other factors, but a proportion of variation explained is not a promise of accurate individual
prediction. The number that governs how far one new market can land from the line is the residual standard
error $s = 3.2587$ thousand units, the typical size of a single market's scatter about the line; it feeds
the prediction interval, which the chapter found spans roughly $(7.7, 20.6)$ thousand units at a budget of
150 (Example 3.7). So the manager's "within a unit or two" is off by an order of magnitude: a significant,
high-$F$ model can still predict a single market only loosely.

*A weak answer* confirms $F = t^2$ but then treats the large $F$ or the high $R^2$ as evidence of
prediction accuracy, missing that $s$ (and the prediction interval built from it), not $F$ or $R^2$, sets
how far one case can miss.

**EP 3.4 (what would change if).** Suppose the Toluca engineers gathered far more production data at the
same spread of lot sizes, so that the sample size $n$ grows very large while $S_{xx}$ grows in proportion
and $\mathrm{MSE}$ stays near $2384$. Explain in full sentences what happens, as $n \to \infty$, to (a) the
width of the 95% confidence interval for the mean hours at $X_h = 100$, and (b) the width of the 95%
prediction interval for a single new 100-unit run. Say why the two behave differently, and what that
difference reveals about the limits of prediction.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The confidence interval for the mean uses $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \mathrm{MSE}(1/n + (X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx})$.
As $n \to \infty$ with $S_{xx}$ growing in proportion, both $1/n$ and $(X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx}$ shrink to
zero, so $s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ and the interval's width shrink to zero: with unlimited data we can pin down the
*average* hours at 100 units as precisely as we like, because the line itself becomes perfectly determined.
The prediction interval uses $s^2\{\text{pred}\} = \mathrm{MSE}(1 + 1/n + (X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx})$. The
same two terms vanish, but the leading $1$ does not, so the prediction variance falls only to
$\mathrm{MSE} \approx 2384$, and the interval's half-width settles near $t \cdot s \approx 1.96 \times 48.8
\approx 96$ hours rather than to zero. The two behave differently because the prediction interval must
cover a single new run's own random scatter $\sigma^2$, which no amount of data removes, whereas the
confidence interval only chases the position of the line. The lesson is that prediction has a hard floor:
even a perfectly estimated model cannot forecast one new run better than that run's own irreducible
variability allows.

*A weak answer* says "both intervals get narrower" without recognizing that the confidence interval goes
to zero while the prediction interval stops at the irreducible $\sigma^2$ floor.
:::

**EP 3.5 (interpret a figure, explain why).** @fig-ch03-band-width plots two estimated-standard-error
curves against lot size: the mean-response standard error $s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ (blue) dips to a minimum near
the mean lot size of $70$ and rises toward both ends, while the prediction standard error $s\{\text{pred}\}$
(yellow) stays nearly flat near $49$ hours across the whole range and sits above the blue curve everywhere.
Explain in full sentences (a) why the blue curve is U-shaped with its lowest point at $\bar{X}$, and (b)
why the yellow curve barely bends even though it lies above the blue one at every lot size.

:::{admonition} Model answer
:class: dropdown
The blue curve tracks $s\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}(1/n + (X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx})}$. The only part
that depends on where you predict is $(X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx}$, which is zero when $X_h = \bar{X} = 70$ and
grows as $X_h$ moves away in either direction. That squared distance makes the curve U-shaped, with its
minimum exactly at the mean lot size, because the fitted line is pinned down most tightly at the center of
the data and a small tilt in the slope swings it more the further out you go. The yellow curve tracks
$s\{\text{pred}\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}(1 + 1/n + (X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx})}$, which has the same
position-dependent term but adds the leading $1$. That leading term contributes $\mathrm{MSE} \approx 2384$
to the variance, far larger than the $1/n$ and $(X_h - \bar{X})^2/S_{xx}$ pieces across the observed range,
so the total is dominated by a constant and $s\{\text{pred}\}$ stays close to $\sqrt{2384} \approx 48.8$
hours everywhere. The curve still bends upward slightly at the extremes for the same reason the blue one
does, but the bend is tiny relative to the flat floor set by a single run's own scatter.

*A weak answer* says the prediction curve is "just higher because prediction is wider" without identifying
the leading $1$ (the run's own variance $\sigma^2$) as the large, position-independent term that flattens
the curve.
:::

:::{admonition} Resumen del capítulo (en español)
:class: dropdown
Este capítulo hace **inferencia (inference)** para la regresión lineal simple, continuando con los datos de la
Toluca Company del Capítulo 2 (recta ajustada $\hat{Y} = 62.37 + 3.5702\,X$, $\mathrm{MSE} = 2384$). El Capítulo
2 dio estimaciones puntuales; aquí añadimos incertidumbre en forma de intervalos y pruebas.

Bajo el modelo de errores normales, los estimadores $b_0$ y $b_1$ son exactamente normales, y al estandarizar
con el error estándar estimado $s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}/S_{xx}}$ se obtiene una distribución
**t (t distribution)** con $n-2$ grados de libertad. De ahí salen el **intervalo de confianza (confidence
interval)** para la pendiente, $b_1 \pm t\,s\{b_1\} = (2.85, 4.29)$, y la **prueba de hipótesis (hypothesis
test)** de $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$, con $t^{\ast} = 10.29$ y valor p de $4.45\times10^{-10}$: el tamaño del lote
sí afecta a las horas.

La distinción central del capítulo es entre el intervalo de confianza para la **respuesta media (mean
response)** y el **intervalo de predicción (prediction interval)** para una observación nueva. El primero
describe el promedio de muchas corridas; el segundo suma la varianza propia de la corrida nueva y por eso es
mucho más ancho. Para un lote de 100 unidades, el intervalo de la media es $(390, 449)$ y el de predicción es
$(314, 525)$. Una simulación con semilla confirma que cada uno cubre su objetivo cerca del 95%.

El enfoque de **análisis de varianza (analysis of variance)** descompone $\mathrm{SSTO} = \mathrm{SSR} + \mathrm{SSE}$
($307{,}203 = 252{,}378 + 54{,}825$) y produce la **prueba F (F test)**, con $F^{\ast} = 105.9$. Demostramos que
en regresión simple $F^{\ast} = (t^{\ast})^2$: son la misma prueba. El **coeficiente de determinación
(coefficient of determination)** $R^2 = 0.82$ es la proporción de variación explicada, no una medida de
exactitud ni de causalidad. Por último, la **banda de Working-Hotelling (Working-Hotelling band)**, con
multiplicador $W = 2.62$, da confianza simultánea para toda la recta a la vez.
